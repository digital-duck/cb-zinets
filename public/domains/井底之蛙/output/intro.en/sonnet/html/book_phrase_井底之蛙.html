🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. 🌱 丶

The stroke 丶 (pronounced zhǔ, though most learners just call it "the dot") is one of the simplest building blocks in all of Chinese. Picture an ink brush touching paper at a slight angle and lifting away — leaving behind a small teardrop shape, thicker at the top and tapering to a point. That's 丶.

Here is the simple rule: 丶 is not decorative — it carries meaning or shape in the characters it lives inside. For example, place a 丶 above 冂 and you get 冗; add two dots to 火 and you see them borrowed into characters like 灬 (the "fire feet" you find under 点 or 热). The dot changes the structure every time.

Try it yourself. Take a piece of paper and practice drawing 丶 ten times. Start your brush (or pen) at the top-left, press down gently, then sweep toward the lower-right and lift. 🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

🌱 口

Imagine you are holding a cup. Look straight down into it — you see a round opening at the top. That opening is exactly what the ancient Chinese character 口 captures.

Picture this: 口 is drawn as a simple square or rectangle with four strokes, like a little window frame. It originally depicted a human mouth — open, ready to speak or eat. Because a mouth is just a hole in your face, 口 quickly came to mean any opening or hole.

This matters for our story of the frog in the well (井底之蛙). The well is a deep shaft dug into the earth, and at the very top of that shaft sits — you guessed it — a 口: an opening, a circular frame of sky. The frog lives far below, at the bottom, and can only see the world through that one tiny 口 above. Its entire universe is whatever fits inside that little square of light.

Simple rule: Whenever you spot 口 inside another character, ask yourself, "Is there a mouth, an opening, or a hole involved here?" You will be right surprisingly often.

Try it yourself: Look around the room you are in right now. Find three things that have an opening — a door frame, a bottle top, a window. Each one is a 口 in the real world. Which one feels most like the frog's view of the sky?

Try it yourself. Trace 土 slowly: one horizontal stroke, one vertical stroke, then a wider horizontal stroke at the base. Say tǔ aloud. What three things in your room are made from or rest on earth?

🌱 之

Everyday example. In English, we use the word of to connect two ideas: "the bottom of the well," "a friend of mine," "the color of the sky." Chinese has a tiny character that does exactly the same job — 之 (pronounced zhī).

Picture this. Our idiom is 井底之蛙 — four characters, one vivid image. Break it apart:

  • 井底 (jǐng dǐ) = well-bottom, meaning the bottom of the well
  • 之 (zhī) = of
  • 蛙 (wā) = frog

Put them together: the frog of the well-bottom — a frog living at the bottom of a well, who has never seen the wide world outside.

Simple rule. 之 acts like a bridge: [Place or Thing] 之 [Person or Thing] means the second item belongs to or comes from the first. You can think of it as an arrow pointing backward: 蛙 ← 之 ← 井底. The frog belongs to that dark, narrow world.

In modern Chinese, people mostly use 的 (de) instead of 之, but 之 lives on inside proverbs, poetry, and set phrases — giving them an elegant, classical feeling.

Try it yourself. Can you spot the pattern in these phrases?

  • 天之骄子 (tiān zhī jiāo zǐ) — heaven's favored child
  • 君子之交 (jūnzǐ zhī jiāo) — a gentleman's friendship

Each time, 之 quietly says: this belongs to that.

🌸 Phrase 井底之蛙

🌸 Phrase 井底之蛙

Imagine you have only ever eaten one flavor of ice cream — say, vanilla. You might genuinely believe vanilla is the best flavor in the world, simply because you have never tasted anything else. Your certainty feels real, but it comes from a very small sample of experience.

Picture this: A frog is born inside a deep stone well. Every day it looks up and sees a small, round patch of sky — maybe the size of a dinner plate. To this frog, that patch is the entire sky. When a sea turtle visits and describes the endless ocean, the frog cannot believe it. Its world is real, but it is also tiny.

This is the story behind 井底之蛙 (jǐng dǐ zhī wā), which translates word-by-word as well–bottom–'s–frog, or simply "the frog at the bottom of the well." Chinese uses the particle 之 to show possession, so 井底之蛙 means the frog that belongs to the bottom of the well. The phrase is used to gently describe someone whose knowledge or experience is too narrow to see the bigger picture.

Simple rule: 井底之蛙 = limited perspective because of a limited environment. It is never used as personal insult — it is an invitation to climb out and look around.

Try it yourself: Think of one belief you hold that might change if you traveled somewhere new or learned a new skill. That moment of realizing "my well might be small" is exactly what this idiom celebrates.
