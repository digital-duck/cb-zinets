🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 ⺮

Have you ever seen a bundle of chopsticks? Or watched someone write with a traditional brush pen? Many everyday objects in Chinese life were once made from bamboo — one of the most useful plants in the world.

This little drawing is called the bamboo radical, and it acts like a label. Whenever you spot ⺮ perched on top of a Chinese character, it whispers a clue: this character has something to do with bamboo or bamboo-like things.

Simple rule. ⺮ is a component — a building block that combines with other pieces to form full characters. Think of it as a prefix tag:

🍃 三

Think about the number three — three wishes, three little pigs, three strikes. The number $3$ shows up everywhere in stories and everyday life because it feels complete: a beginning, a middle, and an end.

Picture this: the Chinese character 三 (pronounced sān) is the word for "three," and it shows you exactly what it means at a glance. Draw one horizontal line. Now draw a second line beneath it. Now draw a third line beneath that. You have written 三. Chinese writing began as pictures of the world, and for small numbers, the picture is the count itself. Each stroke stands for one unit, so the character literally shows $1 + 1 + 1 = 3$.

Simple rule: in Chinese, the numbers one through three are written as stacked horizontal strokes — 一 (yī, one stroke), 二 (èr, two strokes), 三 (sān, three strokes). The form and the meaning match perfectly. There is nothing to decode; the character is the quantity.

Try it yourself: hold a finger in the air and trace three horizontal lines from left to right, one below the other, and say sān aloud as you finish the third stroke. You have just written your first number in Chinese — and spotted your first pattern. Learning 三 is not memorizing an arbitrary symbol; it is reading a picture of counting itself. That moment of recognition — "I see it!" — is a small spark of the joy that the whole phrase 一见钟情 (love at first sight) is all about.

Picture this. Draw two short strokes that start close together at the top and sweep outward — one to the left, one to the right — like a tiny pair of open arms. That is 八. Ancient scribes designed it to show the idea of splitting or dividing, and over time it settled into the number eight. Chinese children learn it early because its shape is simple and its meaning is common.

Simple rule. 八 = eight. Pronounced bā in Mandarin (the first tone — flat and high, like a held musical note). You will also find it hiding inside bigger characters as a building block, often carrying a faint sense of "spreading apart." Whenever you spot two outward-leaning strokes at the base of a character, 八 may be at work.

Try it yourself. Write 八 three times, saying bā aloud each time. Then look for it in these words you may already know: 八月 (bā yuè, August — literally "eighth month") and 八方 (bā fāng, "all directions" — because the ancients counted eight compass points). 🌱 日

Everyday example: Every morning the sun rises, filling the sky with light and warmth. In English we have one word — "sun" — but we also use the sun to mark time: a day is simply one full journey of the sun across the sky. In Chinese, a single character carries both ideas at once.

Picture this: Look at the character 日 (pronounced rì). It is a neat rectangle with one horizontal line drawn straight across its middle — like a small window showing the round, glowing sun with a bright band of light cutting through its center. Ancient Chinese scribes actually drew a circle with a dot inside to represent the sun; over thousands of years that dot stretched into today's middle stroke, and the circle flattened into a tidy rectangle. Every time you write 日, picture that blazing disc hanging in the sky.

Simple rule: 日 carries two linked meanings: sun and day. These ideas are naturally the same thing — a "day" is exactly the time the sun is visible. Whenever 日 appears inside a larger character or word, it usually brings a sense of light, warmth, or passing time.

Try it yourself: Write 日 five times slowly, stroke by stroke. After drawing the middle horizontal line each time, pause and ask yourself: "What part of the sun is this?" Soon the character will feel less like a foreign symbol and more like a tiny picture of daylight.

Everyday example. Imagine a relay race. One runner finishes her lap and hands the baton to the next person waiting behind her. That next runner — the one who comes after — is exactly what 后 captures.

Picture this. 后 (hòu) means after, behind, or what comes next. Think of a timeline laid out in front of you:

$$ \underbrace{\text{past}}_{\text{前}} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{now} \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\text{future}}_{\text{后}} $$

前 (qián) sits on the left — everything that came before. 后 sits on the right — everything that comes after. The arrow between them is time moving forward.

Simple rule. Whenever something follows, succeeds, or comes downstream of something else, 后 is the right character. It shows up in everyday words like 以后 (yǐ hòu, "from now on") and 后来 (hòu lái, "later on"). In our idiom 承前启后, it names the destination: the future generation or era that is being opened up.

Try it yourself. Look at the phrase 启后 — the second half of our idiom. 启 means "to open" or "to initiate." Put them together: "open the after." Can you see how this paints a picture of unlocking a door to the future? What door might a teacher, a book, or a discovery open for the people who come after?

The character itself tells this story. 笋 is made of two parts stacked vertically:

Together, they evoke a young bamboo shoot nestled under its parent plant, neatly organized and ready to spring upward.

Simple rule: When you see ⺮ at the top of a character, you are almost always dealing with something bamboo-related — bamboo objects, bamboo plants, or things traditionally made from bamboo.

Picture this. The character 雨 is a pictograph, meaning it started as a little drawing of the thing it names. Look at it closely:

雨

The horizontal bar at the top is the sky. The box just below it is a cloud. And those four small dots inside? They are falling raindrops. Ancient scribes drew this thousands of years ago, and the modern character still keeps that same shape — sky, cloud, drops.

Simple rule. 雨 (yǔ, second tone) means rain — the weather event itself. It also acts as a radical (a building-block element) inside many other characters related to water falling from the sky: snow (雪), fog (雾), thunder (雷). Whenever you see 雨 sitting at the top of a character, there is a good chance the meaning involves weather or something coming down from the clouds.

Try it yourself. Look at the four dots in 雨 and count them — you will always find exactly four. Now trace the character with your finger: sky-bar, cloud-box, four drops. Can you see the rainstorm hiding inside those strokes? Once you do, you will never forget what 雨 means.
