🌱 丿

🌱 丿

Everyday example. Think of the way water slides off a slanted roof — it flows smoothly from upper-right down to lower-left, then simply lifts away without a hook or pause. That natural, effortless sweep is exactly what the stroke 丿 feels like when you write it.

Picture this. Imagine you are drawing a single blade of grass bending in the wind. You place your pen near the top-right, glide diagonally downward toward the lower-left, and lift your pen off the page at the end. No stopping, no curling, just a clean, confident sweep. That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. Try to keep each stroke smooth and light at the end. Once this feels natural, you have mastered one of the most common building blocks in written Chinese — a small skill with enormous reach.

🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 口

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

🌱 大

🌱 大

Have you ever spread your arms as wide as possible to show a friend just how big something was? Maybe you caught a fish and wanted to say, "It was this big!" That natural gesture is exactly what the character 大 captures.

Picture this. Imagine a person standing upright with both arms stretched out to the sides — a human figure declaring size with their whole body. That is the origin of 大 (pronounced dà). Ancient Chinese scribes drew a stick figure with outstretched arms, and over thousands of years it simplified into the three-stroke shape you see today: one horizontal stroke crossing a central vertical stroke, with a left-leaning leg completing the figure.

Simple rule. 大 means big, large, or great. It is one of the most common characters in the language because size matters everywhere — big cities (大城市), big people (大人, meaning adult), big ideas. When you see 大 inside a more complex character or word, it almost always brings a sense of magnitude or importance.

🍃 句

🍃 句

Imagine you are telling a friend, "The dog jumped over the wall." That whole statement — from "The" to "wall" — is one complete thought. In Chinese, this complete unit of meaning is called a 句 (jù).

Picture this: think of a 句 as a small box. Everything the box holds belongs together — a subject doing something, or a feeling being expressed. When the box is full and closed, you have a 句. Before the box is closed, you just have loose words floating around.

Simple rule: A 句 is the smallest chunk of language that stands on its own and makes sense by itself. In written Chinese, a 句 often ends with a period (。), a question mark (？), or an exclamation mark (！) — just like sentences in English end with punctuation. One 句 = one complete thought.

Our idiom 狗急跳墙 (gǒu jí tiào qiáng, "a desperate dog leaps over the wall") is itself a single 句. It has a subject (狗, the dog), a feeling (急, desperate), and an action (跳墙, leaping over the wall). Everything fits neatly inside one box.

Try it yourself: Look at the idiom again — 狗急跳墙. Can you point to the action word (what the dog does)? That action word is the heart of this 句. Finding it helps you understand any 句 you meet.

🍃 天

🍃 天

Have you ever stepped outside on a clear morning and looked straight up? That vast, open space above your head — in Chinese, that is called 天 (tiān).

Picture this: Imagine a person standing tall with arms stretched wide — that is the character 大, meaning "big" or "a person." Now draw a single horizontal stroke above the person's head. Where is that stroke pointing? Up — toward everything above us. That stroke is exactly what turns 大 into 天. The character is literally showing you a person looking up at what lies overhead.

Simple rule: 天 carries three closely related meanings that all grow from the same idea:

  1. Sky — the blue expanse above (今天的天很蓝, The sky today is very blue)
  2. Heaven or the divine — in older texts, 天 referred to a higher power, the way "the heavens" sounds in English
  3. Day — one sweep of the sky from sunrise to sunset counts as one 天 (三天 = three days)

So one small character holds the sky, the divine, and the passage of time — all because of that single stroke above a person's head.

Try it yourself: Hold a pencil and write 大 first. Then add one short horizontal line above it, near the top. You have just written 天. Say tiān aloud three times. Now think of one sentence: "Today is a beautiful ___." In Chinese, that blank is 天.

Imagine a coach who holds up a player's mistake in front of the whole team — not to embarrass that one player, but so everyone else learns what not to do. That act of making an example out of someone, turning one incident into a lesson for many, is exactly what 儆 captures.

The core idea is this: to warn or alert by example. 🍃 杀

Everyday example. Think about pruning a rose bush. The gardener cuts back certain branches so the whole plant grows stronger. That deliberate cutting — removing something to achieve a purpose — is exactly the idea behind 杀.

Picture this. The character 杀 (shā) means to kill or to cut down. In ancient Chinese writing, the character depicted a weapon striking downward. Today its meaning spans a wide range: slaughtering an animal for food, ending a process, or neutralizing a threat. The core image is always the same — a decisive, intentional act of ending something.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 杀, think: something is deliberately brought to an end. It is not accidental stopping; it is purposeful termination. In cooking, 杀鱼 means to gut a fish. In chess, 将杀 means to checkmate — to decisively finish the game. Even in idioms like 杀鸡儆猴 ("kill the chicken to warn the monkey"), 杀 anchors the action: one targeted act sends a message.

🍃 猴

Monkeys are everywhere in Chinese culture — playful, clever, a little mischievous. The word for monkey is 猴 (hóu), and once you know it, you'll spot it in proverbs, idioms, and even everyday compliments about someone being quick-witted.

Picture a creature that leaps from branch to branch, always watching, always scheming. That image — nimble, alert, cunning — is exactly what 猴 carries in the Chinese imagination. When someone calls you "猴一样" (monkey-like), they usually mean you're sharp and fast, not that you're being rude.

The simple rule: 猴 = monkey, and by extension, anything monkey-like in character — quick thinking, agility, or a hint of slyness. You'll find it in the idiom 杀鸡儆猴 ("kill the chicken to warn the monkey"), where the monkey stands in for any clever observer who learns from watching others' consequences. But 猴 appears in many other contexts too: 猴子 (hóu zi) is the everyday word for monkey, and 猴年 refers to the Year of the Monkey in the Chinese zodiac.

🌸 Phrase 杀鸡儆猴

🌸 Phrase 杀鸡儆猴

Have you ever watched a teacher correct one student's homework in front of the whole class — not to embarrass that student, but so every student quietly recalibrates? That moment is not really about the one notebook on the desk. It is a signal sent to the whole room.

Picture a circus trainer long ago who needs a mischievous monkey to follow commands. The monkey is clever and headstrong; scolding it directly might just start a battle of wills. So the trainer makes a deliberate point of disciplining a small, easy-to-handle animal right where the monkey can see every detail. The lesson lands without a single word aimed at the monkey. The monkey understands perfectly.

That is the heart of 杀鸡儆猴 (shā jī jǐng hóu — literally "kill the chicken to warn the monkey"). The simple rule is: act visibly on a small target so a larger, harder-to-reach audience gets the message without being confronted directly. The chicken is not the real audience; the monkey is. The public action on the lesser case does the work that a private talk with the powerful party could never accomplish.

This strategy shows up everywhere — in management, diplomacy, and storytelling — whenever a communicator needs to shift behavior through demonstration rather than demand.

Try it yourself: Think of a recent news story or film scene where one character was punished publicly. Who was the real intended audience — the person being corrected, or the crowd watching?
