Draw a vertical line for the trunk, a short horizontal stroke across the middle for the branches, and two small strokes angling downward at the base for the roots. You are looking at a tree, simplified into six strokes.

Simple rule. 木 means tree or wood — the material a tree is made of. When you see 木 inside another character, it almost always signals something made of wood or connected to trees. In our idiom 守株待兔, the character 株 (zhū) contains 木 at its left side. That tells you right away: 株 is a wooden thing — specifically, a tree stump, the stubborn leftover after a trunk is felled.

Try it yourself. Look at these two characters side by side: 木 and 株. Can you spot 木 hiding inside 株? Trace just the left-hand portion with your finger. Once you can see 木 in other characters, you will start reading Chinese like a detective — each component is a clue waiting to be found.

Training your eye to spot 丷 and 一 inside bigger characters is your first step toward reading them with confidence.

🌱 刂

Imagine a kitchen knife resting flat on a cutting board. You can see the blade and the handle clearly — it is a simple, practical tool. In Chinese writing, knives show up more often than you might expect, hidden inside everyday characters.

Picture this: The character 刀 (dāo) is the full drawing of a knife. Now imagine that knife squeezed tall and thin so it can stand neatly on the right side of another character without taking up too much room. That slimmed-down version is 刂. It is not a new idea — it is the same knife, just reshaped to fit beside a neighbor.

Simple rule: Whenever you see two vertical strokes on the right edge of a character — a short dash above and a longer stroke below — that is 刂, the knife radical. In the phrase 马到成功, look at 到 (dào, "to arrive"). 🌱 卜

Imagine you are about to make a big decision — should you travel tomorrow, or stay home? In ancient China, people faced the same uncertainty and turned to a remarkable practice for guidance.

Picture this: A wise elder heats a flat animal bone over fire until tiny cracks appear across its surface. The pattern of those cracks — which way they split, how long each line runs — was believed to reveal messages from the spirit world. The elder "read" the cracks like a map of the future.

The character 卜 is a direct snapshot of that moment. The vertical stroke ( | ) represents the main crack running down the bone. The short diagonal stroke ( ╲ ) captures a smaller branch crack shooting off to the side. Together, they draw the simplest possible crack pattern — and that picture became the word for divination.

Simple rule: 卜 (pronounced bǔ, falling-rising tone) means to divine or to foretell — to seek knowledge about what has not yet happened.

In the idiom 亡羊补牢, notice that 补 (to mend, to repair) shares the same sound bǔ as 卜. Ancient characters often bundled sound and meaning this way, so recognizing 卜 as a sound clue helps you remember whole families of related words.

Try it yourself: Write 卜 by drawing the vertical stroke first, top to bottom, then add the short diagonal branch on the right. Two strokes — simple as a crack on a bone.

🌱 月

Everyday example: Picture a clear night sky — that slim, silvery crescent hanging in the darkness. Almost every culture on Earth has its own word for it. In Chinese, that word is 月 (yuè), and it carries two meanings at once: moon and month.

Picture this: The character 月 is literally a tiny drawing of the crescent moon. Thousands of years ago, scribes sketched a curved outline with two small marks inside, suggesting the shadowed bands across the moon's face. Over centuries, brushstrokes were squared off into the compact shape you see today. Think of it as a photograph that slowly became calligraphy: 🌙 → 月.

Simple rule: 月 holds both meanings because they share one root. The moon completes roughly one full cycle every 29.5 days — and that cycle is a month. So whenever you spot 月 inside a new word, ask yourself: is this about the glowing object in the sky, or about a span of time?

Try it yourself: You already know 一 (yī) — "one." Put those two together and you get 一月 (yī yuè), meaning January, literally "month one." Now think about the phrase 一见钟情 — "love at first sight." Everyday example. Imagine a relay race: the first runner sprints as far as she can, then passes the baton to the next runner, who carries it further. No single runner completes the whole distance — each one continues from exactly where the last one stopped.

Picture this. The character 继 (jì) captures this relay idea beautifully. It is built around the radical 糸, which represents silk thread. Picture a thread that has snapped: someone picks up the two loose ends and ties them back together, letting the thread run on without interruption. That act of reconnecting and carrying forward is precisely what 继 means.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 继, think continuation — something was in motion, it was handed to a new carrier, and the motion keeps going unbroken. In the phrase 前仆后继 (qián pū hòu jì), each character adds one piece:

$$ \underbrace{前}_{\text{front/before}} \; \underbrace{仆}_{\text{to fall}} \; \underbrace{后}_{\text{behind/after}} \; \underbrace{继}_{\text{to carry on}} $$

Read together: those in front fall; those behind carry on. Each wave picks up the baton from the wave before it.

🌸 Phrase 前仆后继

🌸 Phrase 前仆后继

Everyday example. Think about a relay race where one runner stumbles and falls. Without hesitation, the next teammate leaps forward and keeps going — and the one after that, and the one after that. No one stops. No one gives up.

Picture this. The idiom 前仆后继 (qián pū hòu jì) paints exactly that scene in four characters. 前 means front or those ahead. 仆 means to fall forward — someone at the front stumbles and goes down. 后 means those behind. 继 means to continue or carry on. Put them together: the front falls, the rear carries on. Wave after wave of people press forward, undeterred by whatever stopped the person before them.

Simple rule. Use 前仆后继 to describe courageous, unstoppable effort — especially when many people contribute over time despite great difficulty. The key feeling is one falls, another rises: sacrifice does not break the momentum; it fuels it. You will often see it describing historical struggles, volunteer movements, or any group that refuses to quit even after setbacks.

消防员们前仆后继，冲入大火。 The firefighters pressed forward one after another, rushing into the blaze.
