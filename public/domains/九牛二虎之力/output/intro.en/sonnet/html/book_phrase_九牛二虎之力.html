🌱 丿

🌱 丿

Everyday example. Think of the way water slides off a slanted roof — it flows smoothly from upper-right down to lower-left, then simply lifts away without a hook or pause. That natural, effortless sweep is exactly what the stroke 丿 feels like when you write it.

Picture this. Imagine you are drawing a single blade of grass bending in the wind. You place your pen near the top-right, glide diagonally downward toward the lower-left, and lift your pen off the page at the end. No stopping, no curling, just a clean, confident sweep. That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. Try to keep each stroke smooth and light at the end. Once this feels natural, you have mastered one of the most common building blocks in written Chinese — a small skill with enormous reach.

🌱 乙

Everyday example: Imagine a vine pushing up through cracked soil, bending sideways before finding the light. That slow, curving struggle is exactly what the stroke of 乙 looks like — and exactly what it once meant.

Picture this: Draw a single curved line: start going right, hook downward, then sweep left and up. That wiggle is 乙 (yǐ). Ancient scribes used it to depict a seedling that cannot grow straight, forced to twist around an obstacle before breaking free. The image is quietly dramatic — life finding a way despite the odds.

Simple rule: In modern Chinese, 乙 is the second position in the classical counting sequence 甲、乙、丙、丁 — the equivalent of A, B, C, D. When a teacher grades papers in China, 乙 means second tier, just below the top. Beyond counting, 乙 also carries a quiet character of its own: not a bold straight line, but a resilient bend that turns, adjusts, and keeps going. That spirit links it beautifully to our phrase 九死一生, nine deaths, one life — the idea that life persists even when the path must twist.

🌱 几

Imagine a friend asks you, "How many apples do you want?" In Chinese, that question word — "how many" — is written with a single simple character: 几 (pronounced jǐ).

Picture this: 几 looks like a small stool or low table with two legs, almost like a tiny "H" missing its middle bar. In fact, that's exactly its oldest meaning — a small side table! Over time, people also began using this same shape to ask about quantity, the way we might casually point at a pile of objects and say "how many of these?" Today, 几 mostly shows up in everyday questions: 几个 (jǐ ge, "how many [things]"), 几点 (jǐ diǎn, "what time," literally "how many o'clock"), and 几岁 (jǐ suì, "how old," literally "how many years").

Simple rule: Use 几 when you expect the answer to be a small number — usually under ten. If you're guessing the count could be large, Chinese speakers switch to a different word (多少, duōshao) instead. So 几 is your go-to character for small, everyday quantities.

Try it yourself: Next time you want to ask a Chinese-speaking friend "How many books do you have?", try saying Nǐ yǒu jǐ běn shū? (你有几本书?). Notice how 几 sits right where "how many" would go in English — small, simple, and easy to spot once you know its little table-like shape.

🌱 之

🌱 之

Everyday example. In English, we use the word of to connect two ideas: "the bottom of the well," "a friend of mine," "the color of the sky." Chinese has a tiny character that does exactly the same job — 之 (pronounced zhī).

Picture this. Our idiom is 井底之蛙 — four characters, one vivid image. Break it apart:

  • 井底 (jǐng dǐ) = well-bottom, meaning the bottom of the well
  • 之 (zhī) = of
  • 蛙 (wā) = frog

Put them together: the frog of the well-bottom — a frog living at the bottom of a well, who has never seen the wide world outside.

Simple rule. 之 acts like a bridge: [Place or Thing] 之 [Person or Thing] means the second item belongs to or comes from the first. You can think of it as an arrow pointing backward: 蛙 ← 之 ← 井底. The frog belongs to that dark, narrow world.

In modern Chinese, people mostly use 的 (de) instead of 之, but 之 lives on inside proverbs, poetry, and set phrases — giving them an elegant, classical feeling.

Try it yourself. Can you spot the pattern in these phrases?

  • 天之骄子 (tiān zhī jiāo zǐ) — heaven's favored child
  • 君子之交 (jūnzǐ zhī jiāo) — a gentleman's friendship

Each time, 之 quietly says: this belongs to that.

Picture this: Hold out two fingers side by side. Now lay them flat and trace each one as a horizontal stroke. The top stroke represents the first unit; the bottom stroke represents the second. Together they form 二 — the Chinese character for two.

$$ 二 = \text{two horizontal strokes} = 2 $$

Simple rule: 二 (èr) means the number two. It is one of the most ancient and recognizable characters in Chinese because it draws what it counts. When you see 二, read it as $2$. This visual logic — letting the shape mirror the quantity — makes early numbers in Chinese unusually easy to remember. The character is honest and direct: you see exactly as many strokes as the number it names.

That same honest simplicity echoes through 一举两得 (yī jǔ liǎng dé), the phrase we are exploring. It is a story about getting two things from one effort — and 二 gives you the clearest picture of what two really means before the phrase adds any layers on top.

Simple rule: 牛 means cow, ox, or bull — any bovine animal. It is pronounced niú (second tone, like a rising question). It also carries the sense of strength and hard work. Interestingly, in modern slang 牛 means "awesome" or "impressive," because an ox is so powerful.

As a building block, 牛 appears inside other characters related to animals, agriculture, or things historically connected to cattle. Spotting it inside a complex character gives you an instant clue about its meaning.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 虎 in Chinese, think: maximum strength, danger, or boldness — not just "an animal in a zoo."

🌸 Phrase 九牛二虎之力

Everyday example: Imagine trying to push a car that has run out of gas. You lean in, plant your feet, and push with everything you have — every muscle straining. That feeling of giving 100% of your strength to a hard task is exactly what this phrase describes.

Picture this: You already know the characters 九 (nine), 牛 (ox), 二 (two), 虎 (tiger), and 力 (strength) from earlier sections. Here they combine with one new piece, 之 (zhī) — a classical Chinese particle that links two words the way "of" or "'s" does in English. So 九牛之力 reads as "the strength of nine oxen," and adding 二虎 gives the full phrase 九牛二虎之力 (jiǔ niú èr hǔ zhī lì): "the strength of nine oxen and two tigers." Picture nine huge oxen and two fierce tigers all straining together — an enormous, almost unimaginable amount of force used to describe a person giving their absolute maximum effort on something stubborn or difficult.

Simple rule: Use 九牛二虎之力 when describing an effort that pushed someone to their limits — physically or mentally. It almost always pairs with a verb like 费 (fèi, "to expend") or 用 (yòng, "to use"): 费了九牛二虎之力 means "expended the strength of nine oxen and two tigers," i.e., "tried extremely hard." It's not used for ordinary tasks — only ones that felt like a real struggle.

Try it yourself: Think of a recent moment when you struggled to finish something — moving heavy furniture, solving a tough problem, or convincing someone stubborn. Try saying: "我费了九牛二虎之力才把箱子搬上楼" ("I used the strength of nine oxen and two tigers just to carry the box upstairs"). Notice how the phrase adds vivid emphasis to ordinary effort.
