🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

Simple rule: Any time you see a brief horizontal line with a downward hook on its right side, you are looking at 乛. Writing it takes just one continuous motion: move your pen to the right, then pivot downward without lifting. You can think of it as a stroke shaped like a reversed $\Gamma$ (the Greek capital letter gamma). One stroke, one pivot — that is the whole story.

Try it yourself: Draw a horizontal dash on paper, about the width of your fingernail. Now, without lifting your pen, turn straight down for a short distance. Hold the result up and compare it to the numeral $7$ — your version should look like $7$ with the diagonal replaced by a vertical drop. Congratulations, you just wrote 乛! 🌱 水

Everyday example. Turn on a tap. Watch water flow downward, fill any container, and find its own level. Water never fights its path — it simply moves where space allows.

Picture this. Imagine a river after a long summer drought. The water level drops lower and lower until, one day, rocks that were hidden beneath the surface finally appear. You can suddenly see the riverbed clearly. This is the image captured in the Chinese idiom 水落石出 — shuǐ luò shí chū, meaning "when the water falls, the stones emerge." The hidden truth becomes visible once what was obscuring it recedes.

Simple rule. 水 (shuǐ) is the Chinese character for water. It is one of the most ancient and frequently used characters in the language. Visually, it resembles a stream with ripples spreading outward from a central current — three strokes on each side suggest flowing waves. 水 also appears as a radical (a building-block component) in hundreds of other characters related to liquids, rivers, and moisture.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 水 and trace the three-stroke pattern with your finger: a central vertical line, then two curved strokes spreading left and right. Now search for 水 hiding inside other characters — you might spot it in 海 (hǎi, sea) or 河 (hé, river). Notice how water's meaning ripples outward into every word it touches.

Think about the number three — three wishes, three little pigs, three strikes. The number $3$ shows up everywhere in stories and everyday life because it feels complete: a beginning, a middle, and an end.

Picture this: the Chinese character 三 (pronounced sān) is the word for "three," and it shows you exactly what it means at a glance. Draw one horizontal line. Now draw a second line beneath it. Now draw a third line beneath that. You have written 三. Chinese writing began as pictures of the world, and for small numbers, the picture is the count itself. Each stroke stands for one unit, so the character literally shows $1 + 1 + 1 = 3$.

Simple rule: in Chinese, the numbers one through three are written as stacked horizontal strokes — 一 (yī, one stroke), 二 (èr, two strokes), 三 (sān, three strokes). The form and the meaning match perfectly. There is nothing to decode; the character is the quantity.

Try it yourself: hold a finger in the air and trace three horizontal lines from left to right, one below the other, and say sān aloud as you finish the third stroke. You have just written your first number in Chinese — and spotted your first pattern. Learning 三 is not memorizing an arbitrary symbol; it is reading a picture of counting itself. That moment of recognition — "I see it!" — is a small spark of the joy that the whole phrase 一见钟情 (love at first sight) is all about.

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

🌱 户

Everyday example. Think about the front door of a house — just a single wooden panel on hinges, the one thing that separates the world outside from the warmth inside. In Chinese, that image gave birth to the character 户.

Picture this. 户 is a pictograph, a drawing that became a letter. Thousands of years ago, scribes sketched a door hanging from a frame: one flat top beam, two short side posts, and a single swinging panel beneath. Simplify that sketch enough times and you get 户 — half of a full gate. (A full two-paneled gate is written 門, which you will meet later. Think of 户 as one door leaf, 門 as two.)

Simple rule. Whenever you see 户 inside a more complex character, it carries the meaning of door, dwelling, or household. A household is the group of people who share that one door, so 户 also means family unit. In modern Chinese, 户口 (hùkǒu) literally "door-mouth" means household registration — the official record of who lives behind which door.

Try it yourself. Look at the stroke order: start with the top horizontal stroke, then the left vertical, then the short horizontal inside, and finally the curved hook that suggests the swinging panel. Write it three times and ask: which strokes feel like the frame, and which one feels like the door itself?

Simple rule: 月 holds both meanings because they share one root. The moon completes roughly one full cycle every 29.5 days — and that cycle is a month. So whenever you spot 月 inside a new word, ask yourself: is this about the glowing object in the sky, or about a span of time?

Try it yourself: You already know 一 (yī) — "one." Put those two together and you get 一月 (yī yuè), meaning January, literally "month one." Now think about the phrase 一见钟情 — "love at first sight." It shows up in everyday words like 以后 (yǐ hòu, "from now on") and 后来 (hòu lái, "later on"). In our idiom 承前启后, it names the destination: the future generation or era that is being opened up.

🌸 Phrase 承前启后

🌸 Phrase 承前启后

Think of a relay race. The runner in the middle leg receives the baton from the teammate behind them and then passes it forward to the next. They are the living link — honoring what came before and making what comes next possible.

That is exactly the spirit of 承前启后 (chéng qián qǐ hòu). Break it into four characters: 承 means to receive or carry on, 前 means what is in front / the past, 启 means to open or set in motion, and 后 means what comes after / the future. Put them together and you get: "receive from the past, open the way for the future."

The phrase describes any person, event, or work that serves as a bridge — it does not simply copy what came before, nor does it invent from nothing. Instead it inherits a tradition and passes it along, transformed and enriched, to the next generation.

A simple rule to remember: if something or someone connects an ending to a beginning, you can call it 承前启后.

Try it yourself. Think of a teacher who studied under a great master and went on to train many students. Would you say that teacher played a 承前启后 role? Now think of a book that summarizes decades of research and proposes new questions for future scientists. Could you use 承前启后 to describe it too? Notice how both cases share the same pattern: receive → bridge → pass forward.
