🌱 八

Imagine you crack open a walnut and watch the two halves fall apart, each leaning away from the other. That image of two things parting is exactly what the character 八 (bā) captures.

Picture this. Draw two short strokes that start close together at the top and sweep outward — one to the left, one to the right — like a tiny pair of open arms. That is 八. Ancient scribes designed it to show the idea of splitting or dividing, and over time it settled into the number eight. Chinese children learn it early because its shape is simple and its meaning is common.

Simple rule. 八 = eight. Pronounced bā in Mandarin (the first tone — flat and high, like a held musical note). You will also find it hiding inside bigger characters as a building block, often carrying a faint sense of "spreading apart." Whenever you spot two outward-leaning strokes at the base of a character, 八 may be at work.

Try it yourself. Write 八 three times, saying bā aloud each time. Then look for it in these words you may already know: 八月 (bā yuè, August — literally "eighth month") and 八方 (bā fāng, "all directions" — because the ancients counted eight compass points). Notice how the same small stroke-pair reappears, carrying its meaning into each new word like a familiar face in a crowd.

Simple rule: 亅 is not a stand-alone character you will read in a sentence. It is a radical — a building-block piece that combines with other strokes to form full characters. Think about the number three — three wishes, three little pigs, three strikes. The number $3$ shows up everywhere in stories and everyday life because it feels complete: a beginning, a middle, and an end.

Simple rule: in Chinese, the numbers one through three are written as stacked horizontal strokes — 一 (yī, one stroke), 二 (èr, two strokes), 三 (sān, three strokes). The form and the meaning match perfectly. There is nothing to decode; the character is the quantity.

Try it yourself: hold a finger in the air and trace three horizontal lines from left to right, one below the other, and say sān aloud as you finish the third stroke. You have just written your first number in Chinese — and spotted your first pattern. Learning 三 is not memorizing an arbitrary symbol; it is reading a picture of counting itself. Simple rule: 少 is the opposite of 多 (duō), which means many or much. So if you know that 多 points to abundance, remember that 少 points to shortage. You can think of it as a small scale tipping toward the lighter side:

$$ \text{少} = \text{多} - \Delta $$

where $\Delta$ represents the missing amount — whatever is lacking.

In our phrase 争分夺秒 (zhēng fēn duó miǎo, "racing against every second"), 少 lives in the background: time is scarce, seconds are few, and every one counts.
