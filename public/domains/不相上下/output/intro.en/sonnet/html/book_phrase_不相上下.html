🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 卜

🌱 卜

Imagine you are about to make a big decision — should you travel tomorrow, or stay home? In ancient China, people faced the same uncertainty and turned to a remarkable practice for guidance.

Picture this: A wise elder heats a flat animal bone over fire until tiny cracks appear across its surface. The pattern of those cracks — which way they split, how long each line runs — was believed to reveal messages from the spirit world. The elder "read" the cracks like a map of the future.

The character 卜 is a direct snapshot of that moment. The vertical stroke ( | ) represents the main crack running down the bone. The short diagonal stroke ( ╲ ) captures a smaller branch crack shooting off to the side. Together, they draw the simplest possible crack pattern — and that picture became the word for divination.

Simple rule: 卜 (pronounced bǔ, falling-rising tone) means to divine or to foretell — to seek knowledge about what has not yet happened.

In the idiom 亡羊补牢, notice that 补 (to mend, to repair) shares the same sound bǔ as 卜. Ancient characters often bundled sound and meaning this way, so recognizing 卜 as a sound clue helps you remember whole families of related words.

Try it yourself: Write 卜 by drawing the vertical stroke first, top to bottom, then add the short diagonal branch on the right. Two strokes — simple as a crack on a bone.

🌱 木

🌱 木

Everyday example. Think of a tree in your backyard — a solid trunk rising from the ground, with roots gripping the earth below and branches reaching upward. Now imagine that tree after it has been cut down, leaving only a stubby stump behind. That image is exactly where our story begins.

Picture this. The character 木 (mù) is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing. A pictograph is a symbol that looks like the thing it represents. Draw a vertical line for the trunk, a short horizontal stroke across the middle for the branches, and two small strokes angling downward at the base for the roots. You are looking at a tree, simplified into six strokes.

Simple rule. 木 means tree or wood — the material a tree is made of. When you see 木 inside another character, it almost always signals something made of wood or connected to trees. In our idiom 守株待兔, the character 株 (zhū) contains 木 at its left side. That tells you right away: 株 is a wooden thing — specifically, a tree stump, the stubborn leftover after a trunk is felled.

Try it yourself. Look at these two characters side by side: 木 and 株. Can you spot 木 hiding inside 株? Trace just the left-hand portion with your finger. Once you can see 木 in other characters, you will start reading Chinese like a detective — each component is a clue waiting to be found.

🌱 目

Have you ever squinted at something far away, trying to get a better look? That simple act — the eye working to see — is exactly what the character 目 captures.

Picture this. Draw an oval for an eye. Inside it, add two horizontal lines to represent the pupil and the eyelid folds. Now tilt the whole thing upright. That is essentially 目 (mù). It is one of the oldest Chinese characters, a pictograph — a word that means "a drawing that became a symbol." You are not memorizing an abstract squiggle; you are reading a tiny sketch of an eye.

Simple rule. 目 (mù) means eye, and by extension, to see or to look. Whenever you spot 目 hiding inside a more complex character, there is almost always something visual going on — seeing, watching, gazing, or appearance. Think of it as a built-in visual clue, like a label that whispers, "something about eyes or sight lives here."

Try it yourself. Look at the character 看 (kàn), which means to look or to watch. Notice the bottom half — that is 目 right there. The top part shows a hand shading the eyes, like someone peering into the distance. Put them together and the meaning arrives naturally: a hand over an eye, looking out. Once you see 目, you cannot unsee it.

🌱 上

🌱 上

Everyday example. Imagine placing a pot directly on top of a campfire. The pot sits on the flames — not beside them, not under them, but right on top. That simple relationship, "on top of," is exactly what 上 captures.

Picture this. Look at the character 上: a short horizontal line sitting above a longer one — like a small object resting on a table. The lower line is the surface; the upper line is whatever rests on it. The shape itself tells the story. Something is elevated, raised, perched above. Ancient writers drew this character to show a dot or mark above a baseline, pointing upward. Over centuries it settled into the clean two-line form we see today.

Simple rule. 上 (shàng) means above, on top of, or upward. It can describe a fixed position ("on the desk") or a direction of movement ("go up the stairs"). In 火上加油 — our key phrase — 上 sits between 火 (fire) and 加油 (add oil), acting as a bridge: oil is being poured on top of the fire. That one small character makes the whole image vivid and precise.

Try it yourself. Picture these two scenes:

  • A book lying on a desk → the book is 上 the desk.
  • Climbing upstairs → you are moving 上.

In both cases, 上 points to the same core idea: elevation, whether as a location or a direction. Hold that image — something rising, something resting on top — and 火上加油 will feel immediately alive.

🍃 下

🍃 下

Imagine you are standing on a hill and you drop a stone. The stone falls — it goes down. In Chinese, that single idea of "downward" or "below" is captured by one character: 下 (xià).

Picture this. Draw a flat horizontal line — that represents a surface, like the ground or a table. Now draw a short vertical stroke going beneath it, with a small foot pointing down. That is exactly how 下 is written: a line on top, and something reaching below it. The picture is the meaning.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 下, think below the line. It marks direction (down), position (underneath), or movement (going lower). You will find it paired with other characters to build bigger meanings: 下面 (xià miàn) means "the area below / underneath," and 下午 (xià wǔ) means "afternoon" — literally "the lower half of noon," the part of the day that has already passed its peak and is heading down toward evening.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 上 (shàng), which means "above" — the short stroke sits on top of the line instead of below it. Can you see how 上 and 下 are mirror images of each other, one reaching up and one reaching down? Writing them side by side — 上下 (shàng xià) — means "up and down" or "top to bottom." You have just learned a whole phrase!

🌱 不

🌱 不

Imagine a traffic light turning red. The red light means stop — do not go. In English, we add the word "not" to flip a sentence from yes to no. Chinese has a single character that does exactly the same job: 不 (bù).

Picture this. You already know 学, which means to learn. The moment you place 不 in front of it — 不学 — the meaning flips completely: not learning. It is like a tiny switch. Whatever word follows 不, that action or quality is negated, turned off, reversed.

In our saying, 不 appears twice:

$$ \text{学而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{思则罔，思而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{学则殆} $$

Each time, 不 reaches forward and negates the character right after it. The second 不 negates 学 — giving us not learning, a phrase you can already read. We will meet the other characters one by one in the sections ahead.

Simple rule. Place 不 directly before any verb or adjective to negate it:

$$ \text{不} + \text{[word]} = \text{not [word]} $$

No extra words needed — 不 does all the work alone.

Try it yourself. You know that 学 means to learn. What does 不学 mean? If you said not learning, you already understand 不. Now look back at the full saying — can you find both places where 不 appears and flips the meaning?

🍃 相

🍃 相

Have you ever stood across from a friend and mirrored each other's movements — you raise your left hand, they raise their right? That shared, back-and-forth quality is exactly what the character 相 (xiāng) captures.

Picture this: 相 is built from two smaller pictures stacked together. At the bottom is 目 (mù), meaning eye, and at the top is 木 (mù), meaning tree. Imagine an eye looking up at a tree, carefully studying every branch. The gaze goes outward — toward something else. That act of looking toward is the seed of 相's meaning.

Simple rule: When 相 appears in a word, it signals a two-way relationship — two things directed at each other, like two eyes meeting across a room. Think of it as a mathematical relation: if $A$ relates to $B$, then something about $B$ also relates back to $A$. The arrow points both ways.

You can already see this in the phrase 左思右想 itself: the mind swings left, then swings right, each pass informing the other. That mutual back-and-forth is 相 in action, even before the character appears explicitly.

Try it yourself: Find a mirror and watch your reflection move. Which hand does your reflection raise when you raise your right hand? That moment of switched symmetry — where two sides respond to each other — is a perfect everyday image for 相.

🌸 Phrase 不相上下

🌸 Phrase 不相上下

Everyday example: Imagine two friends, Mei and Jiao, both finish a math test. Mei scores 92 and Jiao scores 93. Their teacher smiles and says, "You two are neck and neck!" That feeling — when two things are so close it's almost impossible to say which one is better — is exactly what 不相上下 captures.

Picture this: Think of a balance scale. You place a red weight on the left and a blue weight on the right. The scale barely tips either way, hovering almost perfectly level. Neither side is clearly up (上) or clearly down (下). They are not (不) mutually (相) separated into "higher" and "lower." That image is the heart of this phrase.

Simple rule: 不相上下 (bù xiāng shàng xià) literally breaks down as:

  • 不 = not
  • 相 = mutually / with each other
  • 上 = up / higher
  • 下 = down / lower

Together: "not mutually above or below" — meaning two things are essentially equal or evenly matched. In mathematics, you might describe two estimates $a$ and $b$ as 不相上下 when $|a - b| \approx 0$, so small the difference is negligible.

Try it yourself: Think of two things in your life that are almost equal — maybe two favorite foods, two songs, or two players on a team. Can you describe them as 不相上下? What would have to change to make one clearly "above" the other?

Payoff 🎯

Imagine two runners crossing the finish line at exactly the same instant — a photo finish so close that no camera can separate them. Neither wins; neither loses. They are, in every meaningful sense, equal.

That image is exactly what 不相上下 captures. Built from pieces you now know — 不 (not), 相 (mutually), 上 (above), 下 (below) — the phrase says, quite literally, "not mutually above-or-below." Neither side rises; neither side falls. The comparison collapses into perfect balance.

The simple rule: whenever two things are so evenly matched that you cannot name a clear winner, 不相上下 is your phrase. Skill levels, prices, flavors, talent — any domain where ranking feels impossible fits naturally here.
