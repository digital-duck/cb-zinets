🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 又

Everyday example. You finish your homework and also help your little sister with hers. One afternoon, two good things done. In Chinese, that double-win feeling has its own word: 又 (yòu).

Simple rule. When you see 又 before a word, it signals "also" or "one more time." Use it twice in a row — 又…又… — and it becomes "both … and …" all at once. That is the quiet engine inside 一举两得: a single action that delivers both benefits, just as 又 delivers two meanings in one stroke.

🌱 乙

Everyday example: Imagine a vine pushing up through cracked soil, bending sideways before finding the light. That slow, curving struggle is exactly what the stroke of 乙 looks like — and exactly what it once meant.

Picture this: Draw a single curved line: start going right, hook downward, then sweep left and up. That wiggle is 乙 (yǐ). Ancient scribes used it to depict a seedling that cannot grow straight, forced to twist around an obstacle before breaking free. The image is quietly dramatic — life finding a way despite the odds.

Simple rule: In modern Chinese, 乙 is the second position in the classical counting sequence 甲、乙、丙、丁 — the equivalent of A, B, C, D. When a teacher grades papers in China, 乙 means second tier, just below the top. Beyond counting, 乙 also carries a quiet character of its own: not a bold straight line, but a resilient bend that turns, adjusts, and keeps going. That spirit links it beautifully to our phrase 九死一生, nine deaths, one life — the idea that life persists even when the path must twist.

Try it yourself: Write 乙 three times, feeling the curve in your hand. Then look at the character 也 (yě, "also") — can you spot 乙 hiding inside it?

🍃 寸

Everyday example. Have you ever measured something with just your thumb? Before rulers existed, people everywhere used body parts to gauge short distances. A thumb-width was close enough for everyday tasks — and that's exactly where the Chinese character 寸 (cùn) comes from.

Picture this. The character 寸 looks like a small cross with a dot: 寸. Imagine a hand with one finger pointing downward (the vertical stroke), a horizontal line across the wrist, and a tiny dot marking the pulse point just below the wrist. Ancient Chinese healers located this pulse spot to check a patient's health — and that spot sits almost exactly one cùn from the palm crease. The character literally draws the measurement on a hand.

Simple rule. 寸 is a traditional Chinese unit of length equal to roughly $3.33\ \text{cm}$, or about $\frac{1}{10}$ of a 尺 (chǐ, a "Chinese foot"). As an equation:

$$ 1\ 尺 = 10\ 寸 \approx 33.3\ \text{cm} $$

So one 寸 is a little longer than an inch. Today you will find 寸 appearing in poetic phrases and in the names of acupuncture points far more often than on a hardware store ruler.

Try it yourself. Hold up your thumb. The width across the knuckle is roughly one 寸. Now look at the character 寸 again — can you spot the wrist, the hand, and the measuring dot? Once you see it, you never forget it.

🌱 人

Everyday example: Think about the word "person." Every language has one — it's one of the first words children learn, because people are everywhere in our world.

Picture this: The Chinese character 人 (rén) is a small picture of a human being. Imagine someone walking, seen from the side: two strokes form the shape — one leaning left, one leaning right — like two legs in mid-stride. It is one of the simplest and oldest characters in the Chinese writing system, and it carries the meaning person or people.

Simple rule: 人 is a pictograph — a character that started as a drawing of the thing it represents. Over thousands of years, the original stick-figure drawing was simplified into just two brushstrokes, but the idea stayed the same: a human being, standing and walking through the world.

You will see 人 hiding inside hundreds of other characters, quietly adding the meaning "person" or "human" to the bigger idea. For example, 大 (dà, big or great) shows a person stretching their arms wide. Recognizing 人 inside other characters is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Try it yourself: Look at the character 人 and trace the two strokes in the air with your finger. Which stroke goes left? Which goes right? Now see if you can spot the shape of a walking person in it.

Picture this: 气 originally looked like rising steam or swirling air currents. Ancient writers saw something real — air moves, air fills, air gives life. Over time, the word stretched beyond physical air to mean vital energy, the inner force that keeps you animated and upright.

Simple rule: 气 sits at the heart of many Chinese words because life runs on energy. When that energy is strong, you stand tall and act boldly. When it drains away, you slump. In 垂头丧气, the character 丧 means to lose or to forfeit. So 丧气 literally means to lose your 气 — to have your inner energy stripped away. Everyday example. Imagine a runner in a race who sprints past the finish line and keeps going — she doesn't just reach the line, she surpasses it. That feeling of crossing beyond a boundary is exactly what the character 过 (guò) captures.

Picture this. Think of 过 as an arrow that starts on one side of a wall and comes out the other side. Something begins here, moves forward, and ends up past the original point. In the phrase 才气过人 (cái qì guò rén), the characters work together like this: 才气 means "talent and spirit," 人 means "people" or "others," and 过 is the bridge — it says that someone's talent goes beyond (surpasses) other people. The full phrase means: "talent that surpasses everyone else."

Simple rule. When 过 appears between a quality and a group, it almost always means to exceed or to surpass. You can think of it as the "$>$" symbol in mathematics: if talent $=$ your gift and people $=$ everyone else, then 才气过人 says $\text{your talent} > \text{everyone else's}$.

Try it yourself. Look at the phrase 过 once more and picture that runner crossing the finish line. Ask yourself: what is crossing, and what is it crossing past?
