🌱 亅

Imagine you are fishing. You drop a line straight down into the water, and at the very tip hangs a small hook that curves back up to catch a fish. That single image — a straight line with a tiny upward curl at the bottom — is exactly what the stroke 亅 looks like.

Picture this: draw a vertical line downward, as if you are writing the number 1. Just before you lift your pen, flick the tip gently to the left. That little flick is the hook. The whole mark is called jué (亅), and it is one of the most fundamental strokes in written Chinese.

Simple rule: 亅 is not a stand-alone character you will read in a sentence. It is a radical — a building-block piece that combines with other strokes to form full characters. Think of it like the letter "l" in English: on its own it means little, but inside words like "play" or "left" it does important work. You will find 亅 hiding inside characters such as 了 (le, completion) and 事 (shì, matter/affair).

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and practice the stroke three times: down, then hook left at the bottom. Keep the hook small — about one-fifth the length of the vertical line. Notice how a tiny curve completely changes the feel of the mark. That sensitivity to small details is at the heart of learning to read and write Chinese.

🌱 八

Imagine you crack open a walnut and watch the two halves fall apart, each leaning away from the other. That image of two things parting is exactly what the character 八 (bā) captures.

Picture this. Draw two short strokes that start close together at the top and sweep outward — one to the left, one to the right — like a tiny pair of open arms. That is 八. Ancient scribes designed it to show the idea of splitting or dividing, and over time it settled into the number eight. Chinese children learn it early because its shape is simple and its meaning is common.

Simple rule. 八 = eight. Pronounced bā in Mandarin (the first tone — flat and high, like a held musical note). You will also find it hiding inside bigger characters as a building block, often carrying a faint sense of "spreading apart." Whenever you spot two outward-leaning strokes at the base of a character, 八 may be at work.

Try it yourself. Write 八 three times, saying bā aloud each time. Then look for it in these words you may already know: 八月 (bā yuè, August — literally "eighth month") and 八方 (bā fāng, "all directions" — because the ancients counted eight compass points). Notice how the same small stroke-pair reappears, carrying its meaning into each new word like a familiar face in a crowd.

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

Simple rule. 小 means small or little. It appears in everyday words: 小心 (xiǎo xīn, "be careful" — literally "small heart," meaning pay close attention to tiny details) and 小学 (xiǎo xué, "elementary school" — the small steps of learning).

Notice: 学 (to learn) contains the idea of building up from small pieces, just as 小学 names the place where that building begins.

Try it yourself. Look around the room. Find something 小 — a button, a coin, a single character on this page. Say aloud: zhè ge hěn 小 ("This one is very small"). You just used your first adjective in Chinese. That is not a small achievement.

🌱 以

Everyday example. Imagine you are baking cookies and the recipe says "mix with a spoon." That tiny word with tells you how the action happens — it names the tool. In classical Chinese, the character 以 (pronounced yǐ) plays exactly that role: it links a method or instrument to the action that follows.

Picture this. Think of 以 as a bridge connecting a resource on the left to a purpose on the right:

$$ \underbrace{\text{tool / resource}}_{\text{left of 以}} \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{以}\;}\; \underbrace{\text{action / goal}}_{\text{right of 以}} $$

The arrow shows that something is used to accomplish something else. 以 is the connector that makes that relationship visible.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 以, ask: What is being used, and for what purpose? In the phrase 以一当十 (yǐ yī dāng shí — "use one to stand for ten"), 以 introduces 一 (one — the resource), and the rest of the phrase tells you what that one does: it stands in for ten. The pattern is:

$$ 以 + \text{resource} + \text{what you do with it} $$

Try it yourself. Read this: 以笔写字. The character 以 signals that something is being used. 笔 (bǐ) means brush or pen, and 写字 (xiě zì) means write characters. Put it together: "write characters with a brush." Did you feel how 以 points directly from the tool to the task? That bridge is its whole job.

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

That idea is exactly what 当 (dāng) captures here.

Picture this: 当 means "to be equal to," "to serve as," or "to match." In the phrase 以一当十, it acts as a tiny mathematical equals sign between the one and the ten. Think of a scale where one side holds a single heavy gold bar, perfectly balancing ten ordinary stones on the other side. The gold bar equals ten stones in weight.

Simple rule: Whenever you see 当 sandwiched between two quantities, it signals equivalence or substitution:

$$ \text{one} \xrightarrow{\text{当}} \text{acts as / equals} \xrightarrow{} \text{ten} $$

So 当十 means "counts as ten" — not approximately, but fully and powerfully. The character 当 carries the sense of standing in for something, the way an understudy stands in for the lead actor and performs just as well.

Picture this. In ancient China, a single fearless soldier could hold a narrow mountain pass against a much larger army. Because of his skill and courage, that one defender was as effective as an entire squad. The image stuck: one person standing in for ten.

Simple rule. The idiom 以一当十 (yǐ yī dāng shí) is built from four characters:

Character Sound Meaning
以 yǐ using / with
一 yī one
当 dāng to equal / to stand for
十 shí ten

Put together, the phrase says: using one to equal ten. It praises someone whose effort, skill, or courage makes them worth far more than just one person. In math-like shorthand: $1 \times \text{effort} = 10 \times \text{result}$.

Try it yourself. Think of someone in your life — a teacher, a friend, a family member — who always seems to accomplish twice (or ten times!) as much as anyone else. Can you write one sentence in Chinese or English describing them using the spirit of 以一当十?
