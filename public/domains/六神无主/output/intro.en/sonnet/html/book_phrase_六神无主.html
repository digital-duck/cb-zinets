🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Think of it as drawing a $1$ on a piece of paper, but without the little foot or hat — just the bare vertical line itself. Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. 🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

Think of a fence around a backyard, or the walls of a room keeping everything safely inside. That idea — a boundary that holds something in — is exactly what the Chinese character 囗 captures.

Simple rule. 囗 (pronounced roughly wéi, rhyming with "way") functions as a radical — a building block that appears inside larger characters to hint at their meaning. Whenever you see a character shaped like something trapped inside a box, there is a good chance 囗 is at work. Characters such as 囚 (prisoner — a person 人 inside an enclosure), 国 (country — jade 玉 inside a border), and 园 (garden — a winding path inside walls) all grow from this one root idea: a space with a clear edge.

Try it yourself. Write 囗 slowly: four strokes, no gaps, corners crisp. Now look at the characters 因, 囿, and 围. Can you spot the 囗 hiding inside each one? Everyday example: Imagine filling out a form where you have to "state your reason" for something — in Chinese, that action uses the character 申 (shēn), as in 申请 (shēn qǐng), "to apply" or formally request something.

Picture this: The character 申 actually began as a picture of a bolt of lightning, stretching and extending across the sky. Because lightning reaches out in a long, branching line, 申 came to carry the idea of "extending" or "stretching forth" — and from there, the idea of "stating" something clearly, the way lightning stretches clearly across the dark sky. 申 is also one of the twelve Earthly Branches (地支, dì zhī), an old Chinese system originally used to track time, where it represents the Monkey.

Simple rule: Why does this matter for 六神无主 ("six spirits with no master," meaning utterly flustered)? Because 申 is hiding inside the character 神 (shén, "spirit" or "god") — the very word at the heart of this idiom! 神 is built from 示 (a sign of spiritual or sacred things) plus 申, which here lends its sound (shēn → shén) to the whole character.

Try it yourself: Next time you see 神, mentally split it into 示 + 申. Recognizing 申 will help you recall the pronunciation "shén" — and remember that without your 神 (spirits) in order, you'd feel just as scattered as a bolt of lightning with no fixed direction.

Everyday example. Imagine you're learning to count in Chinese, and you've already learned 一 (one) through 五 (five). The next number, six, is written as 六 and pronounced liù. You'll see it everywhere: on clocks, in phone numbers, in addresses, and on calendars.

Picture this. The character 六 looks like a small roof or tent shape sitting above two legs spreading outward — picture a little hut with an open doorway underneath. While the character doesn't draw a literal picture of "six" the way a tally mark might, this roof-and-legs shape is one of the easiest six-stroke characters to recognize once you've seen it a few times. It's compact, symmetrical, and visually distinct from its neighbors 五 (five) and 七 (seven).

Simple rule. Whenever you need to express the quantity 6 in Chinese — whether counting objects, telling time ("6 o'clock" is 六点, liù diǎn), or stating a date — you use 六. In mathematical notation, we can simply write:

$$ 六 = 6 $$

Just like in English, this character can combine with others, such as 十六 (sixteen, $10 + 6$) or 六十 (sixty, $6 \times 10$).

Try it yourself. Practice writing 六 by tracing the roof shape first, then the two legs underneath. Then try saying aloud the numbers one through six in order: 一, 二, 三, 四, 五, 六. Simple rule. Think of 无 as the word "without" or "none." It signals the non-existence of something. 🌸 Phrase 六神无主

🌸 Phrase 六神无主

Everyday example. Imagine you walk into your final exam and discover you studied the wrong chapter. Your hands go cold, your thoughts scatter, and for a moment you can't even remember your own name. That feeling — total mental blankness from shock or fear — is exactly what the Chinese phrase 六神无主 (liù shén wú zhǔ) describes.

Picture this. The phrase is built from four characters: 六 (six), 神 (spirits/gods), 无 (without), 主 (master/ruler). In ancient Chinese belief, six guardian spirits were thought to live inside a person's body, each one in charge of a different organ, quietly keeping the person calm and steady. "六神无主" literally means "the six spirits have no ruler" — as if those inner guardians suddenly abandoned their posts, leaving the person's mind in chaos with no one steering the ship.

Simple rule. Use 六神无主 to describe someone who is so panicked, shocked, or overwhelmed that they cannot think clearly or decide what to do next. It's stronger than ordinary nervousness — it paints a picture of total inner confusion, like a ship with no captain.

Try it yourself. Think of a moment when bad news left you frozen and unable to react — losing your phone with no backup, or hearing an emergency announcement. Try forming a sentence: "听到这个消息，他六神无主" ("Hearing this news, he was utterly at a loss"). Notice how the phrase captures not just fear, but the complete absence of inner direction.
