🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 丨

Everyday example. Think about a tall, straight fence post planted firmly in the ground — it stands upright, confident, and unmoving. That single vertical line is exactly what you are looking at in the stroke 丨.

Picture this. In Chinese writing, every character is built from a small set of basic brushstrokes, much like how every house is built from a handful of shapes — walls, roofs, windows. The stroke 丨 (pronounced shù, meaning "vertical") is one of the most fundamental of all. It is a single, clean downward line, drawn from top to bottom in one smooth motion.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Think of it as drawing a $1$ on a piece of paper, but without the little foot or hat — just the bare vertical line itself. Formally, you can think of it as a line segment along the $y$-axis: moving in the direction $(0, -1)$ from start to end.

Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. Notice how the simple act of drawing that single straight line already feels deliberate and intentional — that sense of purpose is exactly the spirit of 丨, the backbone hidden inside countless Chinese characters you will soon recognize.

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

🌱 口

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. That little square is the character 口 (kǒu). It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing: a picture that became a letter.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

🌱 丿一

Everyday example: Think about how you learned to write the letter "T" as a child — first a straight line across, then a straight line down. Chinese characters are built the same way, from a small set of basic strokes. Two of the very first strokes every learner meets are 一 (yī), a flat horizontal line, and 丿 (piě), a line that sweeps down and to the left, like a gentle ski slope.

Picture this: Imagine drawing a single chopstick lying flat on a table — that's 一. Now imagine picking up a pencil and dragging it from the upper right down to the lower left in one smooth, thinning stroke — that's 丿. Together, these two simple shapes are like LEGO bricks: by themselves they're plain, but stacked and combined with other strokes, they become the building blocks of thousands of characters, including 人 (rén, "person"), which uses a falling stroke like 丿 paired with another stroke leaning the opposite way.

Simple rule: 一 is written left to right in one flat motion. 丿 is written top to bottom, curving slightly left, getting thinner as it goes.

Try it yourself: On paper, draw one flat horizontal line, then below it draw one downward-left sweeping line. Notice how different they feel under your hand — flat and steady versus diagonal and tapering. You've just written your first two Chinese strokes.

Everyday example. Imagine your grandmother shows you a photograph so old and faded that the people in it are wearing clothes nobody wears anymore. You say, "Wow, that's old." In Chinese, the character for that feeling — something from a long, long time ago — is 古 (gǔ).

Picture this. The character 古 is built from two simple parts stacked vertically: 十 (shí, meaning "ten") on top, and 口 (kǒu, meaning "mouth") on the bottom. One traditional way to picture it: "ten mouths" passing a story down, generation after generation, the way old tales survive only by being spoken again and again by many people over many years. Whether or not that's the literal origin, it gives you a sturdy mental hook — many mouths, many years, one old story.

Simple rule. 古 means ancient, old, or from long ago. You'll see it in words like 古代 (gǔdài, "ancient times") and 古老 (gǔlǎo, "ancient, age-old"). Think of it as the opposite of 今 (jīn, "now, present").

Try it yourself. Look at the shape 古 — ten (十) over mouth (口). Picture ten generations of mouths retelling the same story. Now try writing it once, saying "gǔ" out loud, and naming one "old" thing you know — an old book, an old building, an old song. That's 古.

Thousands of years ago, scribes sketched a curved outline with two small marks inside, suggesting the shadowed bands across the moon's face. Over centuries, brushstrokes were squared off into the compact shape you see today. Think of it as a photograph that slowly became calligraphy: 🌙 → 月.

Simple rule: 月 holds both meanings because they share one root. The moon completes roughly one full cycle every 29.5 days — and that cycle is a month. So whenever you spot 月 inside a new word, ask yourself: is this about the glowing object in the sky, or about a span of time?

Try it yourself: You already know 一 (yī) — "one." Put those two together and you get 一月 (yī yuè), meaning January, literally "month one." Now think about the phrase 一见钟情 — "love at first sight." Picture this. Imagine three small droplets of water falling in a column along the left edge of a character — that is exactly what 氵 looks like. It is called the water radical (in Chinese: 三点水, literally "three-dot water"). It is a miniature, squeezed version of the standalone character 水 (shuǐ), which means water. When 水 becomes a building block inside another character, it shrinks into those three little strokes and moves to the left side to make room for the rest.

Simple rule. Whenever you spot 氵 on the left side of a Chinese character, that character almost always has something to do with water or liquid. It acts like a category label stamped directly into the shape of the word:

That's the visual root of 儿: it began as a picture of a child or young person, viewed from the side. Simple rule: 儿 by itself means "son" or "child" (as in 儿子, ér zi, meaning "son"). This is called the érhuà (儿化) sound change, common in Beijing-style Mandarin.

Try it yourself: Say 花 (huā, "flower") and then 花儿 (huār, "little flower"). Notice how 儿 doesn't add a new word — it adds warmth and lightness to a word you already know.

Everyday example

Think about brushing your teeth. You don't do it just once — you do it every day, morning and night. In Chinese, the character 每 (měi) captures exactly this idea: "each one" or "every single one," with nothing left out.

Picture this

Imagine a row of identical lockers in a school hallway. If your teacher says "every locker must be checked," she means all of them, one by one, without skipping any. That's the feeling behind 每. It's not vague like "some" or "most" — it points to each individual item in a group, treated one at a time but covering the whole set.

You'll often see 每 paired with a measure word or noun right after it, like 每天 (měi tiān, "every day") or 每个人 (měi gè rén, "every person"). The character almost always introduces a phrase, setting up the idea of "for each X" before the rest of the sentence tells you what happens.

Simple rule

$$ 每 + (\text{measure word/noun}) = \text{"every"} + (\text{noun}) $$

So 每天 = "every day," 每次 = "every time," 每年 = "every year." The pattern stays consistent — just attach 每 to whatever unit you're counting.

Everyday example. Hold up one hand and spread your fingers wide. Count them: one, two, three, four, five. That last number — five — is one of the first counting words every child learns, in any language. In Chinese, this number has its own character: 五 (wǔ).

Picture this. The character 五 is built from simple strokes that form a kind of grid or crossing pattern, almost like two lines crossing inside a frame. You can think of it as an ancient way of marking a tally — a quick symbol invented long before Arabic numerals 1, 2, 3 existed, when people still needed a clear, memorable shape for "five of something." Just as we might write $5$ today, ancient writers settled on 五 as the agreed symbol for this exact quantity.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 五, it means the number five — no more, no less. Mathematically, $五 = 5$. It behaves exactly like the number 5 does in arithmetic: $五 + 五 = 十$ (five plus five equals ten), and $五 \times 二 = 十$ (five times two equals ten).

Try it yourself. Look around the room and find five matching objects — five fingers, five coins, five books. Say "五" aloud as you count them. Then try writing the character a few times, noticing how its crossing strokes echo the act of tallying. This small character is your gateway into reading numbers in Chinese.

Imagine you're on a family trip, and you arrive at a huge body of calm, fresh water surrounded by land — maybe you go boating, fishing, or just sit and watch the sunset over the water. In Chinese, this place has a name: 湖 (hú), meaning "lake."

Whenever you see 氵 on the left side of a character, you can guess it's related to water in some way — rivers, lakes, oceans, even sweat or tears! This small shortcut helps you recognize dozens of water-related words once you learn just one radical.

🌸 Phrase 五湖四海

Imagine you're at a big family reunion, and your grandmother says, "Our relatives have scattered to every corner of the world — some in Beijing, some in New York, some in Sydney." She doesn't actually mean every single corner, but she means everywhere, far and wide. In Chinese, there's a beautiful four-character phrase for exactly this idea: 五湖四海 (wǔ hú sì hǎi), which literally means "five lakes, four seas."

Picture this: Long ago, "five lakes" and "four seas" referred to all the major bodies of water known across the land — together they stood for the entire known world. So when someone says people come from 五湖四海, they're painting a picture of folks arriving from every direction imaginable, like rivers flowing in from all corners to meet at one place.

Simple rule: Use 五湖四海 whenever you want to describe people, things, or ideas coming from all over — not from one specific place, but from everywhere broadly. It's similar to saying "from all corners of the earth" or "from far and wide" in English.

Try it yourself: Picture a busy train station. You could say: "这里的人来自五湖四海" (zhèlǐ de rén láizì wǔhú-sìhǎi) — "The people here come from all over." Try using the phrase to describe your own classroom, your city, or even a potluck dinner where every dish comes from a different place!

Everyday example. Picture a class reunion where former classmates arrive from every direction — one flew in from Beijing, another from a small fishing village, another from across the ocean. That's why this phrase is the natural finish line — it's the moment scattered pieces become one usable tool.

Simple rule. A phrase like 五湖四海 is "payoff" because you can now use it immediately: in conversation, in writing, in understanding native speech, anywhere people gather from far and wide.
