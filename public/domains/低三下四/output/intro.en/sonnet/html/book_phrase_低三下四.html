🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 氐

Everyday example. Imagine someone so eager to please a boss that they practically bow to the floor — head down, shoulders hunched, making themselves as small as possible. That image of deliberate lowness is exactly the feeling packed into the component 氐 (pronounced dǐ).

Picture this. 氐 is an ancient picture of a figure leaning forward and bending down toward the ground. Think of an arrow or a plant stalk that droops below a horizontal line — reaching its lowest point. That drooping gesture gave the character its core meaning: to be at the base, at the bottom, pressed low.

Simple rule. Whenever you spot 氐 hiding inside another character, it signals "low" or "at the ground level." The character 低 (dī, "low in height") is built from the person radical 亻 plus 氐 — literally, a person who is low. The character 底 (dǐ, "bottom, base") adds a roof over 氐 — the lowest point inside a space. So 氐 works like a quiet anchor, pulling meaning downward.

$$ \text{氐} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{"pressed low, at the base"} $$

Try it yourself. Look at the idiom we are exploring: 低三下四 means to lower oneself beneath everyone. Can you now see how 低 carries 氐 inside it, whispering "low"? Every time you meet 氐 in a new character, ask: what here is close to the ground? You will be surprised how often the answer fits perfectly.

🌱 亻

Imagine watching someone walk. From the side, their body tilts slightly forward — one leg planted, one leg stepping ahead. That silhouette is exactly what the ancient Chinese scribes captured when they drew the character 人 (rén), meaning person.

Picture this: when 人 needs to stand beside another part of a character — sharing space on the left side — it gets squeezed into a narrower shape. The two strokes become a short diagonal leaning against a vertical line: 亻. Think of it as 人 pressed against a wall to make room for a neighbor.

Simple rule: 亻is called the person radical (人字旁, rén zì páng). Whenever you spot 亻on the left side of a character, it signals that the character's meaning involves people — human actions, human qualities, or human relationships. It is not a standalone character; it only lives as a component inside other characters.

In 前仆后继, the very first character, 前 (qián, front/before), has nothing to do with people — but many characters around it do. Learning 亻now gives you a reliable clue: as you meet new characters, if you see that little leaning figure on the left, ask yourself, "Does this relate to a person?" You will be right far more often than not.

Try it yourself: Look at 他 (tā, he/him) and 你 (nǐ, you). Can you spot 亻in each one?

🌱 儿

Everyday example: Think about the word "child" or "son" in English. It's a small word, but it carries a big role in a family — it shows the next generation, someone younger, someone still growing.

Picture this: In Chinese, the character 儿 (pronounced ér) plays a very similar role. Picture a simple drawing of a person, but with legs that look light and a little bent — almost like a small figure walking or crouching. That's the visual root of 儿: it began as a picture of a child or young person, viewed from the side. Over time, the strokes simplified into just two: a short line on top and a curving "leg" stroke underneath, written as 儿.

Simple rule: 儿 by itself means "son" or "child" (as in 儿子, ér zi, meaning "son"). But you'll see it most often attached to the end of other words, where it softens the sound and adds a sense of smallness or familiarity — like adding "-y" or "-let" in English (think "doggy" instead of "dog"). This is called the érhuà (儿化) sound change, common in Beijing-style Mandarin.

Try it yourself: Say 花 (huā, "flower") and then 花儿 (huār, "little flower"). Notice how 儿 doesn't add a new word — it adds warmth and lightness to a word you already know.

🌱 卜

Imagine you are about to make a big decision — should you travel tomorrow, or stay home? In ancient China, people faced the same uncertainty and turned to a remarkable practice for guidance.

Picture this: A wise elder heats a flat animal bone over fire until tiny cracks appear across its surface. The pattern of those cracks — which way they split, how long each line runs — was believed to reveal messages from the spirit world. The elder "read" the cracks like a map of the future.

The character 卜 is a direct snapshot of that moment. The vertical stroke ( | ) represents the main crack running down the bone. The short diagonal stroke ( ╲ ) captures a smaller branch crack shooting off to the side. Together, they draw the simplest possible crack pattern — and that picture became the word for divination.

Simple rule: 卜 (pronounced bǔ, falling-rising tone) means to divine or to foretell — to seek knowledge about what has not yet happened.

In the idiom 亡羊补牢, notice that 补 (to mend, to repair) shares the same sound bǔ as 卜. Ancient characters often bundled sound and meaning this way, so recognizing 卜 as a sound clue helps you remember whole families of related words.

Try it yourself: Write 卜 by drawing the vertical stroke first, top to bottom, then add the short diagonal branch on the right. Two strokes — simple as a crack on a bone.

🌱 囗

Think of a fence around a backyard, or the walls of a room keeping everything safely inside. That idea — a boundary that holds something in — is exactly what the Chinese character 囗 captures.

Picture this. Imagine drawing a simple square with your finger in the air: top line, right side, bottom line, left side — a closed loop. That sketch is 囗. It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing, representing an enclosure, a border, or a contained space.

Simple rule. 囗 (pronounced roughly wéi, rhyming with "way") functions as a radical — a building block that appears inside larger characters to hint at their meaning. Whenever you see a character shaped like something trapped inside a box, there is a good chance 囗 is at work. Characters such as 囚 (prisoner — a person 人 inside an enclosure), 国 (country — jade 玉 inside a border), and 园 (garden — a winding path inside walls) all grow from this one root idea: a space with a clear edge.

Try it yourself. Write 囗 slowly: four strokes, no gaps, corners crisp. Now look at the characters 因, 囿, and 围. Can you spot the 囗 hiding inside each one? As you find it, ask yourself: what is being enclosed here, and why might that matter to the word's meaning? That small act of noticing is exactly the kind of thinking while learning Confucius had in mind.

🍃 三

Think about the number three — three wishes, three little pigs, three strikes. The number $3$ shows up everywhere in stories and everyday life because it feels complete: a beginning, a middle, and an end.

Picture this: the Chinese character 三 (pronounced sān) is the word for "three," and it shows you exactly what it means at a glance. Draw one horizontal line. Now draw a second line beneath it. Now draw a third line beneath that. You have written 三. Chinese writing began as pictures of the world, and for small numbers, the picture is the count itself. Each stroke stands for one unit, so the character literally shows $1 + 1 + 1 = 3$.

Simple rule: in Chinese, the numbers one through three are written as stacked horizontal strokes — 一 (yī, one stroke), 二 (èr, two strokes), 三 (sān, three strokes). The form and the meaning match perfectly. There is nothing to decode; the character is the quantity.

Try it yourself: hold a finger in the air and trace three horizontal lines from left to right, one below the other, and say sān aloud as you finish the third stroke. You have just written your first number in Chinese — and spotted your first pattern. Learning 三 is not memorizing an arbitrary symbol; it is reading a picture of counting itself. That moment of recognition — "I see it!" — is a small spark of the joy that the whole phrase 一见钟情 (love at first sight) is all about.

🍃 下

Imagine you are standing on a hill and you drop a stone. The stone falls — it goes down. In Chinese, that single idea of "downward" or "below" is captured by one character: 下 (xià).

Picture this. Draw a flat horizontal line — that represents a surface, like the ground or a table. Now draw a short vertical stroke going beneath it, with a small foot pointing down. That is exactly how 下 is written: a line on top, and something reaching below it. The picture is the meaning.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 下, think below the line. It marks direction (down), position (underneath), or movement (going lower). You will find it paired with other characters to build bigger meanings: 下面 (xià miàn) means "the area below / underneath," and 下午 (xià wǔ) means "afternoon" — literally "the lower half of noon," the part of the day that has already passed its peak and is heading down toward evening.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 上 (shàng), which means "above" — the short stroke sits on top of the line instead of below it. Can you see how 上 and 下 are mirror images of each other, one reaching up and one reaching down? Writing them side by side — 上下 (shàng xià) — means "up and down" or "top to bottom." You have just learned a whole phrase!

Imagine you are whispering in a library. Your voice drops — it becomes low, quiet, close to the floor. That feeling of "downward, beneath, reduced" is exactly what the Chinese character 低 (dī) carries.

Picture this: think of a person bowing their head so low that their chin nearly touches their chest. The character 低 is built from the person radical 亻(a figure standing upright) on the left, paired with a phonetic component on the right that hints at the sound. Together they paint a picture of someone lowering themselves.

Simple rule: 低 means low — in height, in rank, in volume, or in status. It is the opposite of 高 (gāo), which means high. Whenever something is beneath the usual level — a soft voice ($V_{\text{low}}$), a dipped chin, a humble posture — 低 is the word at work. In the four-character phrase 低三下四, 低 ("low") pairs with 下 ("below") to intensify the sense of someone making themselves very small, almost groveling.

Try it yourself: Can you find two things around you right now — one that is 高 (high) and one that is 低 (low)? Perhaps a ceiling is 高, and the floor is 低. Notice how your eyes move up for one and down for the other. That physical direction is exactly what these characters remember for you.

Everyday example. Imagine you're setting the table for dinner and your mom says, "We need four bowls today — Grandma is visiting!" You count out $1, 2, 3, 4$ bowls. That last number, 4, is written in Chinese as 四 (sì).

Picture this. The character 四 looks like a box with two strokes inside, almost like a window with curtains. A simple way to remember it: picture a square table with four legs, one in each corner — four sides, four corners, the number four.

Simple rule. 四 (sì) means the number $4$. In Chinese, just like in English, we use it to count things: 四个苹果 (sì gè píngguǒ) means "four apples," and 四只猫 (sì zhī māo) means "four cats." The character itself never changes shape no matter what you're counting — unlike English, you don't add an "-s" or change the word at all.

You'll also meet 四 in the phrase 五湖四海 (wǔ hú sì hǎi), which literally means "five lakes, four seas." People use this expression to describe people or things coming "from all over" — from every corner of the country or the world, just like four seas touching every shore.

Try it yourself. Look around your room right now. Can you find four objects that are the same — four chairs, four books, four pencils? Practice saying "四个 [object]" out loud. Then try writing 四 three times, picturing that four-legged table each time.

Imagine a staircase descending underground: you are not just one step below everyone else, you are many steps below, crouching and scraping.

Simple rule. Use 低三下四 to describe behavior that is excessively humble to the point of self-degradation — groveling, flattering, or demeaning oneself in order to please someone more powerful. It carries a negative feeling: this kind of excessive submission is seen as undignified.

他不想低三下四地去求人。 He did not want to demean himself by begging others.

Try it yourself. Think of a situation where someone acts overly submissive — perhaps to a boss, a celebrity, or a powerful relative. Can you describe their behavior using 低三下四? Notice how the phrase captures both the direction (downward) and the degree (very far down). That layered image is exactly what makes it memorable.
