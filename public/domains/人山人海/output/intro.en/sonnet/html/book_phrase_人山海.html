🌱 丿一

🌱 丿一

Everyday example: Think about how you learned to write the letter "T" as a child — first a straight line across, then a straight line down. Chinese characters are built the same way, from a small set of basic strokes. Two of the very first strokes every learner meets are 一 (yī), a flat horizontal line, and 丿 (piě), a line that sweeps down and to the left, like a gentle ski slope.

Picture this: Imagine drawing a single chopstick lying flat on a table — that's 一. Now imagine picking up a pencil and dragging it from the upper right down to the lower left in one smooth, thinning stroke — that's 丿. Together, these two simple shapes are like LEGO bricks: by themselves they're plain, but stacked and combined with other strokes, they become the building blocks of thousands of characters, including 人 (rén, "person"), which uses a falling stroke like 丿 paired with another stroke leaning the opposite way.

Simple rule: 一 is written left to right in one flat motion. 丿 is written top to bottom, curving slightly left, getting thinner as it goes.

Try it yourself: On paper, draw one flat horizontal line, then below it draw one downward-left sweeping line. Notice how different they feel under your hand — flat and steady versus diagonal and tapering. You've just written your first two Chinese strokes.

🌱 母

Everyday example

Think about the word mother in English. Now look at the Chinese character 母 ($m\check{u}$), which also means "mother." Just like English has the word "mom," Chinese has 母 as a building block that appears inside many other characters and words related to family, care, and origin.

Picture this

Imagine the character 母 as a simplified picture of a mother — some scholars say the two dots inside represent a mother nursing a child, a symbol of nurturing and giving life. This idea of "the source that nourishes" is why 母 shows up in words like 母语 ($m\check{u}y\check{u}$, "mother tongue," your native language) and 母校 ($m\check{u}xi\grave{a}o$, "alma mater," your home school).

Simple rule

Whenever you see 母 in a word, think "origin" or "nurturer." It signals something foundational — like a mother is foundational to a family. This single character, $\text{母}$, carries that root meaning wherever it travels into new combinations.

Try it yourself

🍃 每

Everyday example

Think about brushing your teeth. You don't do it just once — you do it every day, morning and night. In Chinese, the character 每 (měi) captures exactly this idea: "each one" or "every single one," with nothing left out.

Picture this

Imagine a row of identical lockers in a school hallway. If your teacher says "every locker must be checked," she means all of them, one by one, without skipping any. That's the feeling behind 每. It's not vague like "some" or "most" — it points to each individual item in a group, treated one at a time but covering the whole set.

You'll often see 每 paired with a measure word or noun right after it, like 每天 (měi tiān, "every day") or 每个人 (měi gè rén, "every person"). The character almost always introduces a phrase, setting up the idea of "for each X" before the rest of the sentence tells you what happens.

Simple rule

$$ 每 + (\text{measure word/noun}) = \text{"every"} + (\text{noun}) $$

So 每天 = "every day," 每次 = "every time," 每年 = "every year." The pattern stays consistent — just attach 每 to whatever unit you're counting.

Try it yourself

Pick a daily habit of yours — eating breakfast, checking your phone, saying hello to someone. Now say it in your head as "每天我..." ("Every day I..."). Notice how 每 instantly signals "this happens for all days, no exceptions."

🌱 氵

🌱 氵

Think about all the words in English that relate to water: river, rain, swim, wash. In Chinese, many of those ideas share a tiny visual clue hidden inside the character.

Picture this. Imagine three small droplets of water falling in a column along the left edge of a character — that is exactly what 氵 looks like. It is called the water radical (in Chinese: 三点水, literally "three-dot water"). It is a miniature, squeezed version of the standalone character 水 (shuǐ), which means water. When 水 becomes a building block inside another character, it shrinks into those three little strokes and moves to the left side to make room for the rest.

Simple rule. Whenever you spot 氵 on the left side of a Chinese character, that character almost always has something to do with water or liquid. It acts like a category label stamped directly into the shape of the word:

  • 河 (hé) — river
  • 海 (hǎi) — sea
  • 洗 (xǐ) — to wash
  • 游 (yóu) — to swim
  • 泡 (pào) — bubble

You do not need to recognize the whole character to make a smart guess. See 氵 → think water is involved.

Try it yourself. Find the character 湖 (hú), which means lake. Can you spot 氵 on its left side? Cover the right half and just look at those three dots. A lake is definitely water — the radical told you so before you learned a single stroke of the rest!

🌱 人

Everyday example: Think about the word "person." Every language has one — it's one of the first words children learn, because people are everywhere in our world.

Picture this: The Chinese character 人 (rén) is a small picture of a human being. Imagine someone walking, seen from the side: two strokes form the shape — one leaning left, one leaning right — like two legs in mid-stride. It is one of the simplest and oldest characters in the Chinese writing system, and it carries the meaning person or people.

Simple rule: 人 is a pictograph — a character that started as a drawing of the thing it represents. Over thousands of years, the original stick-figure drawing was simplified into just two brushstrokes, but the idea stayed the same: a human being, standing and walking through the world.

You will see 人 hiding inside hundreds of other characters, quietly adding the meaning "person" or "human" to the bigger idea. For example, 大 (dà, big or great) shows a person stretching their arms wide. Recognizing 人 inside other characters is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Try it yourself: Look at the character 人 and trace the two strokes in the air with your finger. Which stroke goes left? Which goes right? Now see if you can spot the shape of a walking person in it.

🌱 山

Everyday example. Think about the last time you saw a mountain range on the horizon — three peaks rising side by side, the tallest one standing proudly in the middle. That simple silhouette is exactly what the Chinese character 山 (shān) looks like.

Picture this. Draw three vertical lines. Now connect them at the bottom with a single horizontal stroke. The middle line stretches a little taller than the two beside it. You have just drawn 山. Ancient Chinese writers looked at real mountains and traced their outline directly onto oracle bones thousands of years ago. The character has barely changed since. What you write today is a direct echo of those earliest strokes.

Simple rule. 山 means mountain. It is also a radical — think of a radical as a building-block that appears inside many other characters and hints at their meaning. Whenever you spot 山 hiding inside a larger character, there is a good chance that character has something to do with mountains, rocks, or high places. For example, 岩 (yán, cliff or rock) contains 山 at the top.

Try it yourself. Trace 山 three times, saying shān softly as you write. Then look at the word 火山 (huǒshān, volcano — literally "fire mountain"). Can you find 山 inside it? Notice how the meaning of the whole word comes partly from the building-block you just learned.

🍃 海

Everyday example. Imagine standing on a beach. In front of you, water stretches so far that the line where it meets the sky — the horizon — is the only edge you can see. That huge body of salty water is called 海 (hǎi), which means "sea" or "ocean."

Picture this. Now picture a map of the world. Lakes are small blue patches, rivers are thin blue lines, but 海 is the vast blue that fills entire regions, connecting to other seas and eventually to the open ocean. In the phrase 五湖四海 (wǔ hú sì hǎi), 海 represents the "four seas" — the great waters believed to surround and bound the inhabited world in classical Chinese geography. Paired with 湖 (lake), the phrase moves from smaller, contained waters to the largest, most boundless ones, building a sense of "everywhere" — from every lake to every sea.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 海, think: big, open, connecting water — something larger than a lake, often touching distant lands. In idioms, 海 frequently stands for vastness, openness, or the whole world, not just literal water.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 海. Notice it contains 氵 (the "water" radical) on the left — a visual hint pointing to anything related to water. Can you think of another word using 氵 that also relates to liquid or water? Jot one down before moving to the next section.

🌸 Phrase 人山海

Have you ever arrived at a street festival or a big sports event and thought, "Wow, there are people everywhere I look"? That feeling — walls of people, people spilling into every corner — is exactly what the Chinese phrase 人山人海 (rén shān rén hǎi) captures.

Picture this: you are standing at the entrance to a crowded night market. Look left — a mountain of people. Look right — an ocean of people. The crowd feels as vast and unstoppable as both. That double image is baked right into the phrase. 人 (rén) means person or people, 山 (shān) means mountain, and 海 (hǎi) means sea or ocean.

The simple rule is this: when Chinese pairs a massive land feature with a massive water feature and wraps both in people, it is painting a picture of an enormous crowd. The structure 人 + [land] + 人 + [water] says: "People as far as the eye can see, in every direction." It is a vivid comparison — a metaphor — not a literal description.

Try it yourself: Think of the busiest place you have ever visited. Close your eyes and picture that crowd as both a mountain and a sea. Now you understand exactly what a speaker means the moment they say 人山人海. Next time you see a packed subway platform or a sold-out concert, you have the perfect phrase ready to use!
