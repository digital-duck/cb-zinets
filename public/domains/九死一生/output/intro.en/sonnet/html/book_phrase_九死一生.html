🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Everyday example: Imagine a vine pushing up through cracked soil, bending sideways before finding the light. That slow, curving struggle is exactly what the stroke of 乙 looks like — and exactly what it once meant.

Ancient scribes used it to depict a seedling that cannot grow straight, forced to twist around an obstacle before breaking free. The image is quietly dramatic — life finding a way despite the odds.

Simple rule: In modern Chinese, 乙 is the second position in the classical counting sequence 甲、乙、丙、丁 — the equivalent of A, B, C, D. When a teacher grades papers in China, 乙 means second tier, just below the top. Beyond counting, 乙 also carries a quiet character of its own: not a bold straight line, but a resilient bend that turns, adjusts, and keeps going. That spirit links it beautifully to our phrase 九死一生, nine deaths, one life — the idea that life persists even when the path must twist.

Compare it to 月 (the full moon): 月 has two horizontal strokes inside it, while 夕 has only one. That missing stroke is the storytelling — the moon hasn't fully risen yet. Simple rule. Whenever you see 夕 in a Chinese word, think evening or dusk. It is the marker of day's end. For example, 夕阳 (xī yáng) means "the setting sun" — literally "evening sun." And 除夕 (chú xī), the name for Chinese New Year's Eve, uses 夕 to mark that final, threshold evening before the new year begins.

You can also think of it mathematically: if 月 represents a complete cycle — the full moon $M$ — then 夕 is like $M - 1$ stroke, an incomplete moon, a moon still becoming.

In 九死一生, it is not saying someone died nine times (impossible!). Instead, it uses repetition and exaggeration: "facing death nine times over" expresses just how close to death a person came before barely surviving.

Picture this. The character 生 actually began as a simple drawing: a small plant sprouting up out of the ground, with a line underneath representing the earth and a stroke above representing the new growth breaking through. So whenever you see 生, you can imagine that tiny shoot pushing upward — something starting to live, grow, or exist where before there was nothing.

Simple rule. Whenever a word in Chinese involves birth, life, growth, or freshness, look for 生. For example, 学生 (xuéshēng) means "student" — literally someone who is "growing" through learning. 生日 (shēngrì) means "birthday" — the day life began. Even 生病 (shēngbìng), "to get sick," uses 生 because illness is something that arises or comes into being in the body.

🌸 Phrase 九死一生

Imagine a sailor who survives a terrible storm at sea, one that sinks every other ship nearby. When she finally reaches shore, her friends say, "You lived through that? You're so lucky to be alive!" That feeling — of barely escaping death — is exactly what the Chinese phrase 九死一生 (jiǔ sǐ yī shēng) describes.

Picture this. The phrase is built from four characters: 九 (jiǔ, "nine"), 死 (sǐ, "die" or "death"), 一 (yī, "one"), and 生 (shēng, "live" or "life"). Put together, it literally means "nine deaths, one life." Picture someone facing nine chances to die and only one chance to survive — yet they make it through. The phrase paints a vivid mental image: the odds were almost entirely against living, but life won out anyway.

Simple rule. Use 九死一生 to describe surviving an extremely dangerous situation — a near-fatal accident, a brush with disaster, or an escape so narrow it feels miraculous. It is not about literal math; "nine" and "one" simply mean "almost certain death" versus "the rare chance of survival." Think of it as the Chinese equivalent of saying someone survived "by the skin of their teeth," but with much greater intensity.

Try it yourself. Picture a hiker lost in a blizzard for three days who is finally rescued. You could say: 他经历了九死一生 ("He went through a nine-deaths-one-life experience"). Try thinking of your own "barely survived" story and imagine describing it with this phrase.
