Picture this: Imagine building with LEGO bricks. Each brick rests on the one below it. You can't just start adding bricks; each layer needs to fit securely. Building on a shaky base will make your creation unstable!

Simple rule: 承前启后 means “carry forward, open up behind.” It’s like saying: "Understand what you already know, then use that knowledge to learn something new." Don't try to learn everything from scratch at once – it can be confusing.

Try it yourself: Let’s say we are learning about colors. First, you might learn what red is: a color that often reminds us of apples or fire. Let’s talk about “前” (qián), which is a really important character in Chinese writing. It's one of the most frequently used characters and understanding it will unlock a lot of how sentences are built.

Everyday Example: Think about your morning routine. You "起床" (qǐchuáng) – you get up from bed. “起床” uses “前” as part of its meaning. It means “before” or “ahead of.”

Picture This: Imagine you're planning a trip. You want to go somewhere before the summer holidays start. That "before" is represented by “前”.

Simple Rule: The character “前” generally means ‘front’, ‘ahead’, or ‘before’ in time or position. It indicates something that comes before something else. It's like saying, “prior to,” but much more concise!

Try it yourself: Let’s look at a simple sentence: “前面有棵树。” (qiánmiàn yǒu kē shù) – "There is a tree in front." Notice how “前” tells us the tree is ahead of you. You can even draw a line and mark a point ‘front’.

“前” is an incredibly versatile character, and we’ll be building on this understanding as we explore more Chinese characters. It's a solid foundation for reading and writing!

Simple Rule: “无” (wú) doesn’t just mean “nothing.” It means the absence of something. It describes a state of emptiness, void, or lack. It's like saying "zero" in math – it represents the complete absence of quantity. We write it as 0, which literally means “no” or “none”.

Try it yourself: Close your eyes. Think about a room you’re in. Now, try to identify everything that is not there. The dust motes dancing in the sunbeams? The shadows cast by furniture?
