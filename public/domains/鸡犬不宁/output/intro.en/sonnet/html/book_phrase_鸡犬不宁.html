🌱 丁

🌱 丁

Imagine you are hammering a nail into a wooden board. You hold the nail upright, give it a tap, and it stands firm — a small, sturdy shape against the wood. That everyday image is exactly what the character 丁 (dīng) was born from.

Picture this: draw a short horizontal line across the top, then drop a vertical line straight down from its center. You have just drawn 丁. The horizontal stroke is the flat head of the nail; the vertical stroke is the shank disappearing into the surface. Two strokes, one unforgettable picture.

Here is the simple rule: 丁 carries three related meanings that all flow from that nail idea. First, it literally means nail (as in a metal fastener). Second, because nails are counted one by one, 丁 became the fourth position in China's traditional ten-step counting sequence — think of it as the letter "D" in an ancient alphabet used for ordering things. Third, because a nail is small but essential, 丁 also came to mean a person — specifically a young, able-bodied individual, the kind of person a household "counted on."

So one tiny, two-stroke character quietly holds the ideas of nail, fourth, and person all at once — which is already a little 不可思议 (incredible)!

Try it yourself: write 丁 three times, saying dīng aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like a nail seen from the side. Can you spot the nail head?

🌱 不

Imagine a traffic light turning red. The red light means stop — do not go. In English, we add the word "not" to flip a sentence from yes to no. Chinese has a single character that does exactly the same job: 不 (bù).

Picture this. You already know 学, which means to learn. The moment you place 不 in front of it — 不学 — the meaning flips completely: not learning. It is like a tiny switch. Whatever word follows 不, that action or quality is negated, turned off, reversed.

In our saying, 不 appears twice:

$$ \text{学而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{思则罔，思而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{学则殆} $$

Each time, 不 reaches forward and negates the character right after it. The second 不 negates 学 — giving us not learning, a phrase you can already read. We will meet the other characters one by one in the sections ahead.

Simple rule. Place 不 directly before any verb or adjective to negate it:

$$ \text{不} + \text{[word]} = \text{not [word]} $$

No extra words needed — 不 does all the work alone.

Imagine coming home after a long day to find your house perfectly quiet — the dog is napping, there is no noise, no rushing around. That calm, settled feeling has a name in Chinese: 宁 (níng).

Picture this: 宁 is like a roof (the top stroke) sheltering the things inside a home, keeping them safe and undisturbed. When everything under that roof is still and protected, there is 宁 — peace.

On its own, 宁 means peaceful, calm, at rest. It describes a state where nothing is stirred up or disturbed. You will find it in everyday words like 安宁 (ān níng), meaning tranquility, where 安 (safe) and 宁 (peaceful) team up to paint a picture of complete stillness.

Simple rule: Think of 宁 as a green light for calm. When 宁 is present, things are settled. When something blocks it — like the word 不 (bù), which means not — calm disappears entirely. That is exactly what happens in 鸡犬不宁: 不宁 means not peaceful, so even the chickens and dogs cannot rest!
