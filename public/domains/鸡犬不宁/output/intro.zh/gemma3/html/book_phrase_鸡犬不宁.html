Let's talk about something really fundamental in Chinese: “不” (bù). It’s one of the most common characters you’ll see, and it carries a surprisingly powerful meaning.

Everyday Example: Imagine you’re asking your friend if they want to eat dumplings. You might say “你吃饺子吗？” (Nǐ chī jiǎozi ma?) – "Do you eat dumplings?" But if they say no, you'd respond with “不，我不吃” (Bù, wǒ bù chī) – "No, I don’t eat." See how “不” is used to express negation? It means “no,” “not,” or “don’t.”

Picture this: Think of a stop sign. The red circle with the slash through it tells you not to go forward. “不” works in a similar way, signaling an absence or a refusal.

Simple Rule: "不" is your word for saying something isn't true, isn’t happening, or isn’t allowed. It’s a core element of many Chinese phrases and sentences. It always appears with a tone change – a falling tone (falling-pitch) in Mandarin – to indicate this negation.

Try it yourself: Let's look at another example: “不要” (bù yào) – "Don't want." Notice how “不” is combined with “要” (yào, meaning “want”). Say it out loud: "Bù yào!" You’re telling someone not to do something. This single character, "不," is a building block for understanding so much of Chinese language and thought.
