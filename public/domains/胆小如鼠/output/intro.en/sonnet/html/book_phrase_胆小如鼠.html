Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Every time you write 日, picture that blazing disc hanging in the sky.

Simple rule: 日 carries two linked meanings: sun and day. These ideas are naturally the same thing — a "day" is exactly the time the sun is visible. Whenever 日 appears inside a larger character or word, it usually brings a sense of light, warmth, or passing time.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Simple rule: 月 holds both meanings because they share one root. The moon completes roughly one full cycle every 29.5 days — and that cycle is a month. So whenever you spot 月 inside a new word, ask yourself: is this about the glowing object in the sky, or about a span of time?

🍃 如

Have you ever said "it feels just like home" or "she runs as fast as the wind"? In English, we use the word like or as to draw a comparison — to say that one thing resembles another. Chinese has a dedicated character for exactly this job: 如 (rú).

Picture a measuring stick held up next to an object. You are not saying they are identical; you are saying one matches up to the other in some way. That is the spirit of 如 — it invites you to place two things side by side and notice the similarity.

The simple rule: 如 means "like," "as," or "as if." It slots between two items to signal a comparison. Wherever you see 如, ask yourself: What is being compared to what?

In the idiom 胆小如鼠 ("timid as a mouse"), 如 is doing exactly this work — it connects 胆小 (timid) with 鼠 (mouse) to say the courage is mouse-sized. But 如 is not limited to that phrase. You will meet it in everyday speech: 如愿 means "as wished" (things go as you hoped); 如何 means "how" (literally "like what?").

Try it yourself: Think of something you would compare to the sun — bright, warm, powerful. In your mind, put 如 between your subject and 太阳 (the sun). You have just built a Chinese simile!

🌸 Phrase 胆小如鼠

Think of someone who refuses to speak up in class, jumps at every small noise, and hides whenever there is the slightest trouble. In English you might say that person is "a real scaredy-cat." Chinese has a vivid four-character phrase for the same idea.

The key word here is 如, a little hinge that snaps two things together into a comparison. Whenever you see 如 sitting between a quality and an object, read it as "as … as." This single character does the work that English spreads across phrases like "as brave as a lion" or "as quiet as a mouse."
