🌱 丶

The stroke 丶 (pronounced zhǔ, though most learners just call it "the dot") is one of the simplest building blocks in all of Chinese. Picture an ink brush touching paper at a slight angle and lifting away — leaving behind a small teardrop shape, thicker at the top and tapering to a point. That's 丶.

Here is the simple rule: 丶 is not decorative — it carries meaning or shape in the characters it lives inside. For example, place a 丶 above 冂 and you get 冗; add two dots to 火 and you see them borrowed into characters like 灬 (the "fire feet" you find under 点 or 热). The dot changes the structure every time.

Try it yourself. Take a piece of paper and practice drawing 丶 ten times. Start your brush (or pen) at the top-left, press down gently, then sweep toward the lower-right and lift. Once this motion feels natural in your hand, you will start spotting 丶 hiding inside dozens of characters you already know — and that recognition is the first small step toward reading Chinese with confidence.

Draw three vertical strokes side by side: the two outer strokes represent the banks, and the middle stroke represents the water flowing between them. Ancient scribes literally drew what they saw. Simple rule. 川 (chuān) means river or stream. Whenever you see 川 inside a larger character, it is almost always signaling something to do with flowing water or a waterway.

It looks like a tiny hat: a dot perched above a short horizontal brim. 口 means mouth, and it looks like one too — a small square, open and waiting. Now stack the lid directly on top of the mouth. That stacked pair, 亠口, is a compact building block that hides inside larger characters.

Simple rule: In Chinese characters, 亠 acts as a cap that sits above whatever component comes below it. When it crowns 口 (mouth), the combined unit suggests something enclosed, sealed from the top. You can picture the relationship like this:

$$ \text{亠} \;+\; \text{口} \;=\; \text{cover} + \text{mouth} \;=\; \text{sealed opening} $$

This exact pair forms the upper core of the character 高 (tall, high) — the central character in our idiom 登高望远.

Try it yourself: Write the character 高 slowly. At the very top, trace 亠 — the dot and the brim. Then find 口 sitting just beneath it. You have uncovered the 亠口 building block living inside high. Recognizing these inner bricks is how characters stop looking like puzzles and start feeling like old friends.
