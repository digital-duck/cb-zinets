🌱 八

Imagine you crack open a walnut and watch the two halves fall apart, each leaning away from the other. That image of two things parting is exactly what the character 八 (bā) captures.

Picture this. Draw two short strokes that start close together at the top and sweep outward — one to the left, one to the right — like a tiny pair of open arms. That is 八. Ancient scribes designed it to show the idea of splitting or dividing, and over time it settled into the number eight. Chinese children learn it early because its shape is simple and its meaning is common.

Simple rule. 八 = eight. Pronounced bā in Mandarin (the first tone — flat and high, like a held musical note). You will also find it hiding inside bigger characters as a building block, often carrying a faint sense of "spreading apart." Whenever you spot two outward-leaning strokes at the base of a character, 八 may be at work.

Try it yourself. Write 八 three times, saying bā aloud each time. Then look for it in these words you may already know: 八月 (bā yuè, August — literally "eighth month") and 八方 (bā fāng, "all directions" — because the ancients counted eight compass points). Notice how the same small stroke-pair reappears, carrying its meaning into each new word like a familiar face in a crowd.

That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. Picture this: draw a vertical line downward, as if you are writing the number 1. Just before you lift your pen, flick the tip gently to the left. That little flick is the hook. The whole mark is called jué (亅), and it is one of the most fundamental strokes in written Chinese.

Simple rule: 亅 is not a stand-alone character you will read in a sentence. It is a radical — a building-block piece that combines with other strokes to form full characters. Picture this. The character 寸 looks like a small cross with a dot: 寸. Imagine a hand with one finger pointing downward (the vertical stroke), a horizontal line across the wrist, and a tiny dot marking the pulse point just below the wrist. Ancient Chinese healers located this pulse spot to check a patient's health — and that spot sits almost exactly one cùn from the palm crease. The character literally draws the measurement on a hand.

Simple rule. 寸 is a traditional Chinese unit of length equal to roughly $3.33\ \text{cm}$, or about $\frac{1}{10}$ of a 尺 (chǐ, a "Chinese foot"). As an equation:

$$ 1\ 尺 = 10\ 寸 \approx 33.3\ \text{cm} $$

So one 寸 is a little longer than an inch. Today you will find 寸 appearing in poetic phrases and in the names of acupuncture points far more often than on a hardware store ruler.

🍃 少

Think about the last time you were almost late. Maybe you had only a handful of minutes before the bus left. That feeling of "not enough" — of having fewer things than you need — is exactly what the character 少 captures.

Picture this: Imagine a small pile of coins on a table. Not many — just a few. That scarcity, that smallness in quantity, is the heart of 少. When pronounced shǎo, it means few or little. When pronounced shào, it shifts to mean young — think of a young person as someone with fewer years behind them.

Simple rule: 少 is the opposite of 多 (duō), which means many or much. So if you know that 多 points to abundance, remember that 少 points to shortage. You can think of it as a small scale tipping toward the lighter side:

$$ \text{少} = \text{多} - \Delta $$

where $\Delta$ represents the missing amount — whatever is lacking.

In our phrase 争分夺秒 (zhēng fēn duó miǎo, "racing against every second"), 少 lives in the background: time is scarce, seconds are few, and every one counts.

Try it yourself: Look at two groups of objects nearby — perhaps books on a shelf or chairs in a room. Which group has fewer? Point to it and say shǎo (少). You just used the character in its most natural home: describing what is not quite enough.

Everyday example: Think about slicing an orange in half. Before the cut you have one whole fruit. After the cut you have two pieces. That single act of cutting — of separating one thing into parts — is exactly what the character 分 (fēn) expresses. It means to divide or to split.

Picture this: Look at the character 分 itself. At the top you see two strokes leaning away from each other, already pulling apart. Beneath them sits the shape of a knife (刀). A knife pressing down until the two halves separate — the character is a tiny picture of that moment frozen in ink.

Simple rule: Whenever you divide a whole into equal pieces, 分 is at work. In mathematics, if you start with one whole thing $W$ and split it into $n$ equal parts, each part has the value

$$ \frac{W}{n}. $$

When $n = 2$, you get $\frac{W}{2}$ — exactly half. This fraction notation is a direct echo of what 分 means: one amount divided by another.

Try it yourself: Take a strip of paper and fold it once — you now have $\frac{1}{2}$. Fold it again — you have $\frac{1}{4}$. Each fold is a 分. 🍃 秒

Imagine you are watching a sprinter at the finish line. The difference between gold and silver is not hours or even minutes — it is a tiny sliver of time so small you can almost miss it in a blink. That sliver is called a second, and in Chinese the word for it is 秒 (miǎo).

Picture a stopwatch clicking in your hand. One full sweep of the second hand takes sixty of those clicks. Each click is one 秒. Sixty 秒 make one 分 (fēn, minute). So the relationship is simple:

$$ 60 \text{ 秒} = 1 \text{ 分} $$

Here is the simple rule: 秒 is the smallest common unit of time in everyday speech. When something is urgent — when every heartbeat counts — Chinese speakers reach for 秒 to show just how tight the margin is. In the idiom 争分夺秒 (zhēng fēn duó miǎo), the word literally appears: 分 (minutes) and 秒 (seconds) are both being fought for, because not a single one can be wasted.
