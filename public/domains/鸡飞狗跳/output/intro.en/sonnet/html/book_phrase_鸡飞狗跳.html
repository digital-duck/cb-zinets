🌱 勹

Imagine wrapping a gift — you fold the paper snugly around the box, tucking the edges in so nothing falls out. That simple gesture of enclosing something is exactly what the character component 勹 captures.

Picture this: 勹 looks like a person bent forward with one arm sweeping outward, as if they are crouching down to scoop something up and hold it close. The stroke curves around, leaving just a small opening — like cupped hands about to catch water.

Simple rule: 勹 is a wrapping radical. Whenever you see it in a character, it signals the idea of something being curved, enclosed, or gathered together. It does not stand alone as a word; instead, it acts like a frame that wraps around other strokes inside it.

You already know one famous example from our domain's idiom: 包 (bāo, "to wrap" or "a package") — notice how 勹 forms the outer shell, curling protectively around the inner component 巳. The whole character visually shows wrapping.

Try it yourself: Look at the strokes of 勹 slowly — one sweeping curve down and to the right, then a short horizontal stroke closing the bottom. 🌱 口

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. That little square is the character 口 (kǒu). It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing: a picture that became a letter.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

🍃 句

🍃 句

Imagine you are telling a friend, "The dog jumped over the wall." That whole statement — from "The" to "wall" — is one complete thought. In Chinese, this complete unit of meaning is called a 句 (jù).

Picture this: think of a 句 as a small box. Everything the box holds belongs together — a subject doing something, or a feeling being expressed. When the box is full and closed, you have a 句. Before the box is closed, you just have loose words floating around.

Simple rule: A 句 is the smallest chunk of language that stands on its own and makes sense by itself. In written Chinese, a 句 often ends with a period (。), a question mark (？), or an exclamation mark (！) — just like sentences in English end with punctuation. One 句 = one complete thought.

Our idiom 狗急跳墙 (gǒu jí tiào qiáng, "a desperate dog leaps over the wall") is itself a single 句. It has a subject (狗, the dog), a feeling (急, desperate), and an action (跳墙, leaping over the wall). Everything fits neatly inside one box.

Try it yourself: Look at the idiom again — 狗急跳墙. Can you point to the action word (what the dog does)? That action word is the heart of this 句. Finding it helps you understand any 句 you meet.

Simple rule: Whenever 犭 appears on the left of a Chinese character, the whole character almost always connects to an animal, especially a mammal. It acts like a category label, silently announcing: "an animal lives inside this word." Even 独 (dú), the first character in 独一无二, meaning "one-of-a-kind," carries 犭 on its left side.

Try it yourself: Look closely at 独. Can you spot the 犭 on the left? Trace it with your finger — the three small strokes leaning left, like a dog tucking in its tail.
