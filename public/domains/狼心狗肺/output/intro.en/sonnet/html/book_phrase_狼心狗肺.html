🌱 丶

Everyday example. Think about dotting an i in English. That single tiny mark transforms a bare stick into a complete letter — leave it out and something feels off. Chinese writing has its own version of this small-but-essential mark: a stroke called 丶 (pronounced diǎn, which simply means "dot").

Picture this. Imagine a raindrop caught mid-fall — wide at the top, tapering to a fine point at the bottom. That shape is 丶. In traditional brush calligraphy, a writer presses down with the tip of the brush and then lifts away in one smooth motion, leaving behind this tiny teardrop mark. It takes only a moment to make, yet it appears inside hundreds of everyday Chinese characters, quietly holding them together.

Simple rule. 丶 is a basic stroke — the smallest building block of a written Chinese character. Every character, no matter how complex it looks, is assembled from individual strokes like this one. Think of strokes as letters inside letters: learn the strokes, and you have a key that begins to unlock the entire writing system.

Try it yourself. Pick up a pen and make a small diagonal mark: press firmly at the upper-left, then flick down and to the right, lifting gently as you go. That is 丶. Now look at the character 主 (zhǔ, meaning "main"): notice the dot perched at the very top? You just learned to write that part. 🌱 口

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. That little square is the character 口 (kǒu). It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing: a picture that became a letter.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Simple rule: Whenever 犭 appears on the left of a Chinese character, the whole character almost always connects to an animal, especially a mammal. It acts like a category label, silently announcing: "an animal lives inside this word." Even 独 (dú), the first character in 独一无二, meaning "one-of-a-kind," carries 犭 on its left side.

Try it yourself: Look closely at 独. Can you spot the 犭 on the left? Trace it with your finger — the three small strokes leaning left, like a dog tucking in its tail. Once you recognize 犭, you hold a master key: every character that carries it is telling you, right up front, that something animal-like is at the heart of its meaning. 🍃 句

Imagine you are telling a friend, "The dog jumped over the wall." That whole statement — from "The" to "wall" — is one complete thought. In Chinese, this complete unit of meaning is called a 句 (jù).

Picture this: think of a 句 as a small box. Everything the box holds belongs together — a subject doing something, or a feeling being expressed. When the box is full and closed, you have a 句. Before the box is closed, you just have loose words floating around.

Simple rule: A 句 is the smallest chunk of language that stands on its own and makes sense by itself. In written Chinese, a 句 often ends with a period (。), a question mark (？), or an exclamation mark (！) — just like sentences in English end with punctuation. One 句 = one complete thought.

Our idiom 狗急跳墙 (gǒu jí tiào qiáng, "a desperate dog leaps over the wall") is itself a single 句. It has a subject (狗, the dog), a feeling (急, desperate), and an action (跳墙, leaping over the wall). Everything fits neatly inside one box.

Try it yourself: Look at the idiom again — 狗急跳墙. Can you point to the action word (what the dog does)? That action word is the heart of this 句. Finding it helps you understand any 句 you meet.

Simple rule: 月 holds both meanings because they share one root. The moon completes roughly one full cycle every 29.5 days — and that cycle is a month. So whenever you spot 月 inside a new word, ask yourself: is this about the glowing object in the sky, or about a span of time?

Everyday example

Imagine you lend a close friend a large sum of money when they are in desperate need. Years later, not only do they never repay you, but they spread rumors to damage your reputation. You shake your head and say, "I can't believe how 狼心狗肺 that person is!"

Picture this

The phrase is built from four characters: 狼 (láng) means wolf, 心 (xīn) means heart, 狗 (gǒu) means dog, and 肺 (fèi) means lungs. Picture the cold, predatory heart of a wolf and the lowly, scavenging nature once associated with stray dogs. Together, these animal images paint a vivid portrait of someone whose inner character is savage and ungrateful — a person who repays kindness with cruelty.

Simple rule

Use 狼心狗肺 to describe a person who is heartless, vicious, and utterly without conscience — especially someone who betrays people who have helped them. It is a strong condemnation, so reserve it for genuinely wicked behavior, not everyday rudeness.

Try it yourself

Think of a character in a story or film who received help from others but repaid them with betrayal. Can you describe that character using 狼心狗肺? Try forming a sentence: "那个人真是狼心狗肺！" — "That person is truly heartless and cruel!" Notice how the four-character structure gives the phrase a punchy, memorable rhythm.
