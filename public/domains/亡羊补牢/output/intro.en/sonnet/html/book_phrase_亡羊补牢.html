🌱 丶

Think about the last time you signed your name. Somewhere in that signature, you probably left a small dot or flick — almost without thinking. That tiny mark matters. In Chinese writing, a single dot can be the difference between one character and another.

The stroke 丶 (pronounced zhǔ, though most learners just call it "the dot") is one of the simplest building blocks in all of Chinese. Picture an ink brush touching paper at a slight angle and lifting away — leaving behind a small teardrop shape, thicker at the top and tapering to a point. That's 丶.

Here is the simple rule: 丶 is not decorative — it carries meaning or shape in the characters it lives inside. For example, place a 丶 above 冂 and you get 冗; add two dots to 火 and you see them borrowed into characters like 灬 (the "fire feet" you find under 点 or 热). The dot changes the structure every time.

Try it yourself. Take a piece of paper and practice drawing 丶 ten times. Start your brush (or pen) at the top-left, press down gently, then sweep toward the lower-right and lift. Each stroke should look like a tiny raindrop frozen mid-fall. Once this motion feels natural in your hand, you will start spotting 丶 hiding inside dozens of characters you already know — and that recognition is the first small step toward reading Chinese with confidence.

🌱 卜

Imagine you are about to make a big decision — should you travel tomorrow, or stay home? In ancient China, people faced the same uncertainty and turned to a remarkable practice for guidance.

Picture this: A wise elder heats a flat animal bone over fire until tiny cracks appear across its surface. The pattern of those cracks — which way they split, how long each line runs — was believed to reveal messages from the spirit world. The elder "read" the cracks like a map of the future.

The character 卜 is a direct snapshot of that moment. The vertical stroke ( | ) represents the main crack running down the bone. The short diagonal stroke ( ╲ ) captures a smaller branch crack shooting off to the side. Together, they draw the simplest possible crack pattern — and that picture became the word for divination.

Simple rule: 卜 (pronounced bǔ, falling-rising tone) means to divine or to foretell — to seek knowledge about what has not yet happened.

In the idiom 亡羊补牢, notice that 补 (to mend, to repair) shares the same sound bǔ as 卜. Ancient characters often bundled sound and meaning this way, so recognizing 卜 as a sound clue helps you remember whole families of related words.

Try it yourself: Write 卜 by drawing the vertical stroke first, top to bottom, then add the short diagonal branch on the right. Two strokes — simple as a crack on a bone.

🌱 牛

Everyday example: Think about a farm. The hardest-working animal pulling a plow through a muddy field has traditionally been the ox — powerful, patient, and essential to survival. In Chinese culture, the ox holds that same honored place.

Picture this: The character 牛 (niú) is actually a small drawing. Look at it closely: the horizontal strokes suggest the ox's wide horns spreading left and right, and the vertical stroke below is its body. Ancient versions of this character look even more like a front-facing cow's head. Chinese writing began as pictures of the world, and 牛 is one of the clearest examples.

Simple rule: 牛 means cow, ox, or bull — any bovine animal. It is pronounced niú (second tone, like a rising question). It also carries the sense of strength and hard work. Interestingly, in modern slang 牛 means "awesome" or "impressive," because an ox is so powerful.

As a building block, 牛 appears inside other characters related to animals, agriculture, or things historically connected to cattle. Spotting it inside a complex character gives you an instant clue about its meaning.

Try it yourself: Trace the character slowly — one horizontal stroke for the horns, a shorter horizontal stroke below, then one long vertical stroke through the middle. Say niú as you write it. Can you picture the ox standing there, ready for a day's work?

Simple rule: 牢 carries two closely related meanings that flow from this image:

Both meanings share the same core idea: something built to hold and not let go.

In the phrase 亡羊补牢, 牢 is the sheep pen. The full idiom says: even after losing (亡) the sheep (羊), it still makes sense to repair (补) the pen (牢). The enclosure was broken; now you make it firm again.

Simple rule. 补 means to fill a gap — physically or figuratively. You can patch a hole (补洞, bǔ dòng), replenish supplies (补充, bǔ chōng), or make up for lost time (补救, bǔ jiù). In every case, something was incomplete or broken, and 补 is the action that restores wholeness. Think of it as the "patch" step: spot the gap, then cover it.
