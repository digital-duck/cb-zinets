Simple rule: When 丷 rests above 一, picture a pair of seeds planted on flat ground. These two components often appear together as the upper portion of a larger character. The small pair sits on top; the steady line anchors it below. Together they create a sense of balance — a little detail supported by a firm foundation.

Try it yourself: Write a single horizontal stroke: 一. Now add two small outward-angling marks above it: 丷. Notice how the pair seems to grow out of the horizontal base. Training your eye to spot 丷 and 一 inside bigger characters is your first step toward reading them with confidence.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 凉 (liáng), meaning cool or chilly. Can you find 冫 hiding on the left? Once you spot it, you already know something about the character's meaning — even before learning the whole word. That's the power of recognizing radicals.

🌱 尺

Imagine a tailor measuring fabric for a coat. She stretches a long tape from one end and calls out, "Three chǐ exactly!" That word — 尺 (chǐ) — is one of the oldest units of length in the Chinese world.

Picture this. The character 尺 looks a little like an outstretched hand with a mark on it. Ancient people often measured short distances with the span of a hand or forearm, and 尺 captures that very idea. Over centuries, the standard value was fixed at about one-third of a meter:

$$ 1 \text{ 尺} \approx \frac{1}{3} \text{ m} \approx 33.3 \text{ cm} $$

So three 尺 make roughly one meter. Larger lengths are built from this same unit: ten 尺 equal one 丈 (zhàng), a bigger measure you might see in classical poetry describing tall trees or deep wells.

Simple rule. 尺 is a unit of length equal to about $\frac{1}{3}$ meter. Think of it as a "Chinese foot" — shorter than an English foot ($\approx 30.5$ cm) but the same idea: a handy human-scale step for measuring everyday things.

Try it yourself. A standard school ruler is 30 cm long. How many 尺 is that? Set up the division:

$$ \frac{30 \text{ cm}}{33.3 \text{ cm/尺}} \approx 0.9 \text{ 尺} $$

Almost exactly one 尺 — which is why the ruler fits so naturally in your hand, just as it did for that ancient tailor.
