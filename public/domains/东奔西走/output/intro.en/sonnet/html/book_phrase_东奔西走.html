Picture this: Imagine a map with a compass rose in the center. Someone is bolting east (东, dōng), then doubling back to rush west (西, xī). The key new character here is 奔 (bēn) — to dash or bolt with urgency. It carries a sense of breathless, driven motion, not a leisurely stroll. Pair 奔 with the direction east, then mirror the whole thing with west and 走 (another word for movement), and you get a vivid picture: someone zigzagging in every direction without rest.

Simple rule: 东奔西走 (dōng bēn xī zǒu) pairs two opposite directions — east and west — to suggest everywhere. This mirroring technique is common in Chinese four-character phrases: two halves echo each other to amplify the meaning. The heart of the phrase is 奔, that urgent, driven dash. Without it, you're just traveling; with it, you're frantically on the move.
