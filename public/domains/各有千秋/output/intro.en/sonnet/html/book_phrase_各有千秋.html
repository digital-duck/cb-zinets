🌱 丿

🌱 丿

Everyday example. Think of the way water slides off a slanted roof — it flows smoothly from upper-right down to lower-left, then simply lifts away without a hook or pause. That natural, effortless sweep is exactly what the stroke 丿 feels like when you write it.

Picture this. Imagine you are drawing a single blade of grass bending in the wind. You place your pen near the top-right, glide diagonally downward toward the lower-left, and lift your pen off the page at the end. No stopping, no curling, just a clean, confident sweep. That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. Try to keep each stroke smooth and light at the end. Once this feels natural, you have mastered one of the most common building blocks in written Chinese — a small skill with enormous reach.

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Draw a vertical line for the trunk, a short horizontal stroke across the middle for the branches, and two small strokes angling downward at the base for the roots. You are looking at a tree, simplified into six strokes.

Simple rule. 木 means tree or wood — the material a tree is made of. When you see 木 inside another character, it almost always signals something made of wood or connected to trees. In our idiom 守株待兔, the character 株 (zhū) contains 木 at its left side. That tells you right away: 株 is a wooden thing — specifically, a tree stump, the stubborn leftover after a trunk is felled.

Try it yourself. Look at these two characters side by side: 木 and 株. Can you spot 木 hiding inside 株? Trace just the left-hand portion with your finger. Once you can see 木 in other characters, you will start reading Chinese like a detective — each component is a clue waiting to be found.

🍃 十

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Thousands of years ago, scribes sketched a curved outline with two small marks inside, suggesting the shadowed bands across the moon's face. Simple rule: 月 holds both meanings because they share one root. The moon completes roughly one full cycle every 29.5 days — and that cycle is a month. So whenever you spot 月 inside a new word, ask yourself: is this about the glowing object in the sky, or about a span of time?

The diagonal stroke curving downward represents the heavy seed head drooping to one side, just as real grain does when it is ready to be picked. Ancient Chinese thinkers imagined the tip of a grain stalk — the very finest, smallest part — as a symbol for the tiniest slice of time. That is why 禾 lives inside 秒, and why 秒 lives inside our phrase 争分夺秒, "racing against every second."

🍃 各

Everyday example. Imagine you and three friends are picking seats on a bus. The driver says, "$\text{each}$ person sits in $\text{their own}$ seat." Nobody shares — every single person gets a separate spot. That word "each" is exactly what the Chinese character 各 (gè) means.

Picture this. The character 各 is built from two parts. On top sits 夂 — a new primitive shaped like a foot pointing downward, meaning "to arrive" or "come." Below it is 口, the familiar mouth-shape, here standing for an opening like a doorway. Picture several feet, each arriving at its own doorway, not all crowding into the same one. That image — separate paths leading to separate places — captures the idea of "each one individually" or "respectively."

Simple rule. Use 各 whenever you want to say "each" or "every," emphasizing that things are treated one by one, not as a group. For example:

  • 各位 (gè wèi) — "everyone" (literally "each position/person," a polite way to address a group)
  • 各自 (gè zì) — "each on their own / respectively"

Think of it as the opposite of "all together" — 各 always points to individuality within a group.

Try it yourself. Next time you say goodbye to a group of friends heading different directions, imagine saying "各位平安" (gè wèi píng ān) — "safety to each of you." You're not wishing for one shared safety, but a separate, personal safe journey for every single person — that's the spirit of 各.

Everyday example. Imagine you are holding an apple. You grip it in your hand, and it is yours — you have it. That simple act of holding, of possessing, is the heart of the character 有 (yǒu), which means "to have" or "there is."

Picture this. Look at 有 closely. The top part, 又 (yòu), is an ancient sketch of a hand reaching out — fingers spread, ready to grasp. Below it sits a curved shape that ancient scribes used to suggest something solid and precious being held. Put them together: a hand closing around something valuable. That image captures the exact moment something passes from "out there" into "mine" — from absence into presence.

Simple rule. Whenever you want to say someone has something, or that something exists, 有 is your word. In the language of this book, think of it as the operator that transforms $\varnothing$ (nothing) into $\exists$ (something that exists):

$$ \varnothing \xrightarrow{\,有\,} \exists $$

It is the bridge that turns emptiness into a world with real, hold-able things in it.

Try it yourself. Look around you right now and pick three objects within reach — a pen, a phone, a cup. For each one, say quietly: I have this. Notice the feeling of presence, of something being there. That felt sense of "yes, this exists in my world" is exactly what 有 names. Hold onto it — you will need it when we meet its opposite, 无.

🌸 Phrase 各有千秋

🌸 Phrase 各有千秋

Everyday example: Imagine you ask two friends which season they like best. One says summer — long days, swimming, ice cream. The other says autumn — crisp air, colorful leaves, harvest festivals. Neither is wrong. They simply love different things, and both choices have real beauty.

Picture this: The phrase 各有千秋 (gè yǒu qiān qiū) is built from four characters. 各 means "each" or "every one." 有 means "to have." 千秋 literally means "a thousand autumns" — but in Chinese culture, autumn (秋) is the season of harvest and ripeness, so 千秋 became a poetic way of saying "great merit" or "lasting worth." Put it together: each one has its own thousand autumns — each thing has its own kind of excellence.

Simple rule: Use 各有千秋 when you want to say that two or more things are different but equally valuable — not that one beats the other, but that each shines in its own way. Think of it as the Chinese equivalent of "to each their own merit."

Try it yourself: Think of two styles of music you enjoy — perhaps classical and jazz. How would you describe them using the spirit of 各有千秋? Write one sentence explaining what makes each one special in its own way, without ranking them. Notice how this phrase encourages appreciation rather than competition.
