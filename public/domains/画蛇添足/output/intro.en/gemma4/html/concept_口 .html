🌱 口

Have you ever explained something simple—like how to make toast—and found yourself adding steps that weren't necessary? You might detail the toaster model, explain the optimal bread density, and predict crumbs. This over-detailing is what we mean by "speaking into the mouth" (口). In everyday life, this happens when we use excessive words or unnecessary explanations just to sound knowledgeable. We are adding features that don't exist in the original idea.

Picture this: Imagine a friend drew a perfect stick figure of a person. Instead of simply pointing and saying, "This is John," you start listing his favorite snacks, his childhood pet, and his shoe size. You haven't added core information; you’ve just filled the space with tangential details. The mouth (口) here represents the unnecessary outpouring—the verbal equivalent of drawing legs on a snake. It dilutes the message's power.

The simple rule to remember is: Clarity does not require volume. When communicating, ask yourself if the piece of information you are about to add is essential for understanding the main point. If the answer is no, it might be over-elaboration. Focus on directness and conciseness rather than comprehensive coverage.

Try it yourself by summarizing a complex topic you know well (like how photosynthesis works) into exactly three simple sentences. Keep your language tight and identify any word that could potentially be cut without losing the core meaning.
