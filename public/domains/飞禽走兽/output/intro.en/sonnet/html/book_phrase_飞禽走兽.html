🌱 亠乂凵

Think about building with LEGO blocks. No matter how complex the finished model looks, it is always made from the same familiar pieces snapping together. Chinese characters work exactly the same way — every character is built from a small set of recurring shapes called components (sometimes called radicals when they carry meaning on their own).

Today, meet three of these fundamental pieces: 亠, 乂, and 凵.

Picture this: 亠 looks like a small hat or lid — two strokes that cap whatever sits beneath them. 乂 resembles two paths crossing each other, like a brushstroke X drawn with gentle curves. And 凵 is like an open box or cup sitting upright, ready to hold something inside.

A simple rule to carry forward: each component has a shape identity you can recognize at a glance, no matter which full character it appears in. Once you lock in what 亠, 乂, and 凵 look like in isolation, your eye will automatically spot them hiding inside larger, more complex characters — just as you might recognize a familiar LEGO brick inside a finished rocket ship.

🌱 田

Think about how a farmer lays out a plot of land. She draws one line down the middle and one across, and suddenly one large patch becomes four tidy, equal sections. That simple grid is exactly what the character 田 (tián) shows: a rectangle divided by a cross into four boxes. It is an ancient, aerial picture of a plowed field, frozen into a single brushstroke.

Picture yourself looking down from above at farmland. You would see the outer boundary — the edge of the property — and then the inner paths that split the soil into workable squares. The character 田 captures that bird's-eye view. Every line has a purpose: the border keeps the field contained, and the cross divides it into equal, organized parts. Order and structure, side by side.

Carry this image forward: whenever you spot 田 inside a character, it is signaling something divided, organized, or measured out. Fields were the foundation of ancient life, so 田 shows up inside many characters connected to work, effort, and the natural world.

Here is the rule to carry forward: 田 means "a field divided into equal parts" — organized earth, ready for something to grow.

Try it yourself: Look at 男 (nán, meaning man), which places 田 on top of 力 (strength). What story does that pairing hint at?

🌸 Phrase 飞禽走兽

Imagine you are describing a nature documentary. Instead of listing every creature one by one — eagles, deer, wolves, sparrows, tigers — you wave your hand and say, "the whole animal kingdom." One sweep, everything included. Chinese has a elegant four-character phrase that does exactly this.

飞禽走兽 (fēi qín zǒu shòu) pairs two vivid images side by side: "flying birds" and "running beasts." The first half, 飞禽, points upward — creatures of the sky. The second half, 走兽, points to the ground — four-legged creatures that roam the earth. Together they form a complete picture of the animal world, not by listing every species but by naming the two extremes and letting your imagination fill in everything between.

This is a recurring trick in Chinese: choose two contrasting poles — sky and ground, morning and evening, mountains and seas — then present them as a pair to suggest the whole. You are not just describing two things; you are drawing a line between opposite ends and implying everything along that line is included.

Simple rule: pair two vivid contrasts → imply a complete category.

Try it yourself: Think of another pair of opposites that together cover a whole group. Hot and cold? Young and old? How would you use just two images to suggest "all temperatures" or "all ages"? Jot down your pair — you are already thinking the way this phrase does.
