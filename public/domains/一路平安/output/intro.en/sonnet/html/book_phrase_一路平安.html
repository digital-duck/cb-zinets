🌱 口

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. That little square is the character 口 (kǒu). It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing: a picture that became a letter.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

🌱 夂

Everyday example: Think about the last steps you take when you finally walk through your own front door after a long day — slow, a little tired, but arriving all the same. That feeling of "getting there" is exactly what the character component 夂 captures.

Picture this: 夂 is a tiny pictograph — a drawing turned into a symbol — of a foot or leg stepping downward, trailing behind the body. Ancient writers used it to show someone arriving somewhere, often with a sense of slowness or finality, like the last leg of a journey. You won't usually see 夂 standing alone as a full word; instead, it shows up as a building block inside bigger characters, quietly adding the meaning "to go," "to arrive," or "to come to an end."

Simple rule: Whenever you spot 夂 tucked into a character, ask yourself: "Could this character be about movement, arrival, or something winding down?" For example, it appears in 冬 (dōng, "winter") — the season when the year's journey comes to a close, and in 各 (gè, "each"), where each person arrives at their own place.

Try it yourself: Next time you see a character with a small hook-like shape near the bottom, look for 夂. Ask: is something here finishing its journey? That question alone will help you start reading characters like a detective, piece by piece.

🍃 各

🍃 各

Everyday example. Imagine you and three friends are picking seats on a bus. The driver says, "$\text{each}$ person sits in $\text{their own}$ seat." Nobody shares — every single person gets a separate spot. That word "each" is exactly what the Chinese character 各 (gè) means.

Picture this. The character 各 is built from two parts. On top sits 夂 — a new primitive shaped like a foot pointing downward, meaning "to arrive" or "come." Below it is 口, the familiar mouth-shape, here standing for an opening like a doorway. Picture several feet, each arriving at its own doorway, not all crowding into the same one. That image — separate paths leading to separate places — captures the idea of "each one individually" or "respectively."

Simple rule. Use 各 whenever you want to say "each" or "every," emphasizing that things are treated one by one, not as a group. For example:

  • 各位 (gè wèi) — "everyone" (literally "each position/person," a polite way to address a group)
  • 各自 (gè zì) — "each on their own / respectively"

Think of it as the opposite of "all together" — 各 always points to individuality within a group.

Try it yourself. Next time you say goodbye to a group of friends heading different directions, imagine saying "各位平安" (gè wèi píng ān) — "safety to each of you." You're not wishing for one shared safety, but a separate, personal safe journey for every single person — that's the spirit of 各.

Everyday example. Imagine you are walking down a street in China and you see a sign for a "Women's Restroom." Next to the English words, there is often a single Chinese character: 女. This character means "woman" or "female," and it is one of the oldest and most useful building blocks in the Chinese writing system.

Picture this. The character 女 was originally drawn to look like a kneeling woman, with curved lines suggesting her arms and posture. Over thousands of years, the picture was simplified into the three brushstrokes you see today, but you can still imagine a gentle, flowing figure if you look closely. A "character" here just means one written symbol that carries its own meaning and sound — unlike English letters, which only make sounds.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 女 standing alone, it means "woman" or "female." But here's the exciting part: 女 also appears as a small piece inside many other characters, often hinting that the new character relates to women, family, or relationships — for example, 好 (good, made of "woman" + "child") or 妈 (mother). Spotting 女 inside a bigger character is like recognizing a familiar face in a crowd — it gives you a clue about meaning.

Try it yourself. Next time you see a Chinese character, scan it for small, recognizable shapes like 女. Can you find 女 hiding inside the word for "mother," 妈?

Everyday example

Imagine you just got home after a long day. You open the door, step inside, and immediately feel safe — the walls and roof around you keep out the wind, the rain, and the noise of the street. That feeling of "I am sheltered" is exactly what the Chinese component 宀 represents.

Picture this

宀 looks like a small roof sitting on top of two side walls, almost like a tiny house drawn with just a few strokes: a dot on top, a horizontal line, and two strokes sloping down on either side. It is not a complete character by itself in everyday writing — instead, it works as a radical, a recurring piece that sits at the top of many characters and gives a hint about their meaning. Whenever you see 宀 capping a character, think "this character has something to do with a house, a room, or being covered and protected."

Simple rule

Rule: 宀 = roof/house meaning, always placed at the top of a character.

You'll meet it again soon in characters like 家 (home), 安 (peaceful, literally "a woman under a roof"), and 室 (room). Each time, the 宀 on top is doing the same job — telling you "shelter is involved here."

Try it yourself

Look at any character you're learning that starts with a shape resembling a tiny roof. Cover up everything below 宀 with your finger and ask: "What part of this character is hiding under the roof?" That question is the first step toward understanding how 宀 builds meaning into bigger characters.

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Picture this. The character 安 is built from two parts stacked together. On top is 宀 (mián), a small roof-like shape that represents a house or shelter — this is the one new building block to remember today. Underneath it sits a simple figure, like a person curled up and resting. Put them together and you get a person settled safely beneath a roof: the picture of security, stillness, and relief. When you see 安, imagine someone finally arriving home after a long journey and breathing a sigh of relief.

Simple rule. Whenever 安 appears in a word, it usually points toward safety, stability, or calm. For example, 平安 (píng ān) means "peace and safety" — exactly the meaning behind the phrase 一路平安, "safe travels the whole way." The roof (宀) is your visual cue for protection: spot it, and you know shelter and comfort are nearby. Think of it as a simple formula: $\text{shelter} + \text{person resting} = \text{peace}$.

Everyday example. Picture a table with one short leg. Your cup wobbles, your bowl tips, your homework slides off the edge. Now picture the table fixed — all four legs touching the ground evenly. Nothing wobbles. Nothing tips. That steady, even table is exactly what the Chinese character 平 (píng) means: flat, even, level, calm.

Picture this. The character 平 looks like a balance scale seen from the front — a flat horizontal line resting evenly across the middle, with small symmetrical strokes on either side, like two pans hanging in perfect balance. Nothing leans left or right. That visual balance is the meaning: when two sides match exactly, we say things are 平.

Simple rule. Think of 平 as describing any situation where there is no difference, no bump, no disturbance between two sides or two moments in time:

  • A flat road has no hills: 平 (level).
  • A calm sea has no waves: 平 (calm).
  • A fair trade has no one losing out: 平 (equal, fair).

In the phrase 一路平安 ("yī lù píng ān," a common travel blessing), 平 carries the sense of a journey with no bumps — smooth and uneventful in the best way.

Try it yourself. Next time someone is about to travel, imagine wishing them a road with no potholes and a sea with no waves. That wish — "may everything stay level and calm for you" — is the feeling carried by 平. Can you think of one English word that captures both "flat" and "calm" at once? Put it together, and you get a character that literally carries the idea of "feet finding their way."

Simple rule: Whenever you see 路 in a word, think "route" or "journey." For example, in the phrase 一路平安 (yī lù píng ān) — "may your whole journey be peaceful" — 路 marks the entire trip, not just the road surface. It's the experience of traveling, start to finish.

Try it yourself: Next time you say goodbye to someone heading on a trip, try saying "一路平安!" Picture their whole path — every step, every mile — wrapped in safety. That mental image is 路: not a static object, but a living stretch of travel connecting one place to another.

🌸 Phrase 一路平安

Everyday example: Imagine your friend is leaving for a long trip. Before she gets in the car, you wave and say, "Have a safe journey!" In Chinese, people express this exact wish with four characters: 一路平安 (yí lù píng ān).

Picture this: Break the phrase into its building blocks. 一 (yī) means "one," and 路 (lù) means "road" or "journey" — together, 一路 means "the whole way" or "all along the road." 平 (píng) means "peaceful" or "level," and 安 (ān) means "safe" or "at ease" — together, 平安 means "peace and safety." Combine them, and 一路平安 literally paints the picture of someone traveling smoothly down a calm, level road, safe from start to finish.

Simple rule: 一路平安 is a fixed four-character phrase called a chengyu (成语) — a compact saying whose meaning goes beyond just adding up the individual words. You say it as a farewell wish, especially to someone about to travel by car, train, plane, or even on a long walk. It works like "safe travels" or "bon voyage" in English, but carries a slightly more heartfelt, blessing-like tone.

Try it yourself: Next time someone you know is heading off on a trip — to the airport, the train station, or just driving home late at night — try saying "一路平安!" to them. Notice how it combines a wish for the whole journey (一路) with a wish for safety and calm (平安) in just four syllables.
