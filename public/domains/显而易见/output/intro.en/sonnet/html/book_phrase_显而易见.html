🌸 Phrase 显而易见

Everyday example. Imagine you walk outside and the ground is soaking wet. Without checking a weather app, you know it rained last night. No one needs to explain it — the answer is right in front of you.

Picture this. The phrase 显而易见 (xiǎn ér yì jiàn) captures exactly that feeling. Break it into four characters: 显 means visible or apparent, 而 is a small linking word meaning and, 易 means easy, and 见 means to see. Put them together: "visible and easy to see." Together they describe something so plain that spotting it requires almost no effort at all.

Simple rule. Use 显而易见 when a conclusion follows so naturally from what is right in front of you that stating it feels almost unnecessary. In English the closest match is "it is obvious that…" or "evidently…". For example: 显而易见，他很累了 — "Obviously, he is very tired" (you can see it on his face).

Try it yourself. Look around the room. Pick one thing that anyone could notice at a glance — a bright color, a loud sound, an open window. Now complete this sentence aloud:

显而易见，____________。

The phrase is your signal that the evidence is already visible; you are simply inviting others to look at what is already there. Keep it in mind whenever something feels too clear to need explanation.

You have spent this book learning individual characters — tiny building blocks of meaning. Now those blocks click together into something bigger: 显而易见 (xiǎn ér yì jiàn), a four-character phrase that means "obvious" or "plain to see."

Everyday example. Imagine a friend asks why the sky is blue. You might shrug and say, "Well, it's just… obvious, isn't it?" That shrug — that feeling of instant clarity — is exactly what 显而易见 captures.

Picture this. The phrase is a little sentence hidden inside four characters: 显 (visible) + 而 (and) + 易 (easy) + 见 (to see). Together they paint the image: "it appears, and it is easy to see." The meaning arrives the same way the thing it describes does — all at once, without effort.

Simple rule. When something requires no explanation, no proof, no argument — when the evidence is right there in front of you — Chinese speakers reach for 显而易见. It works like the English phrases "clearly" or "needless to say" placed at the start of a sentence.

Try it yourself. Notice a moment today when something feels undeniably true — the sun is warm, a smile means happiness. Try framing it as: 显而易见，___。 ("It is plain to see that ___.") Writing even one sentence cements the phrase in your memory far better than any list.
