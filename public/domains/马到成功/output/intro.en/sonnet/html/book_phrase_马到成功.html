🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Think of it as drawing a $1$ on a piece of paper, but without the little foot or hat — just the bare vertical line itself. Formally, you can think of it as a line segment along the $y$-axis: moving in the direction $(0, -1)$ from start to end.

Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. 🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

Picture this: 厶 looks like a little curling loop — imagine a snail tucking into its shell, or a person hugging something tightly to their chest, keeping it close. It is one of the smallest building blocks you will encounter, but do not let its size fool you. When you spot this curled shape nestled inside a larger character, it often carries a quiet hint about the self or something personal and hidden.

Simple rule: On its own, 厶 means private or self. Now watch what happens when you place 八 (meaning "to divide" or "to share") on top of it:

$$ 八 + 厶 = 公 $$

That new character, 公 (gōng), means public. The logic is elegant: when you share (八) something private (厶), it stops belonging just to you and becomes something for everyone. One small component, flipped in meaning by a single addition.

Try it yourself: Scan characters you are beginning to learn and hunt for that little curling loop. Can you find 厶 hiding inside 去 (qù, "to go") or 允 (yǔn, "to permit")? 🌱 戊

Everyday example. Imagine a kitchen timer that clicks through ten positions before returning to start. Ancient Chinese scholars invented exactly that kind of timer — not for cooking, but for counting days, months, and years. They called the ten positions the Heavenly Stems (天干, tiāngān). 戊 (wù) is position number five — the midpoint click.

Picture this. Draw the number line $1, 2, 3, 4, \mathbf{5}, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10$. Underline the bold 5. That underlined spot is 戊 — the exact center of the cycle. Because it sits at the midpoint, traditional scholars linked it to earth (土, tǔ), the element of balance and stability, the ground beneath your feet.

Simple rule. The ten Heavenly Stems form an ordered set $\{甲, 乙, 丙, 丁, 戊, 己, 庚, 辛, 壬, 癸\}$, indexed $1$ through $10$. So:

$$ \text{戊} = \text{Stem}_5 $$

Whenever you see 戊 in a date — say, 戊戌年 (the Year of the Earth Dog) — you know you are in a year whose Stem index is exactly $5$ within that sixty-year cycle.

Try it yourself. Look up the Chinese year you were born. Find its Heavenly Stem. Is it 戊? If the answer is yes, your birth year lands right on that stable, earthy midpoint. If not, count forward or backward from 戊 to locate your own Stem — each step is one position in the sequence.

🍃 至

Imagine an arrow flying through the air and finally hitting the ground — it has arrived. That moment of landing is exactly what the character 至 (zhì) captures.

Picture this. The ancient form of 至 looks like an upside-down arrow with a short horizontal line beneath it. The line is the ground; the arrow is pointing straight down at it. Together they show the instant something reaches its destination — the journey is complete, the point has been touched.

Simple rule. 至 carries two related meanings that grow from the same image. First, it means to arrive at or to reach: 至今 (zhì jīn) means "up to now" — time has arrived at the present moment. Second, because arriving at a limit means you can go no further, 至 also means utmost or extreme: 至少 (zhì shǎo) means "at least," the smallest possible amount — the very bottom of a range.

Both meanings share one idea: something has gone as far as it can go.

The very first character in that phrase is 马, and it simply means horse.

Picture this. The character 马 is a simplified drawing of a horse seen from the side. In ancient oracle-bone script — carvings made on bones thousands of years ago — 马 looked almost like a cartoon: a head, a body, four legs, and a flowing mane. Over centuries the picture was streamlined into the tidy shape we write today, but the horse is still hiding inside those strokes.

Simple rule. 马 is pronounced mǎ (third tone — your voice dips then rises, like a question that answers itself). It means horse on its own, and it acts as a building block inside compound words. Whenever you spot 马 in a new word, think horse and you will often be close to the meaning: 马路 (mǎ lù, "horse road" → street), 马上 (mǎ shàng, "on horseback" → immediately, right now).

Try it yourself. Trace 马 with your finger: start with the left downstroke, add the enclosing body, then finish with three short horizontal strokes at the bottom representing the legs. Say mǎ out loud three times. Now say the full phrase — mǎ dào chéng gōng — and congratulate yourself: you just used your first four-character Chinese idiom!

🌸 Phrase 马到成功

🌸 Phrase 马到成功

Have you ever wished a friend "good luck" before a big test or job interview? Chinese has a phrase that does the same thing — but with a vivid image packed inside it.

Picture this. Imagine ancient China: a general sends his cavalry galloping toward a distant goal. The moment the horses arrive (到, dào), the battle is already won. Speed, power, and instant victory — all in one image.

That image gave us the four-character phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng):

Character Pinyin Meaning
马 mǎ horse
到 dào to arrive
成 chéng to accomplish
功 gōng merit / achievement

Simple rule. 马到成功 is a chéngyǔ — a fixed four-character idiom that carries a complete story in just four syllables. You use it as a blessing: say it to someone before they attempt something difficult, wishing them swift, effortless success. Think of it as the Chinese equivalent of "you've got this!" but with the cavalry already on the way.

Try it yourself. A classmate is about to present a project. You can say:

祝你马到成功！(Zhù nǐ mǎ dào chéng gōng!) — "Wishing you immediate success!"

Notice how the four characters paint a complete scene: the horse arrives, success follows. That's the magic of chéngyǔ — ancient wisdom in a single breath.
