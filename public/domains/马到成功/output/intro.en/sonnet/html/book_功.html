🌱 力

Everyday example. Think about pushing open a heavy door. Your muscles tighten, your feet press into the floor, and energy flows from your body into that door. The Chinese character 力 (lì) captures exactly this idea: force, strength, power — the raw effort you put into anything you do.

Picture this. The character 力 started as a tiny sketch of a plow cutting through soil, or perhaps a flexed arm ready to lift. Look at its two strokes: a curved sweep and a firm horizontal cross. Imagine a farmer leaning into a plow — that tension between body and earth is 力 in a single image.

Simple rule. 力 works in two ways. On its own it means strength or effort. Inside other characters it acts as a radical — a building block that hints at meaning. A radical is simply a recurring piece that tells you a character involves some kind of force or exertion. Once you learn to recognize 力, you will start spotting it tucked inside many characters, always whispering "strength lives here." We will meet one of those characters in the next section.

Try it yourself. Write 力 a few times. Notice how the downward stroke feels like pressing energy into the page. As you practise, pay attention to the shape — soon you will be hunting for it hidden inside more complex characters.

🌱 工

Think about a carpenter shaping a piece of wood, a potter at the wheel, or a coder typing away at a keyboard. What do they all share? They are doing work — applying skill and effort to create something. The Chinese character 工 (gōng) captures exactly this idea.

Picture this. Look at the shape of 工. It has a flat top stroke, a vertical stroke dropping straight down, and a flat bottom stroke. Some people imagine a simple tool — like a ruler or a set square — sitting on a workbench. A tool is something you use to get work done, and that is the heart of this character's meaning.

Simple rule. 工 means work, worker, or craft. Whenever you see it inside a larger character or a phrase, it usually points to effort, skill, or something being made with care. In our phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng), the character 工 lives inside 功 (gōng), which means achievement or success. The idea is lovely: work is the foundation that achievement is built on. You cannot have 功 without 工 sitting right there at its core.

Try it yourself. Write 工 three times: top horizontal stroke first, then the vertical stroke downward, then the bottom horizontal stroke. As you write, think of one kind of work you enjoy. That feeling? That is 工.

🍃 功

Everyday example. Think about the moment you finally ride a bicycle without training wheels. All those wobbly afternoons added up — your effort turned into a real achievement. In Chinese, the single character 功 (gōng) captures exactly that idea: merit gained through effort, or simply achievement.

Picture this. Look at 功 and you will spot two pieces sitting side by side. On the left is 工 (gōng, "work") — a simple frame that looks like a carpenter's bench, with a flat top, a flat base, and a vertical stroke connecting them. On the right is 力 (lì, "strength"), which you already know. Put work together with strength and you get 功: the result that comes from applying real effort.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 功 in a word, it points to something accomplished or a skill earned. For example, 成功 (chéng gōng) means "success," and 功夫 (gōng fu) means a skill perfected through practice — yes, the same word behind "kung fu." In our phrase 马到成功, 功 is the payoff at the finish line: the achievement that arrives the moment the horse charges in.

Try it yourself. Cover the right half of 功. You should see 工 — work. Now cover the left half. You see 力 — strength. Say the full character aloud: gōng. Can you name one personal 功, one achievement that took both work and strength?
