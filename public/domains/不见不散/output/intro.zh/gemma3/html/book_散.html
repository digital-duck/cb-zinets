🌱 井

Let’s talk about something incredibly common – a well, or 井 (jǐ) in Chinese. You've probably seen one before, maybe in a village in China, or perhaps even in an old storybook! It looks like a hole dug deep into the ground, and it holds water.

Everyday Example: Think about getting a drink of water on a hot day. You might go to a tap, right? A 井 is essentially a very, very long-term tap. Instead of drawing water from a river or lake, people dug these wells deep into the earth where the groundwater – water trapped underground – was naturally located.

Picture this: Imagine you’re building a castle. You need water to wash your hands and maybe even power a mill. A 井 provides that reliable source of fresh water. It's like having a hidden reservoir, always ready when you need it.

Simple Rule: A 井 works because the ground isn’t completely solid rock. There are spaces between the dirt and stones – tiny gaps really – where water can collect and stay. These spaces form what we call groundwater. The 井 is just a way to access that groundwater.

Try it yourself: Next time you see a well, think about how it gets its water. It’s not magic! It’s simply tapping into a natural supply of water hidden beneath our feet. The depth of the 井 (jǐ) often tells us something about how much water is available – deeper wells usually mean more water.

🌱 攵

🌱 攵

Let’s explore a unique stroke in Chinese calligraphy – the 攵. It looks like a small arrow pointing downwards, as if you're pushing something away gently. Think of it this way: imagine cleaning up a messy table with a cloth. You use a quick wipe, right? The 攵 is similar, but shorter and more energetic.

Picture this: you’re a farmer gathering crops in autumn (秋收 – qiū shōu – “autumn harvest”). As the warm summer days pass (春生夏长 – chūn shēng xià cháng – “spring gives birth, summer grows long”), the rice plants are full and heavy with grain. You use a tool to cut the stalks quickly downwards. That’s what the 攵 stroke shows!

The basic rule is: the 攵 always moves from the top of the line down to the bottom. It represents a direction – downward movement – and it's meant to feel energetic. Don't curve or swirl it; just draw it straight down.

Try it yourself! Grab a piece of paper and a pen or pencil. Draw the 攵 stroke several times, focusing on moving your hand downwards clearly. It’s okay if it doesn’t look perfect at first – just practice getting a feel for this simple but strong symbol.

🌱 月

🌱 月

Let’s talk about the moon! It's a beautiful sight in the night sky, isn't it? Think about when you’re sitting outside on a warm evening, and you look up – there it is, hanging like a giant pearl. It’s a constant companion to us here on Earth.

Everyday Example: Imagine you’re baking cookies. You might use a timer to make sure they don't burn. The moon seems to follow a similar pattern – it rises, shines brightly, and then sets, just like the sun!

Picture this: Imagine a giant ball of rock and dust, much smaller than Earth, orbiting our planet. That’s essentially what the Moon is. It doesn’t make its own light; instead, it reflects sunlight back to us.

Simple Rule: The moon goes through phases – we call these phases. These are simply different ways we see how much of the sunlit side of the Moon is facing us as it orbits Earth. It's like seeing a partially lit orange from different angles!

Try it yourself: Look up at the night sky tonight. Can you spot the moon? If it’s not full, try to notice if it looks smaller or larger. These changes are the moon’s phases in action. You can even find a simple lunar phase calendar online – they show you exactly when each phase will occur!

🍃 散

🍃 散

Let’s start with a simple observation: think about scattering seeds on grass. You throw them out, and they don't all land perfectly together, do they? They spread out – that’s what “散” (sǎn) is often referring to.

Picture this: Imagine you’re building a sandcastle on the beach. The waves come in and wash over it, slowly eroding the walls and spreading the sand around. That spreading of sand is an example of something becoming 散.

Simple rule: “散” describes when things separate or spread out from a central point or arrangement. It’s about losing order and becoming dispersed. It doesn't mean completely disappearing, just that you no longer have a tight grouping. Think of it like this: a group of friends might 散 (sǎn) after a fun day together, meaning they move on to do their own things independently.

Try it yourself: Take a handful of rice grains. Now, gently shake your hand. What happens to the rice? They’ll scatter – they become 散. You can also try drawing a circle with a marker and then quickly spreading your fingers around it; you'll see the ink starts to spread out, becoming 散. This simple action helps illustrate the core idea of “散”.
