🌱 井

🌱 井

Everyday example. Imagine you are thirsty on a hot summer day. Long before water came from faucets, people dug deep holes in the ground to reach underground water. That hole — a well — is exactly what the character 井 (jǐng, pronounced like "jing") represents.

Picture this. Look at the shape of 井 closely. It looks like a square divided into four sections — almost like a bird's-eye view of a stone-lined well with two wooden beams crossing its opening to hold a bucket rope. Ancient Chinese writers drew precisely that: a top-down sketch of a well's mouth. The two horizontal strokes and two vertical strokes crossing them form a little grid, mapping the shaft below and the rim above.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 井, think well — a deep, narrow hole in the ground that holds water. In our idiom 井底之蛙, 井 appears first and sets the entire scene. A well is small, deep, and enclosed. Those three qualities — smallness, depth, enclosure — echo through every character and idea that follows in the story.

Try it yourself. Draw 井 from memory: lay down two horizontal strokes first, then cross them with two vertical strokes. Does it remind you of a tic-tac-toe grid? That square pattern is the exact shape a well's opening makes when you peer down at it from above. Every time you write it, you are drawing a tiny map of a world that is about to feel very, very small.

🌱 攵

Imagine a teacher tapping a student's shoulder to get their attention, or a parent gently patting a child's hand to guide them. That small, deliberate action — a controlled tap or strike — is exactly what the component 攵 (pronounced roughly like pū) captures.

Picture this: 攵 looks like a hand holding a short stick, poised to give a gentle knock. In the ancient pictograph, you can almost see an arm swinging downward in a purposeful, light motion. It is not a violent blow; it is a directed, intentional touch.

Simple rule: Whenever you spot 攵 on the right side of a character, think guided action — someone is doing something with purpose and force. 攵 acts like a signal that says, "a hand is involved, and something is being set in motion." In the character 放 (fàng, to release or set free), 攵 combines with 方 (a direction marker) to suggest directing something outward — nudging it loose, letting it go. That is the heart of 解放: first 解 unties the knot, then 放 (with its 攵) pushes the thing free.

Try it yourself: Look at the character 改 (gǎi, to change) and 收 (shōu, to collect). Can you spot 攵 in each one? Notice how both involve a deliberate human action — correcting something, or gathering something in. 攵 is the silent reminder that a hand made this happen.

🌱 月

Everyday example: Picture a clear night sky — that slim, silvery crescent hanging in the darkness. Almost every culture on Earth has its own word for it. In Chinese, that word is 月 (yuè), and it carries two meanings at once: moon and month.

Picture this: The character 月 is literally a tiny drawing of the crescent moon. Thousands of years ago, scribes sketched a curved outline with two small marks inside, suggesting the shadowed bands across the moon's face. Over centuries, brushstrokes were squared off into the compact shape you see today. Think of it as a photograph that slowly became calligraphy: 🌙 → 月.

Simple rule: 月 holds both meanings because they share one root. The moon completes roughly one full cycle every 29.5 days — and that cycle is a month. So whenever you spot 月 inside a new word, ask yourself: is this about the glowing object in the sky, or about a span of time?

Try it yourself: You already know 一 (yī) — "one." Put those two together and you get 一月 (yī yuè), meaning January, literally "month one." Now think about the phrase 一见钟情 — "love at first sight." Which meaning of 月 matches that sudden, electric feeling: the moon glowing in the sky, or a measured stretch of time? Say your answer aloud before reading on.

🌱 不

Imagine a traffic light turning red. The red light means stop — do not go. In English, we add the word "not" to flip a sentence from yes to no. Chinese has a single character that does exactly the same job: 不 (bù).

Picture this. You already know 学, which means to learn. The moment you place 不 in front of it — 不学 — the meaning flips completely: not learning. It is like a tiny switch. Whatever word follows 不, that action or quality is negated, turned off, reversed.

In our saying, 不 appears twice:

$$ \text{学而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{思则罔，思而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{学则殆} $$

Each time, 不 reaches forward and negates the character right after it. The second 不 negates 学 — giving us not learning, a phrase you can already read. We will meet the other characters one by one in the sections ahead.

Simple rule. Place 不 directly before any verb or adjective to negate it:

$$ \text{不} + \text{[word]} = \text{not [word]} $$

No extra words needed — 不 does all the work alone.

Try it yourself. You know that 学 means to learn. What does 不学 mean? If you said not learning, you already understand 不. Now look back at the full saying — can you find both places where 不 appears and flips the meaning?

🍃 散

🍃 散

Everyday example. Imagine a handful of dandelion seeds. The moment you blow, they drift apart in every direction — each seed finds its own path through the air. That moment of flying apart is exactly what 散 (sàn) captures.

Picture this. 散 means to scatter or to disperse — things that were together come apart and spread out. Think of a crowd leaving a stadium after a match: people who filled one space now fan out across the whole city. That spreading-out motion is the heart of 散.

In the phrase 不见不散, the character plays a promise-keeping role. 不 means "not," 见 means "meet/see," so the phrase reads literally as "not see, not scatter" — in other words, "I won't leave until I see you." Rather than drifting apart like those dandelion seeds, the two people stay put, waiting for the moment they find each other.

Simple rule. Whenever 散 appears, look for something moving from one place to many places — seeds, people, light, even a crowd's energy. The core idea is always: together → apart.

Try it yourself. Close your eyes and picture rain falling on a still pond. Each drop hits the surface and sends ripples spreading outward in every direction. Can you feel that outward movement? That's 散 in action. Next time you make plans with a friend, try saying 不见不散 — promise each other: neither of us leaves until we meet!

🌱 见

Have you ever walked into a room and instantly noticed someone — before they even said a word? That moment of seeing is exactly what the character 见 (jiàn) captures.

Picture this: Imagine a person standing upright, with one enormous eye at the top. Ancient Chinese scribes drew 见 almost exactly that way — a figure whose entire upper body is an eye. The character is telling you: this is a being defined by the act of looking.

Simple rule: 见 means to see or to meet — and in Chinese, those two ideas are deliciously linked. When you truly see someone, you are also meeting them; when you meet someone, you are truly seeing them for the first time.

In our phrase 一见钟情 (yī jiàn zhōng qíng — "love at first sight"), 见 is the electric moment: one glance, and everything changes. It is the hinge the whole phrase swings on.

🌸 Phrase 不见散

🌸 Phrase 不见散

Everyday example. Imagine you and a friend are heading to a busy train station to meet, but you're not sure exactly which entrance they'll use. Before you leave home, you text: "I'll keep waiting — don't worry, I won't go anywhere until I see you." That promise of loyal waiting is captured perfectly by the phrase 不见不散 (bù jiàn bù sàn).

Picture this. The phrase is built from just three distinct characters used four times:

$$ \underbrace{不}_{not}\;\underbrace{见}_{see/meet}\;\underbrace{不}_{not}\;\underbrace{散}_{scatter/leave} $$

Read left to right, you get: "Not see, not scatter" — meaning "I will not leave until I have seen you."

Simple rule. In Chinese, doubling a negative condition this way creates a strong mutual pledge: if A doesn't happen, B won't happen either. Here the rule is:

$$ \text{if } \neg\,\text{见} \implies \neg\,\text{散} $$

In plain words: as long as we haven't met, neither of us walks away. It turns waiting into a two-sided promise.

Try it yourself. Next time you arrange to meet a friend somewhere crowded — a market, a concert, a festival — try saying 不见不散 (bù jiàn bù sàn) before you part ways. Notice how four short syllables carry the warmth of "I've got you; I'll be there." That's the quiet power packed into this tiny phrase.
