🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

🌸 Phrase 狗急跳墙

🌸 Phrase 狗急跳墙

Have you ever seen someone act completely out of character when they felt totally cornered — maybe a usually quiet classmate suddenly shouting during an argument, or a shy person making a wild move in a game when losing badly? That surprising behavior has a name in Chinese.

Picture this: A dog is being chased around a yard. Normally, dogs don't jump walls — they're too high, too risky. But when the dog is desperate, when it has absolutely no other way out, it gathers every bit of strength and leaps over the wall. The impossible suddenly becomes possible — not from skill, but from sheer panic.

The idiom 狗急跳墙 (gǒu jí tiào qiáng) captures exactly this idea. Broken down: 狗 means dog, 急 means anxious or desperate, 跳 means jump, and 墙 means wall. Together: "A desperate dog jumps over the wall."

Simple rule: Use 狗急跳墙 when someone who is cornered or in extreme danger does something surprising, risky, or even dangerous — behavior they would never show under normal circumstances. It often carries a note of warning: don't push someone too far, or they may react unpredictably.

Try it yourself: Think of a movie villain who, just when they're about to be caught, makes one last reckless move. Could you describe that moment using 狗急跳墙? What made them "jump the wall"?

Try it yourself. Think of a time you did something you never thought you could — stayed up all night, asked a stranger for help, learned something in a single afternoon — simply because you had to. That feeling is exactly what this idiom captures.

This phrase is your lens for recognizing desperation-driven behavior in stories, history, and everyday life. Ready to go deeper? Pick one real-world story — a historical event, a film, a news headline — and ask yourself: who here just jumped the wall?
