🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 丨

Everyday example. Think about a tall, straight fence post planted firmly in the ground — it stands upright, confident, and unmoving. That single vertical line is exactly what you are looking at in the stroke 丨.

Picture this. In Chinese writing, every character is built from a small set of basic brushstrokes, much like how every house is built from a handful of shapes — walls, roofs, windows. The stroke 丨 (pronounced shù, meaning "vertical") is one of the most fundamental of all. It is a single, clean downward line, drawn from top to bottom in one smooth motion.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Think of it as drawing a $1$ on a piece of paper, but without the little foot or hat — just the bare vertical line itself. Formally, you can think of it as a line segment along the $y$-axis: moving in the direction $(0, -1)$ from start to end.

Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. Notice how the simple act of drawing that single straight line already feels deliberate and intentional — that sense of purpose is exactly the spirit of 丨, the backbone hidden inside countless Chinese characters you will soon recognize.

🌱 丿

🌱 丿

Everyday example. Think of the way water slides off a slanted roof — it flows smoothly from upper-right down to lower-left, then simply lifts away without a hook or pause. That natural, effortless sweep is exactly what the stroke 丿 feels like when you write it.

Picture this. Imagine you are drawing a single blade of grass bending in the wind. You place your pen near the top-right, glide diagonally downward toward the lower-left, and lift your pen off the page at the end. No stopping, no curling, just a clean, confident sweep. That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. Try to keep each stroke smooth and light at the end. Once this feels natural, you have mastered one of the most common building blocks in written Chinese — a small skill with enormous reach.

🍃 十

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

🌱 日

🌱 日

Everyday example: Every morning the sun rises, filling the sky with light and warmth. In English we have one word — "sun" — but we also use the sun to mark time: a day is simply one full journey of the sun across the sky. In Chinese, a single character carries both ideas at once.

Picture this: Look at the character 日 (pronounced rì). It is a neat rectangle with one horizontal line drawn straight across its middle — like a small window showing the round, glowing sun with a bright band of light cutting through its center. Ancient Chinese scribes actually drew a circle with a dot inside to represent the sun; over thousands of years that dot stretched into today's middle stroke, and the circle flattened into a tidy rectangle. Every time you write 日, picture that blazing disc hanging in the sky.

Simple rule: 日 carries two linked meanings: sun and day. These ideas are naturally the same thing — a "day" is exactly the time the sun is visible. Whenever 日 appears inside a larger character or word, it usually brings a sense of light, warmth, or passing time.

Try it yourself: Write 日 five times slowly, stroke by stroke. After drawing the middle horizontal line each time, pause and ask yourself: "What part of the sun is this?" Soon the character will feel less like a foreign symbol and more like a tiny picture of daylight.

🌱 万

Imagine you are at a market and someone says, "This city has ten thousand stalls!" In Chinese, that number has its own single character: 万 (wàn). Learning 万 is like unlocking a superpower for reading prices, populations, and distances in Chinese.

Picture this. You already know how to count to nine, and you know that $10 = 十$, $100 = 百$, $1{,}000 = 千$. Chinese adds one more step on the ladder: $10{,}000 = $ 万. Every time you multiply by ten thousand, 万 steps in.

Simple rule. In Chinese, large numbers are built in groups of four digits rather than three. So where English says "one million" ($1{,}000{,}000 = 10^6$), Chinese says 一百万 — literally "one hundred 万," because $100 \times 10{,}000 = 1{,}000{,}000$. The pattern looks like this:

$$ \underbrace{1}_{\text{一}} \underbrace{0{,}000}_{\text{万}} $$

Read it left to right: yī wàn — "one ten-thousand."

Try it yourself. What Chinese number phrase matches $50{,}000$? Hint: think of it as $5 \times 10{,}000$. The answer is 五万 (wǔ wàn). Now try $30{,}000$ on your own — write the characters and say them aloud. Notice how 万 always anchors the group of four zeros that follow it.

🌱 丶

Think about the last time you signed your name. Somewhere in that signature, you probably left a small dot or flick — almost without thinking. That tiny mark matters. In Chinese writing, a single dot can be the difference between one character and another.

The stroke 丶 (pronounced zhǔ, though most learners just call it "the dot") is one of the simplest building blocks in all of Chinese. Picture an ink brush touching paper at a slight angle and lifting away — leaving behind a small teardrop shape, thicker at the top and tapering to a point. That's 丶.

Here is the simple rule: 丶 is not decorative — it carries meaning or shape in the characters it lives inside. For example, place a 丶 above 冂 and you get 冗; add two dots to 火 and you see them borrowed into characters like 灬 (the "fire feet" you find under 点 or 热). The dot changes the structure every time.

Try it yourself. Take a piece of paper and practice drawing 丶 ten times. Start your brush (or pen) at the top-left, press down gently, then sweep toward the lower-right and lift. Each stroke should look like a tiny raindrop frozen mid-fall. Once this motion feels natural in your hand, you will start spotting 丶 hiding inside dozens of characters you already know — and that recognition is the first small step toward reading Chinese with confidence.

🍃 白

Everyday example. Think of a blank sheet of paper before you write anything on it. In English you might say it is "plain white." In Chinese, that single idea — white, plain, empty — is captured by one elegant character: 白 (bái).

Picture this. The character 白 looks like a tiny candle flame sitting above a short horizontal base. Scholars trace it back to an ancient drawing of the sun just cresting the horizon, flooding the sky with pure white light. Dawn is the moment when darkness is wiped clean and everything becomes visible and clear.

Simple rule. 白 carries two closely related meanings that flow from that image of bright, uncluttered light:

$$ \text{白} \;\longrightarrow\; \begin{cases} \text{white (the colour)} \\ \text{plain / clear / blank} \end{cases} $$

So 白纸 (bái zhǐ) is literally "white paper" — a blank page. And when someone says 说白了 ("speaking 白"), they mean "to put it plainly." Both uses share the same core idea: nothing hidden, nothing added.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 白 and trace it with your finger: a small flame shape on top, a base below. Now ask: where else does whiteness suggest clarity? When you blank out a chalkboard, you make it white — and ready. 白 works the same way. Can you think of one thing in your daily life that is both white and "plain" at the same time?

Picture this: the full character 言 (yán) means "word" or "speech." It looks a little like a mouth with sound waves rising above it. When 言 appears on the left side of a character — squeezed into a tighter space — it transforms into the compact stroke-cluster 讠. Think of it as 言 wearing a smaller outfit to fit comfortably beside other pieces.

The simple rule is this: whenever you see 讠on the left side of a character, the whole character almost certainly has something to do with speaking, language, or communication. This little two-stroke hook is called a radical — a building-block that hints at meaning. Just as the prefix "tele-" signals distance in English words like telephone or television, 讠signals speech in Chinese characters.

Imagine you have saved up your coins for a whole year. One day, you count them and reach one thousand — a number so big it feels like a small fortune. In Chinese, that number has its own character: 千 (qiān), meaning one thousand, or $1{,}000$.

Picture a single vertical stroke standing tall, with a short horizontal bar crossing it near the top — like a person stretching their arms wide to show off something grand. That visual energy matches the meaning: 千 represents a quantity that feels large, impressive, and plentiful.

Here is the simple rule: whenever you see 千 in a Chinese text, it signals the number $1{,}000$ or a multiple of it. Just as English uses the word "thousand," Chinese uses 千. So $3{,}000$ becomes 三千 (sān qiān — literally "three thousand"), and $10{,}000$ is a whole new word (万, wàn), but 千 carries you confidently up to that boundary.

In the idiom 千方百计 (qiān fāng bǎi jì), 千 lends its sense of vastness — "a thousand ways." The idiom describes someone trying every possible approach to solve a problem, as if they have an endless supply of plans.

🍃 方

Everyday example: Think about giving someone directions to your home. You might say "go left, cross two blocks, and it's on the right side." In Chinese, the character 方 (fāng) lives right at the heart of that kind of language — it captures the idea of a side, a direction, or a place.

Picture this: Hold a square tile in your hand. It has four equal sides, and you can rotate it to face any direction. The character 方 echoes that image: a compact, bounded shape with a clear orientation. When you write it, you are sketching the idea of something defined and pointing somewhere.

Simple rule: 方 carries two meanings that spring from the same root idea. First, it means square — a shape where every side is equal and every corner is clean. Second, it means direction or region — north, south, or simply "that side over there." A square has perfectly clear sides pointing in perfectly clear directions, so both meanings live comfortably in one character.

You will find 方 tucked inside many everyday words: 方向 (fāng xiàng) means direction, and 地方 (dì fāng) means place or region. Once you recognise 方, it lights up everywhere.

Try it yourself: Trace the strokes of 方 slowly. Notice the corner-like quality of the shape. Now pick three spots in the room you are in right now — a window, a door, a lamp. Describe each one using a direction word: is it in front of you, behind you, to the left, to the right? You are already thinking in 方.

Think about the last time you tried really hard to solve a problem — maybe you asked a friend, searched online, tried three different approaches, then tried three more. You were using many strategies, one after another.

That is exactly what 百 (bǎi) means: hundred, and by extension, a great many. In Chinese, round numbers carry extra weight. Just as English speakers say "I've told you a thousand times," Chinese speakers use 百 to signal abundance — not literally one hundred, but so many you can hardly count them.

In our idiom 千方百计 (qiān fāng bǎi jì), 百 pairs with 计 (jì), which means plan or strategy. Together, 百计 paints the image of a hundred plans — every trick, every route, every idea available.

Simple rule: When you see 百 in an idiom, think "many, many" — a multiplying word that turns one strategy into an arsenal of strategies.

Have you ever tried to solve a puzzle and kept switching strategies — first trying the corners, then sorting by color, then hunting for edge pieces? Each switch is a new plan of attack. That constant stream of "let me try this instead" is exactly the spirit of 计 (jì).

Picture this: a general stands before a map, finger tracing routes, crossing them out, drawing new ones. Every line she draws is a 计 — a scheme, a calculated move, a thought-through strategy. The character itself carries the idea of counting your steps before you take them: plotting, reckoning, measuring possibilities.

Here is the simple rule: 计 = a deliberate plan, worked out in advance. It is not a random guess. It is the kind of thinking that asks, "If I do X, what happens? And if that fails, what is my next move?" In mathematics, you might recognize this as building a decision tree — mapping out branches before committing to one path.

Inside 千方百计 — our idiom — 百计 means "a hundred schemes." Paired with 千方 ("a thousand methods"), the full phrase paints someone who has exhausted every clever strategy imaginable: trying, adapting, retrying without giving up.

🌸 Phrase 千方百计

🌸 Phrase 千方百计

Everyday example. Imagine a child who desperately wants a puppy. She draws her parents a picture of a sad dog, sets her alarm to prove she can wake up early, promises to do extra chores, and even enlists grandma to plead her case. She is trying every possible method until someone says yes.

Picture this. The phrase 千方百计 (qiān fāng bǎi jì) paints exactly that picture. Break it into pairs: 千方 means a thousand ways (千 = thousand, 方 = way or method), and 百计 means a hundred schemes (百 = hundred, 计 = plan or trick). Together they say: "a thousand ways and a hundred plans" — in other words, every trick in the book.

Simple rule. Use 千方百计 when someone leaves no stone unturned trying to reach a goal. The numbers 千 and 百 are not exact counts; they are a traditional Chinese device for saying "so many it is impossible to count." Think of it the way English speakers say "a million reasons."

Try it yourself. Read these two sentences and decide which fits 千方百计 better:

  • (A) She glanced briefly at the map.
  • (B) She asked locals, retraced her steps, and searched online until she found the lost temple.

Sentence (B) is the match — relentless effort from every angle. When you next describe someone who refuses to give up no matter what, reach for 千方百计.
