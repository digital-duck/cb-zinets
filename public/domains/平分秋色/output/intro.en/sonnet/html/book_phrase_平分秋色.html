🌱 丿

🌱 丿

Everyday example. Think of the way water slides off a slanted roof — it flows smoothly from upper-right down to lower-left, then simply lifts away without a hook or pause. That natural, effortless sweep is exactly what the stroke 丿 feels like when you write it.

Picture this. Imagine you are drawing a single blade of grass bending in the wind. You place your pen near the top-right, glide diagonally downward toward the lower-left, and lift your pen off the page at the end. No stopping, no curling, just a clean, confident sweep. That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. Try to keep each stroke smooth and light at the end. Once this feels natural, you have mastered one of the most common building blocks in written Chinese — a small skill with enormous reach.

Simple rule. 刀 means knife or blade, and it carries the idea of cutting or separating whenever it appears inside a larger character. In 解 (the first half of 解放, meaning to loosen or to free), 刀 acts like a tiny tool: it is the knife that cuts the knot — releasing what was held tight. Without the blade, there is no liberation.

🌱 巴

Have you ever waited so eagerly for something — a birthday, a letter, a friend — that you kept craning your neck, looking, hoping? That feeling of longing and sticking close is exactly what the character 巴 (bā) captures.

Picture this: Imagine a person crouched low, wrapping their arms around something they refuse to let go. Ancient versions of 巴 looked like a coiling snake gripping its prey — something that clings. Over time, this image gave birth to two related meanings: to long for and to stick to.

Simple rule: 巴 pulls double duty in modern Chinese.

  • As a standalone verb, it means to hope or to long for — 巴不得 (bā bu de) means "to wish desperately."
  • As a suffix, it attaches to body-part words to make them feel complete: 尾巴 (wěibā, tail), 嘴巴 (zuǐbā, mouth), 下巴 (xiàbā, chin).

Think of the suffix use like adding "-y" in English — "tail" becomes "taily," something familiar and tangible.

You will also spot 巴 hiding inside bigger characters. Whenever you see 把 (bǎ, handle/grip) or 爸 (bà, dad), 巴 is there in the base, quietly lending its sense of holding close.

Try it yourself: Look at the word 尾巴. Can you feel how 巴 makes the tail feel like something that sticks and dangles? Write 巴 three times and trace the coiling curve — let it remind you of clinging and longing.

The diagonal stroke curving downward represents the heavy seed head drooping to one side, just as real grain does when it is ready to be picked. That is why 禾 lives inside 秒, and why 秒 lives inside our phrase 争分夺秒, "racing against every second."

Everyday example: Think about slicing an orange in half. Before the cut you have one whole fruit. After the cut you have two pieces. That single act of cutting — of separating one thing into parts — is exactly what the character 分 (fēn) expresses. It means to divide or to split.

Picture this: Look at the character 分 itself. At the top you see two strokes leaning away from each other, already pulling apart. Beneath them sits the shape of a knife (刀). A knife pressing down until the two halves separate — the character is a tiny picture of that moment frozen in ink.

Simple rule: Whenever you divide a whole into equal pieces, 分 is at work. In mathematics, if you start with one whole thing $W$ and split it into $n$ equal parts, each part has the value

$$ \frac{W}{n}. $$

When $n = 2$, you get $\frac{W}{2}$ — exactly half. This fraction notation is a direct echo of what 分 means: one amount divided by another.

Try it yourself: Take a strip of paper and fold it once — you now have $\frac{1}{2}$. Fold it again — you have $\frac{1}{4}$. Each fold is a 分. After three folds, what fraction does each piece represent? Write it as $\frac{1}{?}$ and say the division out loud. You just practiced 分 three times in a row.

Everyday example. Picture a table with one short leg. Your cup wobbles, your bowl tips, your homework slides off the edge. Now picture the table fixed — all four legs touching the ground evenly. Nothing wobbles. Nothing tips. That steady, even table is exactly what the Chinese character 平 (píng) means: flat, even, level, calm.

Picture this. The character 平 looks like a balance scale seen from the front — a flat horizontal line resting evenly across the middle, with small symmetrical strokes on either side, like two pans hanging in perfect balance. Nothing leans left or right. That visual balance is the meaning: when two sides match exactly, we say things are 平.

Simple rule. Think of 平 as describing any situation where there is no difference, no bump, no disturbance between two sides or two moments in time:

  • A flat road has no hills: 平 (level).
  • A calm sea has no waves: 平 (calm).
  • A fair trade has no one losing out: 平 (equal, fair).

In the phrase 一路平安 ("yī lù píng ān," a common travel blessing), 平 carries the sense of a journey with no bumps — smooth and uneventful in the best way.

Try it yourself. Next time someone is about to travel, imagine wishing them a road with no potholes and a sea with no waves. That wish — "may everything stay level and calm for you" — is the feeling carried by 平. Can you think of one English word that captures both "flat" and "calm" at once?
