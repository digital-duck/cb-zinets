🌱 ⺁

Imagine standing under a rocky ledge on a hiking trail. Rain starts falling, but you stay dry — the cliff face juts out just enough to shelter you. That jutting overhang is exactly what ⺁ pictures.

This small stroke is called the cliff radical. Draw a horizontal line, then let a short vertical line drop straight down from its left end — like the silhouette of a rocky shelf seen from the side. Simple as it looks, that shape carries a clear visual idea: something that sticks out overhead and creates space beneath it.

The rule is easy to remember: whenever you spot ⺁ at the top of a Chinese character, think shelter, overhang, something protective jutting forward. The stroke acts like an architectural label — it tells you the character involves the idea of a ledge, an enclosure, or a flat roof-like surface. In the word 自相矛盾 (self-contradiction), the character 盾, meaning shield, wears ⺁ at its crown — the overhanging brim of a shield held up to stop a blow. That is one vivid example, but ⺁ shows up in many other characters for the same basic reason.

Try it yourself: Sketch ⺁ three times — one slow horizontal stroke, then a short vertical drop on the left. Now look at the top of 盾. Can you see the cliff ledge sheltering everything beneath it? Once you see it, you will notice it everywhere characters talk about flat overhangs and protective coverings.

🌸 Phrase 自相矛盾

🌸 Phrase 自相矛盾

Imagine a friend tells you, "I never check my phone at dinner" — and then, two minutes later, pulls it out to show you a video. Those two claims knock each other flat. The first says never; the second punches a hole straight through it. When your own words defeat your own words, that is a self-contradiction.

Picture two puzzle pieces that refuse to fit no matter how you turn them. Each looks reasonable on its own, but the moment you press them together, you can see they clash. A self-contradiction works the same way: two statements, both claimed at once, yet impossible to hold at the same time.

The four-character Chinese phrase 自相矛盾 (zì xiāng máo dùn) captures this idea with a sharp image — literally "one's own spear against one's own shield." The phrase points to any situation where a person's own claims undercut each other, the way a perfectly unstoppable spear and a perfectly impenetrable shield cannot both exist in the same world.

The simple rule: a self-contradiction appears whenever a statement and its direct opposite are both asserted at once. One must be false — they cannot coexist.

Try it yourself: read these two sentences — "The jar is completely empty" and "There is one coin inside the jar." Do they contradict each other? Yes. Completely empty and one coin inside cannot both be true at the same moment. Catching that kind of clash is the heart of clear thinking.

Picture a merchant at a market shouting two claims in the same breath: "My spear pierces every shield!" and "My shield blocks every spear!" A child in the crowd raises a hand: "What happens if your spear hits your own shield?" The merchant falls silent. His own words have attacked him. That is 自相矛盾 — a contradiction that comes from inside, where your left hand fights your right.

Here is the simple rule: whenever a single claim secretly destroys itself, you have found a 自相矛盾. It does not need a second person to point out the flaw; the flaw was always there, waiting.

This phrase is a natural endpoint because it is the ultimate test of everything you have built. Every character you studied asks: does this idea hold together? 自相矛盾 is the name for when the answer is no.

Try it yourself: think of a rule someone in your life states often. Ask whether that rule, taken seriously, contradicts something else they insist on. If it does, you have just spotted a 自相矛盾 in the wild — and named it in Chinese.
