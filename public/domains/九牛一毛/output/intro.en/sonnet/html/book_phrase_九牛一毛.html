Simple rule: In modern Chinese, 乙 is the second position in the classical counting sequence 甲、乙、丙、丁 — the equivalent of A, B, C, D. Beyond counting, 乙 also carries a quiet character of its own: not a bold straight line, but a resilient bend that turns, adjusts, and keeps going. Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Simple rule: 牛 means cow, ox, or bull — any bovine animal. It is pronounced niú (second tone, like a rising question). It also carries the sense of strength and hard work. Interestingly, in modern slang 牛 means "awesome" or "impressive," because an ox is so powerful.

As a building block, 牛 appears inside other characters related to animals, agriculture, or things historically connected to cattle. Spotting it inside a complex character gives you an instant clue about its meaning.
