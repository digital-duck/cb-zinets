The simple rule for practicing 止 is the Three-Second Rule. When you feel that surge of emotion, physically or mentally count three seconds. This brief pause is enough time for your brain to shift from the emotional center (the reactive part) to the logical center (the thoughtful part).

Try it yourself today: The next time you feel a strong urge—to complain, to criticize, or to send an immediate text—take one deep breath and count silently to three. This exercise trains your mind that you are in control of the speed, not the emotion.

Imagine you are pointing at a fluffy cat and announcing, "It is a cat." The word 是 (shì) is one of the most fundamental building blocks in Chinese; it is the verbal glue that connects two ideas. It means "to be" or indicates identification. This function makes it what we call a copula—a gentle link that tells us that the subject and the description belong together.

Picture this concept like an equals sign (=). When you say, "I am happy," the word shì is doing the job of the equal sign. It takes the person (the subject) and links them directly to their state or identity (the predicate). It’s not just saying two things next to each other; it’s making a firm statement that one thing is the other.

The simple rule for using 是 is: Subject + 是 + Predicate. The Subject tells us who we are talking about, and the Predicate tells us what they are or what state they exist in. For example, if your name is Alex, you would say: Alex $\text{是}$ student (Alex is a student).

Now, try it yourself! If I tell you that the sky is blue, which word acts as the glue linking "sky" and "blue"? It must be 是. This simple particle allows us to make clear statements of fact about who or what something fundamentally is.
