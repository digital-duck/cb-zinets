Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Simple rule: 日 carries two linked meanings: sun and day. These ideas are naturally the same thing — a "day" is exactly the time the sun is visible. Whenever 日 appears inside a larger character or word, it usually brings a sense of light, warmth, or passing time.

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. That little square is the character 口 (kǒu). It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing: a picture that became a letter.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Have you ever felt your chest tighten with excitement before something important — a race, a test, a first day somewhere new? That flutter you feel is what ancient Chinese thinkers were pointing to when they carved the character 心 (xīn).

Picture this. The character 心 is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing. Look at it closely: it resembles a heart cradled between two curving arms, with three small strokes representing the beating chambers. When ancient scribes drew it, they meant the physical heart — but they also meant something deeper: the seat of thought, feeling, and intention all at once.

Simple rule. In Chinese, 心 means both heart and mind. Unlike English, which separates "I feel" (heart) from "I think" (mind), Chinese tradition treats them as one organ. To think carefully is already an act of the heart. To feel genuinely is already a kind of knowing. This is why Confucius warns us: studying without 心 — without turning ideas over in your whole self — leaves you lost (罔). But pure inner rumination without study leaves you adrift (殆). 心 is the meeting point.

Try it yourself. Say the word xīn aloud and place one hand on your chest. Notice you are touching the very thing the character depicts. Now ask: when you read something today, did your 心 engage — did you feel curious, not just move your eyes across the page?

Everyday example. Imagine a friend asks, "Did you enjoy the movie?" You smile and say, "Yes, it was great!" — but inside you're thinking, That was two hours of my life I'll never get back. Your mouth said yes; your heart said no. That gap is exactly what the idiom 口是心非 captures, and the character at its center is 是 (shì).

Picture this. 是 is the Chinese word for yes, correct, or the linking verb to be — like the equals sign in an equation. When you say 这是书 (zhè shì shū, "this is a book"), 是 acts like $=$, connecting "this" to "book" and confirming it is true. It is the character that stamps two things as matching.

Simple rule. Whenever 是 appears, read it as a signal of agreement or identity:

$$ \text{subject} \xrightarrow{\text{是}} \text{what it is confirmed to be} $$

In 口是心非, the mouth uses 是 to declare agreement out loud — even when the feeling inside points the other way.

Try it yourself. Look at these two mini-sentences and decide what 是 is linking in each:

  1. 他是老师。(Tā shì lǎoshī. — "He is a teacher.")
  2. 这是对的。(Zhè shì duì de. — "This is correct.")

Notice how 是 always bridges two ideas and stamps them as matching. Once you spot that pattern, you'll see 是 everywhere — and you'll understand why a 是 spoken aloud while the inner feeling disagrees is such a vivid image of dishonesty.

Simple rule. 非 carries two closely related meanings that work together. First, it means "not" or "non-" — a flat denial. Second, it means something is wrong or contrary to what it should be.
