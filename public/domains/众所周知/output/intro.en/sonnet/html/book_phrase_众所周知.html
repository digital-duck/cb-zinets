🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 丨

Everyday example. Think about a tall, straight fence post planted firmly in the ground — it stands upright, confident, and unmoving. That single vertical line is exactly what you are looking at in the stroke 丨.

Picture this. In Chinese writing, every character is built from a small set of basic brushstrokes, much like how every house is built from a handful of shapes — walls, roofs, windows. The stroke 丨 (pronounced shù, meaning "vertical") is one of the most fundamental of all. It is a single, clean downward line, drawn from top to bottom in one smooth motion.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Think of it as drawing a $1$ on a piece of paper, but without the little foot or hat — just the bare vertical line itself. Formally, you can think of it as a line segment along the $y$-axis: moving in the direction $(0, -1)$ from start to end.

Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. 🌱 大

🌱 大

Have you ever spread your arms as wide as possible to show a friend just how big something was? Maybe you caught a fish and wanted to say, "It was this big!" That natural gesture is exactly what the character 大 captures.

Picture this. Imagine a person standing upright with both arms stretched out to the sides — a human figure declaring size with their whole body. That is the origin of 大 (pronounced dà). Ancient Chinese scribes drew a stick figure with outstretched arms, and over thousands of years it simplified into the three-stroke shape you see today: one horizontal stroke crossing a central vertical stroke, with a left-leaning leg completing the figure.

Simple rule. 大 means big, large, or great. It is one of the most common characters in the language because size matters everywhere — big cities (大城市), big people (大人, meaning adult), big ideas. When you see 大 inside a more complex character or word, it almost always brings a sense of magnitude or importance.

Try it yourself. Write 大 three times, saying dà each time. Then think of one thing in your life that feels truly dà — your school, your family, the sky. Notice how the character's outstretched arms seem to hold that bigness. Once you feel that image, you will never forget this character.

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

🌱 口

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. That little square is the character 口 (kǒu). It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing: a picture that became a letter.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

🍃 天

🍃 天

Have you ever stepped outside on a clear morning and looked straight up? That vast, open space above your head — in Chinese, that is called 天 (tiān).

Picture this: Imagine a person standing tall with arms stretched wide — that is the character 大, meaning "big" or "a person." Now draw a single horizontal stroke above the person's head. Where is that stroke pointing? Up — toward everything above us. That stroke is exactly what turns 大 into 天. The character is literally showing you a person looking up at what lies overhead.

Simple rule: 天 carries three closely related meanings that all grow from the same idea:

  1. Sky — the blue expanse above (今天的天很蓝, The sky today is very blue)
  2. Heaven or the divine — in older texts, 天 referred to a higher power, the way "the heavens" sounds in English
  3. Day — one sweep of the sky from sunrise to sunset counts as one 天 (三天 = three days)

So one small character holds the sky, the divine, and the passage of time — all because of that single stroke above a person's head.

Try it yourself: Hold a pencil and write 大 first. Then add one short horizontal line above it, near the top. You have just written 天. Say tiān aloud three times. Now think of one sentence: "Today is a beautiful ___." In Chinese, that blank is 天.

🌱 人

Everyday example: Think about the word "person." Every language has one — it's one of the first words children learn, because people are everywhere in our world.

Picture this: The Chinese character 人 (rén) is a small picture of a human being. It is one of the simplest and oldest characters in the Chinese writing system, and it carries the meaning person or people.

Simple rule: 人 is a pictograph — a character that started as a drawing of the thing it represents. Over thousands of years, the original stick-figure drawing was simplified into just two brushstrokes, but the idea stayed the same: a human being, standing and walking through the world.

You will see 人 hiding inside hundreds of other characters, quietly adding the meaning "person" or "human" to the bigger idea. For example, 大 (dà, big or great) shows a person stretching their arms wide. Recognizing 人 inside other characters is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Picture this: Long ago, Chinese writing used simple pictures to represent real objects. 戶 began as a sketch of one half of a door — a single panel attached to a frame, swinging on its own. Simple rule: When you see 戶 in a character, think "household" or "single door." Over time, 戶 grew beyond just meaning "door" — it came to stand for a family unit or home, since every household had its own door. You'll see this idea reused in words like 戶口 (hùkǒu, household registration) and 一戶人家 (yī hù rén jiā, "one household/family").

🍃 众

Everyday example: Imagine you're at a busy market. One person shopping alone is just "a person," but when a big crowd gathers around a street performer, you suddenly have "a crowd" — many people together. Chinese has a single character that captures this exact idea: 众 (zhòng), meaning "many people" or "the masses."

Picture this: Look at the shape of 众. It's built from three copies of the character 人 (rén), meaning "person," stacked together — one on top, two below. Three people standing side by side visually become a crowd. The character isn't just telling you the meaning with a definition; it's showing you a picture of people grouped together. This is a wonderful example of how some Chinese characters work like little drawings of the idea they represent.

Simple rule: When you see multiple identical pieces repeated inside a character, think "more of this thing" or "many of this thing." Here: $人 + 人 + 人 = 众$ — one person repeated three times signals "many people" or "the public." You'll see 众 in everyday words like 众人 (zhòngrén, "everyone, the crowd") and 公众 (gōngzhòng, "the public").

Try it yourself: Next time you see 众, picture three people standing together. Ask yourself: does this word feel like it's about a group, a crowd, or "everyone" rather than just one individual? If yes, you've understood the heart of 众.

Everyday example: Think about your weekly routine. You go to school or work Monday through Friday, then rest on Saturday and Sunday. That whole cycle — seven days, repeating again and again — is what the Chinese character 周 (zhōu) is built around. It means "week" or "cycle," something that goes around and comes back to where it started.

Picture this: Imagine a clock with seven days marked on its face instead of twelve hours. Each day you move forward, and after the seventh day, the hand swings back to day one. 周 captures that loop — not a straight line moving forward forever, but a circle that returns. This is why 周 also appears in words like 周围 (zhōuwéi, "surrounding," the area that wraps around something) and 周年 (zhōunián, "anniversary," the day that comes back around every year).

Simple rule: Whenever you see 周, ask yourself: "What is going around and coming back?" If the answer is time, it's likely a week or a cycle. If the answer is space, it's likely "surrounding" or "all around."

🍃 所

Everyday example: Imagine you're texting a friend: "The restaurant where we ate was amazing." That little phrase "where we ate" turns an action (eating) into a place or thing being talked about. In Chinese, the character 所 (suǒ) does something similar — it's a small grammatical word that transforms a verb into a noun-like concept, almost like adding "the place/thing that..." in English.

Picture this: 所 originally meant "a place" — picture a physical location, like a station or institute (you'll see it in words like 场所, meaning "venue"). Over time, this idea of "a place where something happens" stretched into a grammatical role: 所 attaches in front of a verb to mean "that which is [verb]ed." For example, 所知 literally means "that which is known," and 所见 means "that which is seen." It's like 所 builds an invisible signpost saying, "Here's the thing resulting from this action."

Simple rule: Think of 所 as a converter: $\text{所} + \text{verb} \rightarrow \text{noun phrase}$. It often pairs with another word, like 是, to form structures meaning "what is being [verb]ed is..." This is exactly how it appears in 众所周知 ("known to all") — 所 turns 周知 ("widely known") into a description applying to "what everyone knows."

Try it yourself: Next time you see 所 in a sentence, pause and ask: "What action is happening right after this character, and what thing does it create?" That's 所 doing its quiet, transformative work.
