Simple rule: “万” (wàn) always means ten thousand. It's a shortcut for a really big number. We use it to express a general idea of something being very plentiful or extensive. Think of it as a convenient way to say “a lot.”

Try it yourself: Let’s say you want to describe the amount of rain that fell last week. You could say it was “万分” (wàn fēn) – meaning "ten thousandths" of an inch. Let’s explore a unique stroke in Chinese calligraphy – the 攵. It looks like a small arrow pointing downwards, as if you're pushing something away gently. Think of it this way: imagine cleaning up a messy table with a cloth. You use a quick wipe, right? The 攵 is similar, but shorter and more energetic.

Picture this: you’re a farmer gathering crops in autumn (秋收 – qiū shōu – “autumn harvest”). The basic rule is: the 攵 always moves from the top of the line down to the bottom. It represents a direction – downward movement – and it's meant to feel energetic. Don't curve or swirl it; just draw it straight down.

Try it yourself! Grab a piece of paper and a pen or pencil. Draw the 攵 stroke several times, focusing on moving your hand downwards clearly. It’s okay if it doesn’t look perfect at first – just practice getting a feel for this simple but strong symbol.

🌱 牛

Let’s talk about “牛” (niú), which literally translates to “cow.” Now, you might be picturing a gentle animal grazing in a field – and that's exactly what a cow is! But "牛" in this context represents something much bigger: it’s a fundamental unit of measurement used in Chinese traditional currency.

Picture this: Before China adopted the modern Renminbi (RMB) as its main currency, they used a system called “หยวน” (yuán), meaning ‘weight.’ Each yuan was divided into smaller units, and one of those units was the ‘牛’. Think of it like this – if you were buying something and needed to pay 10 牛, that meant you owed 10 of these basic measuring units.

The simple rule is: a “牛” is equal to 40 wei (魏). “Wei” itself means 'weight,' and was a standard unit used for weighing grains like rice or wheat. So, one 牛 represented 40 times that weight. It was directly linked to the amount of grain you could actually hold in your hands.

Try it yourself: Imagine you’re buying some rice. If someone tells you you owe them 3 牛, how much rice are you effectively paying for? That's 3 multiplied by 40 wei, or 120 wei of rice – a significant amount! Understanding “牛” gives you a glimpse into the ancient Chinese system of trade and measurement.
