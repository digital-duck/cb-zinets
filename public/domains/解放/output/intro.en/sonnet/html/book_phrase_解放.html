🌱 万

🌱 万

Imagine you are at a market and someone says, "This city has ten thousand stalls!" In Chinese, that number has its own single character: 万 (wàn). Learning 万 is like unlocking a superpower for reading prices, populations, and distances in Chinese.

Picture this. You already know how to count to nine, and you know that $10 = 十$, $100 = 百$, $1{,}000 = 千$. Chinese adds one more step on the ladder: $10{,}000 = $ 万. Every time you multiply by ten thousand, 万 steps in.

Simple rule. In Chinese, large numbers are built in groups of four digits rather than three. So where English says "one million" ($1{,}000{,}000 = 10^6$), Chinese says 一百万 — literally "one hundred 万," because $100 \times 10{,}000 = 1{,}000{,}000$. The pattern looks like this:

$$ \underbrace{1}_{\text{一}} \underbrace{0{,}000}_{\text{万}} $$

Read it left to right: yī wàn — "one ten-thousand."

Try it yourself. What Chinese number phrase matches $50{,}000$? Hint: think of it as $5 \times 10{,}000$. The answer is 五万 (wǔ wàn). Now try $30{,}000$ on your own — write the characters and say them aloud. Notice how 万 always anchors the group of four zeros that follow it.

🌱 丶

🌱 丶

Think about the last time you signed your name. Somewhere in that signature, you probably left a small dot or flick — almost without thinking. That tiny mark matters. In Chinese writing, a single dot can be the difference between one character and another.

The stroke 丶 (pronounced zhǔ, though most learners just call it "the dot") is one of the simplest building blocks in all of Chinese. Picture an ink brush touching paper at a slight angle and lifting away — leaving behind a small teardrop shape, thicker at the top and tapering to a point. That's 丶.

Here is the simple rule: 丶 is not decorative — it carries meaning or shape in the characters it lives inside. For example, place a 丶 above 冂 and you get 冗; add two dots to 火 and you see them borrowed into characters like 灬 (the "fire feet" you find under 点 or 热). The dot changes the structure every time.

Try it yourself. Take a piece of paper and practice drawing 丶 ten times. Start your brush (or pen) at the top-left, press down gently, then sweep toward the lower-right and lift. Each stroke should look like a tiny raindrop frozen mid-fall. Once this motion feels natural in your hand, you will start spotting 丶 hiding inside dozens of characters you already know — and that recognition is the first small step toward reading Chinese with confidence.

🌱 刀

🌱 刀

Everyday example. Think of a chef slicing a tangerine. The knife separates the peel from the fruit cleanly, in one smooth motion — freeing something that was bound together.

Picture this. The character 刀 looks exactly like what it means. Draw a gentle curve leaning to the left — that is the blade. Add a short hook at the bottom right — that is the spine and tip. Ancient scribes looked at a real knife and traced its silhouette onto oracle bones thousands of years ago. What you see today is that same picture, sharpened into brushstrokes.

$$ \text{刀} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{blade (curved left)} + \text{spine (hook right)} $$

Simple rule. 刀 means knife or blade, and it carries the idea of cutting or separating whenever it appears inside a larger character. In 解 (the first half of 解放, meaning to loosen or to free), 刀 acts like a tiny tool: it is the knife that cuts the knot — releasing what was held tight. Without the blade, there is no liberation.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 解 and see if you can spot 刀 hiding inside it. It does not sit alone — it is tucked among other strokes that together paint a scene of something being taken apart piece by piece. Your job right now is just to find the blade. Once you can pick out 刀 on sight, you have found the cutting heart of 解放 — the tool that makes freedom possible.

🌱 攵

🌱 攵

Imagine a teacher tapping a student's shoulder to get their attention, or a parent gently patting a child's hand to guide them. That small, deliberate action — a controlled tap or strike — is exactly what the component 攵 (pronounced roughly like pū) captures.

Picture this: 攵 looks like a hand holding a short stick, poised to give a gentle knock. In the ancient pictograph, you can almost see an arm swinging downward in a purposeful, light motion. It is not a violent blow; it is a directed, intentional touch.

Simple rule: Whenever you spot 攵 on the right side of a character, think guided action — someone is doing something with purpose and force. 攵 acts like a signal that says, "a hand is involved, and something is being set in motion." In the character 放 (fàng, to release or set free), 攵 combines with 方 (a direction marker) to suggest directing something outward — nudging it loose, letting it go. That is the heart of 解放: first 解 unties the knot, then 放 (with its 攵) pushes the thing free.

Try it yourself: Look at the character 改 (gǎi, to change) and 收 (shōu, to collect). Can you spot 攵 in each one? Notice how both involve a deliberate human action — correcting something, or gathering something in. 攵 is the silent reminder that a hand made this happen.

🍃 方

🍃 方

Everyday example: Think about giving someone directions to your home. You might say "go left, cross two blocks, and it's on the right side." In Chinese, the character 方 (fāng) lives right at the heart of that kind of language — it captures the idea of a side, a direction, or a place.

Picture this: Hold a square tile in your hand. It has four equal sides, and you can rotate it to face any direction. The character 方 echoes that image: a compact, bounded shape with a clear orientation. When you write it, you are sketching the idea of something defined and pointing somewhere.

Simple rule: 方 carries two meanings that spring from the same root idea. First, it means square — a shape where every side is equal and every corner is clean. Second, it means direction or region — north, south, or simply "that side over there." A square has perfectly clear sides pointing in perfectly clear directions, so both meanings live comfortably in one character.

You will find 方 tucked inside many everyday words: 方向 (fāng xiàng) means direction, and 地方 (dì fāng) means place or region. Once you recognise 方, it lights up everywhere.

Try it yourself: Trace the strokes of 方 slowly. Notice the corner-like quality of the shape. Now pick three spots in the room you are in right now — a window, a door, a lamp. Describe each one using a direction word: is it in front of you, behind you, to the left, to the right? You are already thinking in 方.

🌱 牛

🌱 牛

Everyday example: Think about a farm. The hardest-working animal pulling a plow through a muddy field has traditionally been the ox — powerful, patient, and essential to survival. In Chinese culture, the ox holds that same honored place.

Picture this: The character 牛 (niú) is actually a small drawing. Look at it closely: the horizontal strokes suggest the ox's wide horns spreading left and right, and the vertical stroke below is its body. Ancient versions of this character look even more like a front-facing cow's head. Chinese writing began as pictures of the world, and 牛 is one of the clearest examples.

Simple rule: 牛 means cow, ox, or bull — any bovine animal. It is pronounced niú (second tone, like a rising question). It also carries the sense of strength and hard work. Interestingly, in modern slang 牛 means "awesome" or "impressive," because an ox is so powerful.

As a building block, 牛 appears inside other characters related to animals, agriculture, or things historically connected to cattle. Spotting it inside a complex character gives you an instant clue about its meaning.

Try it yourself: Trace the character slowly — one horizontal stroke for the horns, a shorter horizontal stroke below, then one long vertical stroke through the middle. Say niú as you write it. Can you picture the ox standing there, ready for a day's work?

🌱 角

🌱 角

Imagine a bull standing in a field, head lowered, two sharp horns pointing outward. Those horns are the original image behind the character 角 (jiǎo). When ancient Chinese scribes first drew this character, they sketched exactly that — a horn curving to a point.

Picture this: Hold your two index fingers up, tips touching, to form a pointed shape like the letter V flipped upside down. That pointed meeting-place is what 角 captures. Over centuries the brushstroke was simplified, but the idea of something that comes to a point stayed at its heart.

Simple rule: 角 carries two related meanings that grow from the same root image. First, it means horn — the bony point on an animal's head. Second, because a horn tapers to a sharp corner, 角 also means corner or angle — the pointy place where two lines or walls meet. In geometry, when two lines meet and form a pointed gap between them, that gap is called an angle, written $\angle$, and 角 is the exact word Chinese uses for it.

A right angle — where two lines meet perfectly straight, like the corner of a page — is $90°$, and in Chinese it is 直角 (zhí jiǎo): straight + angle.

Try it yourself: Look around the room. Find three corners — a table edge, a door frame, a book cover. Each one is a 角. Can you spot which looks closest to $90°$?

🍃 放

Everyday example. Think about opening a birdcage. You unlatch the door, step back, and let the bird fly free. You did not push it out — you simply stopped holding it. That action of releasing, of letting something go, is exactly what 放 (fàng) captures.

Picture this. The character 放 combines two parts. On the left is 方, which gives a sense of direction and shape — imagine a boundary, a square pen where something is kept. On the right is 攵, a small hand holding a stick, suggesting an action or effort. Together they paint the image of someone deliberately acting to open a boundary and let what is inside move freely. The energy flows outward.

Simple rule. 放 means to release or let go — to stop constraining something so it can move or act on its own. You will find it in words like 放松 (fàngsōng, relax — literally "release tension"), 放手 (fàngshǒu, let go — literally "release the hand"), and, in our target word, 解放 (jiěfàng, liberation — where 解 means "to untie" and 放 completes the idea of setting free).

Try it yourself. When you arrive home and kick off your shoes, you are 放松-ing. When a parent watches a child ride a bike alone for the first time, they 放手. Look for 放 today — notice how every use involves removing a hold on something.

🍃 解

Everyday example. Imagine you've just received a gift wrapped in string so tightly knotted that you have to work each loop apart, one by one, until the cord finally falls away and the box is free. That moment of loosening — of making something that was bound suddenly come apart — is exactly what the character 解 captures.

Picture this. The ancient form of 解 (jiě) is built around 刀, a knife — picture a short, curved blade. The full character shows that knife being used to separate an animal's horn from the body, skillfully taking apart something that was once joined. You don't need to know the other pieces yet; what matters is the knife at the center of the action. Over time, the meaning broadened from "cut apart" to any act of undoing, explaining, or resolving.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 解, think untangle. It can mean untying a knot (解开, jiě kāi), solving a problem (解题, jiě tí), or explaining an idea (解释, jiě shì). In every case something complex or bound is being taken apart so it can be understood or freed.

Try it yourself. Look at the word 解放 (jiěfàng), which means liberation. Can you see 解 doing its work? The first character undoes whatever holds something back; the second character, 放, means to release. Together they describe a complete freeing. Next time you meet a tangled problem, picture 解 in action — knife in hand, carefully working each piece loose.

🌸 Phrase 解放

Have you ever kicked off your shoes after a long day and felt instant relief? That sudden "ahh" moment — the weight lifting, the freedom returning — is exactly what 解放 (jiěfàng) captures.

Picture this. Imagine a bird in a cage. Someone unlocks the door and swings it open. The bird steps out and flies. That act — unlocking and releasing — is 解放. It is not just removing a lock; it is actively setting something free.

Simple rule. 解放 is built from two characters that work as a team:

  • 解 (jiě) means to untie or to undo — like loosening a knot

Put them together: undo the binding, then let go → liberation. In Chinese history the word carries great weight — 解放 often refers to emancipation from oppression. In everyday speech it can be lighter: finishing a difficult exam can feel like a small 解放!
