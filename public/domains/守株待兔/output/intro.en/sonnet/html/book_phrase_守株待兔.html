🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 丨

Everyday example. Think about a tall, straight fence post planted firmly in the ground — it stands upright, confident, and unmoving. That single vertical line is exactly what you are looking at in the stroke 丨.

Picture this. In Chinese writing, every character is built from a small set of basic brushstrokes, much like how every house is built from a handful of shapes — walls, roofs, windows. The stroke 丨 (pronounced shù, meaning "vertical") is one of the most fundamental of all. It is a single, clean downward line, drawn from top to bottom in one smooth motion.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Think of it as drawing a $1$ on a piece of paper, but without the little foot or hat — just the bare vertical line itself. Formally, you can think of it as a line segment along the $y$-axis: moving in the direction $(0, -1)$ from start to end.

Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. Notice how the simple act of drawing that single straight line already feels deliberate and intentional — that sense of purpose is exactly the spirit of 丨, the backbone hidden inside countless Chinese characters you will soon recognize.

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

🍃 寸

Everyday example. Have you ever measured something with just your thumb? Before rulers existed, people everywhere used body parts to gauge short distances. A thumb-width was close enough for everyday tasks — and that's exactly where the Chinese character 寸 (cùn) comes from.

Picture this. The character 寸 looks like a small cross with a dot: 寸. Imagine a hand with one finger pointing downward (the vertical stroke), a horizontal line across the wrist, and a tiny dot marking the pulse point just below the wrist. Ancient Chinese healers located this pulse spot to check a patient's health — and that spot sits almost exactly one cùn from the palm crease. The character literally draws the measurement on a hand.

Simple rule. 寸 is a traditional Chinese unit of length equal to roughly $3.33\ \text{cm}$, or about $\frac{1}{10}$ of a 尺 (chǐ, a "Chinese foot"). As an equation:

$$ 1\ 尺 = 10\ 寸 \approx 33.3\ \text{cm} $$

So one 寸 is a little longer than an inch. Today you will find 寸 appearing in poetic phrases and in the names of acupuncture points far more often than on a hardware store ruler.

Try it yourself. Hold up your thumb. The width across the knuckle is roughly one 寸. Now look at the character 寸 again — can you spot the wrist, the hand, and the measuring dot? Once you see it, you never forget it.

🌱 丿

🌱 丿

Everyday example. Think of the way water slides off a slanted roof — it flows smoothly from upper-right down to lower-left, then simply lifts away without a hook or pause. That natural, effortless sweep is exactly what the stroke 丿 feels like when you write it.

Picture this. Imagine you are drawing a single blade of grass bending in the wind. You place your pen near the top-right, glide diagonally downward toward the lower-left, and lift your pen off the page at the end. No stopping, no curling, just a clean, confident sweep. That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. Try to keep each stroke smooth and light at the end. Once this feels natural, you have mastered one of the most common building blocks in written Chinese — a small skill with enormous reach.

🍃 土

Everyday example. Think about planting a garden. Before any seed can grow, you need good soil beneath your feet — the ground that holds everything together and makes growth possible. Without it, there is nowhere to begin.

Picture this. In ancient China, an army rode on horseback across vast open land. The moment a general's horse set hoof on new earth — 土 (tǔ) — it signaled that territory had been claimed. The ground itself was the prize. 土 is drawn to look like exactly that: a cross shape with a flat line at the bottom, like a small plant rooted into the soil, or a boundary marker driven into the ground.

Simple rule. 土 means earth, soil, or ground — the physical stuff under your feet and the land you stand on. It is one of the five classical elements in Chinese thought (alongside water, fire, wood, and metal). Whenever you see 土 inside another character, there is usually a connection to the ground, materials, or something solid and foundational.

In 马到成功 — "success the moment the horse arrives" — the horse does not gallop through thin air. It charges across 土, across real ground, claiming real territory. The earth beneath is what makes the victory solid.

Try it yourself. Trace 土 slowly: one horizontal stroke, one vertical stroke, then a wider horizontal stroke at the base. Say tǔ aloud. What three things in your room are made from or rest on earth?

Everyday example. Think of a tree in your backyard — a solid trunk rising from the ground, with roots gripping the earth below and branches reaching upward. Now imagine that tree after it has been cut down, leaving only a stubby stump behind. That image is exactly where our story begins.

Picture this. The character 木 (mù) is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing. A pictograph is a symbol that looks like the thing it represents. Draw a vertical line for the trunk, a short horizontal stroke across the middle for the branches, and two small strokes angling downward at the base for the roots. You are looking at a tree, simplified into six strokes.

Simple rule. 木 means tree or wood — the material a tree is made of. When you see 木 inside another character, it almost always signals something made of wood or connected to trees. In our idiom 守株待兔, the character 株 (zhū) contains 木 at its left side. That tells you right away: 株 is a wooden thing — specifically, a tree stump, the stubborn leftover after a trunk is felled.

Picture this: Draw a small tent or a house rooftop — a peak in the middle, with two sloping sides coming down. That shape is 宀. Ancient Chinese scribes drew it to mean "a roof" or "a covering from above."

Simple rule: Whenever you see 宀 sitting at the top of a character, think shelter, home, or something happening under a roof. For example:

  • 安 (ān, "peaceful") = 宀 + 女 (woman under a roof → safe and at rest)

In the story 亡羊补牢, the sheep owner eventually rebuilds the pen — a new enclosure, a new shelter. The wisdom is the same: a roof matters. Without protection, what we value can slip away.

Imagine you are hiking through a forest after a storm. Along the path, you spot a thick, round stump — all that remains of a tree that has fallen. In Chinese, that stump (or the base trunk of a standing tree) is called 株 (zhū).

Picture this: A farmer in ancient China is working his field. Suddenly, a rabbit dashes out of the bushes, crashes into a tree stump, and — thud — it falls over, stunned. The farmer picks it up for free! Easy dinner.

Here is the key idea: 株 refers to the rooted base of a tree — the solid, fixed part that stays in one place even after the tree itself is gone. It is not the branch, not the leaf, not the bark. It is specifically the stump anchored to the ground.

Simple rule: Whenever you see 株, think rooted and stationary — a tree base that does not move. In our idiom 守株待兔, this character sets the scene: the farmer goes back to that same fixed stump every single day, hoping lightning strikes twice.

Try it yourself: Look around your home. Can you find something that is heavy, rooted, and never moves from its spot — like a table leg or a door frame? That fixed, immovable quality is the spirit of 株.

🌸 Phrase 守株待兔

🌸 Phrase 守株待兔

Everyday example: Imagine a farmer who once found a rabbit that had accidentally run into a tree stump and knocked itself out. He picked up the rabbit without any effort and had a delicious meal. The next day, instead of tending his fields, he sat beside the same stump all day, waiting for another rabbit to appear. And the next day. And the day after that. His crops withered, and no rabbit ever came.

Picture this: Think of someone who wins the lottery once and then quits their job, spending every day buying tickets and waiting to win again — convinced that lightning will strike twice in the same spot.

Simple rule: 守株待兔 (shǒu zhū dài tù) literally means "guard the stump, wait for the rabbit." It describes the mistake of expecting a lucky accident to repeat itself, or clinging to an old method that worked once instead of adapting to new circumstances. In life and learning, we call this kind of rigid thinking a fixed mindset — the opposite of being flexible and open to change.

Try it yourself: Can you think of a moment when you (or someone you know) kept using an old strategy hoping it would work again, even when the situation had clearly changed? That feeling of "but it worked before!" is exactly the spirit of 守株待兔. Next time you catch yourself thinking that way, ask: Is the stump still there — or has the world moved on?

The simple rule the phrase teaches: past luck is not a plan. Conditions change. What worked once — or what seemed to work — rarely works again by pure waiting.
