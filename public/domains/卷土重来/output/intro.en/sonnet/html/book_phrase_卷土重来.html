🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. Imagine you are keeping score in a game. Your friend scores one point, then another. You want to write that down quickly — just two marks on a piece of paper: one line, then another line on top. That instinct is exactly how the character 二 was born.

Picture this: Hold out two fingers side by side. Now lay them flat and trace each one as a horizontal stroke. The top stroke represents the first unit; the bottom stroke represents the second. Together they form 二 — the Chinese character for two.

$$ 二 = \text{two horizontal strokes} = 2 $$

Simple rule: 二 (èr) means the number two. It is one of the most ancient and recognizable characters in Chinese because it draws what it counts. When you see 二, read it as $2$. This visual logic — letting the shape mirror the quantity — makes early numbers in Chinese unusually easy to remember. The character is honest and direct: you see exactly as many strokes as the number it names.

That same honest simplicity echoes through 一举两得 (yī jǔ liǎng dé), the phrase we are exploring. It is a story about getting two things from one effort — and 二 gives you the clearest picture of what two really means before the phrase adds any layers on top.

Try it yourself: Draw 二 by writing a shorter stroke on top and a longer stroke below. Say èr aloud. Now hold up two fingers. Notice how the character, the sound, and your hand all point to the same simple fact: $2$.

🌸 Phrase 卷土重来

Everyday example. Imagine a sports team that loses the championship in the final minute. Instead of giving up, they train harder all winter and return the next season — fiercer, more focused, and ready to win. That spirit of bouncing back from defeat has a perfect four-character phrase in Chinese: 卷土重来 (juǎn tǔ chóng lái).

Picture this. The image inside the phrase is vivid. Picture an ancient cavalry charge: horses pound the dirt road so hard that clouds of dust roll into the sky. Now imagine those same horses turning around after a defeat and charging back — dust swirling again. 卷 means to roll or curl, 土 means earth or dust, 重 means again, and 来 means to come. Put it together: rolling dust comes again — a defeated force storming back.

Simple rule. Use 卷土重来 whenever someone who has suffered a setback returns with renewed energy and determination. It always implies a comeback after failure, not just a second attempt at something easy.

他失业后努力学习，终于卷土重来，创办了自己的公司。 After losing his job, he studied hard and finally made a comeback, founding his own company.
