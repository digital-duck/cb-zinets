🌱 丿

Everyday example. Think of the way water slides off a slanted roof — it flows smoothly from upper-right down to lower-left, then simply lifts away without a hook or pause. That natural, effortless sweep is exactly what the stroke 丿 feels like when you write it.

Picture this. Imagine you are drawing a single blade of grass bending in the wind. That is 丿 — called piě in Chinese, which roughly means "to throw aside." The stroke literally tosses itself off the page.

Simple rule. 丿 always travels from upper-right → lower-left and ends by lifting the pen cleanly. You can remember it as the "slash" stroke. It appears in dozens of everyday characters — look closely at 人 (person), 入 (enter), and even in 兔 (rabbit), one of the characters in the phrase 守株待兔 that we are exploring in this book.

Try it yourself. On a piece of paper, draw five 丿 strokes in a row, each one starting a little further right than the last, like a row of falling raindrops. 巧 qiǎo skill, cleverness

Read together: "Familiarity can give rise to skill." In everyday English: practice makes perfect. Repetition transforms something difficult into something fluent — that is the whole promise of this phrase.

Try it yourself. Pick one small thing you want to improve — writing a character, playing a chord, chopping vegetables. Do it slowly ten times today. Notice what becomes easier. That small shift is 熟能生巧 at work.

Payoff 🎯

Think about the first time you tried to tie your shoelaces. Your fingers fumbled, you had to look down at every loop, and the whole thing felt impossibly slow. Then one day — without noticing exactly when — your hands just knew what to do.

That invisible crossing-point is what 熟能生巧 (shú néng shēng qiǎo) names. The phrase breaks into four characters: 熟 (shú, ripe / familiar), 能 (néng, can / is able to), 生 (shēng, give birth to / produce), and 巧 (qiǎo, skill / cleverness). Read together: familiarity produces skill. The richer English echo is "practice makes perfect," but the Chinese framing is subtler — it says that deep familiarity is itself the mechanism, not just a side effect.

This is the natural endpoint of the book because every concept you studied — how characters are built from radicals, how tones shift in context, how strokes flow into a rhythm — only becomes yours through repeated, attentive encounter. Understanding the rule is the beginning; 熟能生巧 is what happens after you revisit it a hundred times in real sentences.

Try it yourself: choose any single character from this book that still feels slippery. Write it by hand ten times today, say it aloud in one sentence, and find it in one article you read this week. Return to it tomorrow. Notice what changes. That noticing is 熟能生巧 in action — and it is the doorway to everything that comes next.
