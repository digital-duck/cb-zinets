🌱 一

🌱 一

Everyday example: Think about the last time you counted something — apples in a bowl, steps to the door, coins in your pocket. The very first count is always one. Before two, before ten, before a million, there is one.

Picture this: The Chinese character 一 (pronounced yī) is as simple as it looks: a single horizontal stroke, like a line drawn with one confident sweep of a brush. It means one — the number, the unit, the beginning. Nothing in written Chinese is simpler. That is its beauty.

Simple rule: 一 represents the number $1$ and carries a deeper idea too: wholeness and unity. When something is described as 一, it is complete in itself — undivided. In the phrase 马到成功 (mǎ dào chéng gōng, "success upon the horse's arrival"), the spirit of 一 lives underneath: commit as one, with single-minded focus, and success follows instantly.

Try it yourself: Write 一 now — just one slow, left-to-right horizontal line. Say yī aloud as you do. Notice how the stroke and the sound together feel complete. Then count three objects near you: yī, èr, sān (one, two, three). You have just spoken your first Chinese numbers, and 一 — the simplest stroke of all — was your starting point.

🌱 丨

🌱 丨

Everyday example. Think about a tall, straight fence post planted firmly in the ground — it stands upright, confident, and unmoving. That single vertical line is exactly what you are looking at in the stroke 丨.

Picture this. In Chinese writing, every character is built from a small set of basic brushstrokes, much like how every house is built from a handful of shapes — walls, roofs, windows. The stroke 丨 (pronounced shù, meaning "vertical") is one of the most fundamental of all. It is a single, clean downward line, drawn from top to bottom in one smooth motion.

Simple rule. Whenever you write 丨, your brush or pen starts at the top and travels straight down. Think of it as drawing a $1$ on a piece of paper, but without the little foot or hat — just the bare vertical line itself. Formally, you can think of it as a line segment along the $y$-axis: moving in the direction $(0, -1)$ from start to end.

Try it yourself. Grab a pencil and draw five vertical lines, one after another, each about the same height. Try to keep them parallel and equally spaced. Notice how the simple act of drawing that single straight line already feels deliberate and intentional — that sense of purpose is exactly the spirit of 丨, the backbone hidden inside countless Chinese characters you will soon recognize.

🍃 十

🍃 十

Imagine you are counting on your fingers. You start with one hand, then two — and when all ten fingers are up, you spread your palms wide in a satisfying cross shape. That moment of completeness is exactly what the character 十 (shí) captures.

Picture this: draw one horizontal line across the page, like a calm horizon. Now draw one vertical line straight through its center, like a pillar reaching up and down. What you have is a perfect cross — and that is 十, the character for ten.

The simple rule is this: 十 is always written in exactly two strokes, in a fixed order. First the horizontal stroke (left to right), then the vertical stroke (top to bottom). In Chinese, stroke order matters — it keeps the character balanced and beautiful, and it helps your hand build muscle memory.

十 is more than just the number ten, though. In Chinese culture, ten symbolizes completeness, the feeling that nothing is missing. When you achieve 马到成功 — instant, full success — you might say you gave 十 分 (shí fēn), meaning ten parts, or one hundred percent of yourself.

Try it yourself: Pick up a pen and write 十 five times, saying shí aloud with each stroke. Notice how two simple lines create something whole. You have just written one of the most ancient Chinese characters — and you did it in two strokes. That is already a small victory!

🌱 口

🌱 口

Think about the last time you opened your mouth to speak, eat, or sing. That simple, everyday action — the opening of your lips — has been part of human life for thousands of years. Ancient Chinese writers noticed this too, and they turned it into a character.

Picture this: Draw a small rectangle on paper, like an open box. Now squint at it and imagine looking straight down at an open mouth from above — top lip, bottom lip, and the dark space in between. That little square is the character 口 (kǒu). It is one of the oldest pictographs in Chinese writing: a picture that became a letter.

Simple rule: 口 means mouth, and it carries that meaning wherever it appears. On its own, it is pronounced kǒu (third tone — your voice dips down, then rises). More importantly, 口 acts as a building block. When you see it tucked inside a larger character, it often signals that the word has something to do with the mouth, speech, breath, or sound. Recognizing 口 is like finding a familiar face in a crowd — it tells you something useful right away.

Try it yourself: Write 口 three times, starting with the left vertical stroke, then the top horizontal, then the right vertical, and finally the bottom horizontal. Say kǒu aloud each time. Notice how the shape really does look like an open mouth — once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

🍃 古

🍃 古

Everyday example. Imagine your grandmother shows you a photograph so old and faded that the people in it are wearing clothes nobody wears anymore. You say, "Wow, that's old." In Chinese, the character for that feeling — something from a long, long time ago — is 古 (gǔ).

Picture this. The character 古 is built from two simple parts stacked vertically: 十 (shí, meaning "ten") on top, and 口 (kǒu, meaning "mouth") on the bottom. One traditional way to picture it: "ten mouths" passing a story down, generation after generation, the way old tales survive only by being spoken again and again by many people over many years. Whether or not that's the literal origin, it gives you a sturdy mental hook — many mouths, many years, one old story.

Simple rule. 古 means ancient, old, or from long ago. You'll see it in words like 古代 (gǔdài, "ancient times") and 古老 (gǔlǎo, "ancient, age-old"). Think of it as the opposite of 今 (jīn, "now, present").

Try it yourself. Look at the shape 古 — ten (十) over mouth (口). Picture ten generations of mouths retelling the same story. Now try writing it once, saying "gǔ" out loud, and naming one "old" thing you know — an old book, an old building, an old song. That's 古.

Picture this: 尸 (pronounced shī) looks like a simple side-view sketch of a person: a short horizontal stroke at the top, then a bent line curving down and reaching out to the right — almost like someone slumped forward or lying down. Ancient scribes drew this shape to represent the human body itself, especially in a reclining or folded position.

Simple rule: 尸 is a radical — a small visual building block that appears inside larger characters and quietly lends them its meaning. Whenever you spot 尸 tucked at the top of a character, think: a person in a settled, bent, or resting position. It points toward ideas connected to the human body, posture, and place.

🌱 上

Everyday example. Imagine placing a pot directly on top of a campfire. The pot sits on the flames — not beside them, not under them, but right on top. That simple relationship, "on top of," is exactly what 上 captures.

Picture this. Look at the character 上: a short horizontal line sitting above a longer one — like a small object resting on a table. The lower line is the surface; the upper line is whatever rests on it. The shape itself tells the story. Something is elevated, raised, perched above. Ancient writers drew this character to show a dot or mark above a baseline, pointing upward. Over centuries it settled into the clean two-line form we see today.

Simple rule. 上 (shàng) means above, on top of, or upward. It can describe a fixed position ("on the desk") or a direction of movement ("go up the stairs"). In 火上加油 — our key phrase — 上 sits between 火 (fire) and 加油 (add oil), acting as a bridge: oil is being poured on top of the fire. That one small character makes the whole image vivid and precise.

Try it yourself. Picture these two scenes:

  • A book lying on a desk → the book is 上 the desk.
  • Climbing upstairs → you are moving 上.

In both cases, 上 points to the same core idea: elevation, whether as a location or a direction. Hold that image — something rising, something resting on top — and 火上加油 will feel immediately alive.

🌱 后

🌱 后

Everyday example. Imagine a relay race. One runner finishes her lap and hands the baton to the next person waiting behind her. That next runner — the one who comes after — is exactly what 后 captures.

Picture this. 后 (hòu) means after, behind, or what comes next. Think of a timeline laid out in front of you:

$$ \underbrace{\text{past}}_{\text{前}} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{now} \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\text{future}}_{\text{后}} $$

前 (qián) sits on the left — everything that came before. 后 sits on the right — everything that comes after. The arrow between them is time moving forward.

Simple rule. Whenever something follows, succeeds, or comes downstream of something else, 后 is the right character. It shows up in everyday words like 以后 (yǐ hòu, "from now on") and 后来 (hòu lái, "later on"). In our idiom 承前启后, it names the destination: the future generation or era that is being opened up.

Try it yourself. Look at the phrase 启后 — the second half of our idiom. 启 means "to open" or "to initiate." Put them together: "open the after." Can you see how this paints a picture of unlocking a door to the future? What door might a teacher, a book, or a discovery open for the people who come after?

Imagine moving into a new apartment. You settle in, hang your coat on the wall, and make the space yours. That act of occupying a place — of being firmly situated somewhere — is exactly what 居 (jū) captures.

Picture this: A family arrives last at a campsite but claims the best, shadiest spot by the stream. Everyone else watches, a little envious. The latecomers have settled into the prime position. That is 居 in action.

居 carries the core meaning of to dwell, to reside, to hold a place. It can describe where you live — 居住 (jū zhù) means "to reside" — or the position you occupy in any sense: physical, social, or ranked. In 后来居上, the piece 居上 means "to occupy the top position" or "to sit at the highest place." Reading the whole idiom now feels natural: the one who came later (后来) ends up holding the highest spot (居上).

Simple rule: Think of 居 as settling firmly into a position — a home, a rank, or a role.

🌱 来

Imagine you text a friend, "I'm on my way!" A moment later, you walk through the door. That moment of arriving — of movement completing itself — is exactly what 来 captures.

Picture this: The ancient form of 来 looked like a wheat stalk, heavy with grain, bending toward the earth as it ripens. Over centuries, scribes began using this image to mean come or arrive — the idea of something moving toward you, reaching its destination.

In 卷土重来, 来 is the final stroke of the whole phrase. You have the dust (土) rolling and swirling (卷), and then — 来 — it arrives. The force returns. Think of 来 as the landing, the moment the wave crashes ashore after a long journey across the sea.

Simple rule: Whenever you see 来, think movement completing toward a point. It answers the question "Where did it end up?" with a single, confident syllable: here. It came.

Try it yourself: Look at these two phrases:

  • 回来 (huí lái) — return (go back + come = come back)
  • 进来 (jìn lái) — come in (enter + come = arrive inside)

In each case, 来 is the arrival half — the moment of reaching. In 卷土重来, that arrival is triumphant: the storm has gathered, traveled, and now — unmistakably — it is here.

The whole phrase works as one idea — someone who arrives later than everyone else eventually claims the highest position. You don't need to disassemble it to feel its meaning; just hold onto that image of finishing first despite starting last.

Picture a race where the runner in last place slowly, steadily overtakes everyone — not because the others were weak, but because this runner kept pushing past the finish line others thought was good enough. 后来居上 celebrates exactly that reversal of expectations.

Simple rule: whenever a newcomer or a later effort ends up outperforming what came before it, you can reach for 后来居上. It fits a younger sibling who earns higher grades, a startup that beats an older company, or a student who eventually surpasses the teacher.
