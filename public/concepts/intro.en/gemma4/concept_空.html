🍃 空

🍃 空

Think about a blank sheet of printer paper. Right now, it holds no specific image—it is empty. But that very emptiness isn't nothing; it’s pure potential. This simple example helps us understand kū, or "emptiness." It doesn't mean zero objects exist; rather, it describes the vast openness that allows anything to appear later—a poem, a drawing, an equation.

Picture this blank page not as lacking content, but as having infinite capacity. The paper itself is merely a medium, a potential field waiting for ink, light, or thought. In philosophy, kū means understanding that nothing truly exists in isolation; everything relies on what surrounds it. This concept of "emptiness" doesn't mean absence, but rather the foundational space—the underlying possibility—that makes things possible to begin with.

The simple rule is this: Emptiness (空) is not a void, but the ground state of potentiality. It is the background that allows all forms to appear and disappear. Because everything arises in this open field, nothing has a fixed, permanent 'self.'

To try it yourself today, pause for one minute and look at an everyday object—a chair, a cup, or even your own hand. Instead of just seeing the solid form, notice the space around it, beneath it, and within its shape. Recognize that the solidity of the object is defined by the emptiness supporting it.
