Think about a tide pool at the beach. At first, it might be full and still; then, as the ocean recedes, it empties out until only a few puddles remain. This constant shift—from fullness to emptiness, and back again—is an everyday example of Yì, which simply means Change. Change is not just something that happens; it is the fundamental state of existence: nothing stays exactly the same forever.

Picture this universe as a massive river flowing from one point to another. The water itself never stops moving, constantly transforming its location and speed. This steady flow is what we call flux. Flux describes the continuous process of transformation—the idea that everything is always on the move. Change reminds us that even solid mountains are subject to erosion over millennia, proving that no state is permanent.

The simple rule derived from observing this flux is: change happens in cyclical patterns. Things don't just randomly break; they shift from one condition to a complementary opposing condition and then back again. Instead of seeing things as fixed states (A $\to$ B), think of them as balanced interactions (A ↔ B). This inherent balance means that when one force gets too strong, it naturally creates the conditions for its opposite to rise.

To try this yourself, take a moment and observe an object near you—a pencil, a cup, or a plant leaf. Instead of only noticing its current form, try to identify how it was yesterday and how it might be tomorrow. Recognizing the potential shifts in that single item already connects you to the profound wisdom of recognizing universal change.
