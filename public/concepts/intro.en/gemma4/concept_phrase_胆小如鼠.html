🌸 Phrase 胆小如鼠

Imagine a friend who has a brilliant idea in class, but every time they open their mouth, they feel a wave of nervousness wash over them, and they keep silent. This situation perfectly illustrates the phrase dǎn xiǎo rú shǔ. It is an idiom that describes someone who is extremely timid or fearful—someone whose courage (胆) is as small (小) as a mouse (鼠). When we say someone is dǎn xiǎo rú shǔ, we are not just saying they are slightly nervous; we mean their fear makes them almost freeze up.

Picture this: If you see a tiny creature, like a field mouse, how would it react to a sudden loud noise? It wouldn't hesitate—it would bolt instantly! The phrase uses the image of the mouse to show that the level of timidity is maximal and instinctive. It’s a vivid comparison used when someone's fear makes them seem overly cautious or easily startled in situations where they should be brave.

The simple rule for using this idiom is: Reserve it only for describing extreme cases of cowardice or crippling nervousness. If your friend merely feels a little nervous, you might use another phrase. But if their anxiety prevents them from taking necessary action—if they are paralyzed by fear—then calling them dǎn xiǎo rú shǔ captures the severity of their timidity.

Try it yourself: The next time you notice someone hesitating to speak up or take a risk, reflect on why. Understanding this phrase is the first step toward realizing that courage isn't about never being afraid; it’s about acting even when your heart races like a mouse!
