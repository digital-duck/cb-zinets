🍃 油

Imagine two friends having a small disagreement—maybe about who left the dishes out. This is the initial fire, the minor conflict itself. Now, let's say a third person walks by and immediately says, "Oh yeah, remember last month when you did X?" Suddenly, that small disagreement has exploded into an argument filled with old grievances. That extra comment—the mention of past mistakes—is the oil. In this context, 'oil' doesn't mean physical oil; it is a metaphor for anything added to a situation that dramatically increases its emotional heat or intensity. It’s not necessarily creating the problem, but making the existing problem much bigger and harder to solve.

Picture this: The conflict already exists (the smoke). If you add nothing, the fire slowly burns down. But if you pour oil on it, the flames instantly leap higher, becoming overwhelming and chaotic. Recognizing 'oil' means learning to identify those specific comments or actions that take a minor disagreement and escalate it into a major emotional crisis. These are usually generalizations, dredging up old issues, or using extreme language when simple facts would do.

The simple rule is this: When you detect rising tension, pause and ask yourself if the current contribution is providing new information or just fuel for the drama. If your comment serves only to amplify past hurts or exaggerate a minor point, it might be acting as oil. To keep things manageable, focus on cooling down the situation rather than adding anything extra.

Try it yourself: Think about a time you witnessed an argument that escalated quickly. What was the original fire (the subject), and what specific comment or action do you now identify as the moment the 'oil' was poured on it?
