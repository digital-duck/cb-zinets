🌸 Phrase 狼心狗肺

Imagine a scenario: You spent weeks helping a friend prepare for a big job interview—driving you to practice, reviewing your notes late at night—only for that friend to quickly forget your efforts and make cruel jokes about your nervousness the moment you succeed. This extreme lack of gratitude is exactly what wáng xīn gǒu fèi describes. It is an idiom used when someone behaves in a deeply ungrateful or callous manner, betraying kindness with cold indifference.

Picture this phrase literally: "Wolf heart" and "dog lungs." The imagery suggests that the person's moral core (the wolf) and even their empathy (the dog) are fundamentally animalistic—wild, brutal, and lacking human warmth. It is a powerful accusation that someone has abandoned basic human decency in exchange for self-interest or malice.

The simple rule to remember is this: If a person betrays deep trust or shows spectacular ingratitude toward those who have genuinely helped them, you can use wáng xīn gǒu fèi. This phrase is reserved for profound moral failing, not just minor disappointment. It signals that the behavior was utterly heartless.

To practice, consider this sentence: "He abandoned his family after spending all their savings on him." Based on the severity of the betrayal, do you think wáng xīn gǒu fèi is an appropriate description? Think about how profound moral failure translates into language!
