🍃 色

Think about the last time you saw a vivid sunset—the brilliant orange bleeding into soft lavender. That experience is an example of 色 (se, color or physical quality). When we talk about Se in this context, we are not just talking about pigment; we are talking about all observable qualities: the brightness of light, the hardness of stone, or the warmth of emotion. These qualities are what our senses register and form into a cohesive experience.

Picture this: Imagine your eyes are like tiny cameras that capture wavelengths of light. When light hits something, the object reflects certain wavelengths and absorbs others. The specific combination of reflected energy is the se we perceive as "red" or "blue." This phenomenon shows us that physical qualities always require a medium—like light, sound waves, or touch—to exist. Sensory data is simply the information gathered by our five senses (sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch); it is the raw input before our minds organize it into concepts.

The simple rule to remember about Se is this: For something to be categorized as a physical quality, it must be perceivable through interaction with external reality. It has dimension and observable change. If you can point to it, measure it (like length $L$), or categorize its visible spectrum $\Lambda$, then it falls under the domain of se.

To try this yourself: Close your eyes for a moment and recall an object in the room that is not colorful—perhaps a piece of plain wood. What physical qualities do you still notice? Do you sense texture (rough, smooth), temperature (cool, warm), or weight? Recognizing these non-visual, yet measurable, attributes helps solidify the idea that se encompasses everything we can empirically observe about our material world.
