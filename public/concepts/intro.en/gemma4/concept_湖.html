🍃 湖

Have you ever stood by a large body of water that seems completely still? Maybe it’s a local pond or a vast inland expanse. That is often what we mean when we talk about a lake ($\text{湖}$). Unlike a river, which flows constantly from one point to another, a lake holds water in place. It is a natural area of standing fresh water—meaning the liquid hasn't been moving significantly for long periods—that is completely surrounded by land on all sides.

Picture this: Imagine an enormous bathtub cupped within the landscape. The edges are defined by hills and earth, keeping the water contained. This containment is key; if water flows out one side, it’s a river or stream. If it's connected to the massive ocean, it might be called a bay. But when land forms a natural basin, trapping fresh water within, you have a lake.

The simple rule for identifying a lake is this: it must be an enclosed body of standing water that has no direct outflow path (or if it does, the outflow is minor compared to its size). We can think of its volume ($V$) as being contained by surrounding geographical boundaries ($\text{Boundary}$). $\text{Lake} = V \text{ within } \text{Boundary}$.

Try it yourself: If a body of water slowly drains out through one end and flows into the sea, is it more accurately called a lake or a river? Think about whether its primary feature is movement or containment.
