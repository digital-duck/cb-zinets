Imagine you are at a local market buying fresh produce, perhaps some sweet potatoes or leafy greens. When you ask the vendor how much the goods cost, they don't just give you a price; they use a specific unit of weight to measure your purchase. This unit is called jīn (literally meaning 'half a kilogram'). It’s one of the traditional units we use in China for measuring mass or weight.

Picture this: Think about weighing something on a familiar scale, like those used in grocery stores today. While modern scales might display kilograms ($\text{kg}$) or pounds ($\text{lb}$), historically and locally, jīn was the standard measure. To make it concrete, remember that one jīn is exactly equal to 500 grams (or $0.5 \text{ kg}$). It’s a straightforward conversion: $\text{1 jīn} = 500 \text{ g}$.

The simple rule for understanding jīn is this: If you see a price listed per jīn, they are referring to the cost of 500 grams of the item. This unit helps keep transactions consistent, making it easy for both buyers and sellers to agree on the quantity being exchanged.
