🍃 欠

Imagine you are helping a friend move furniture, and they forget the heavy dolly. You lend them your own—a temporary loan of equipment. When the job is done, even though you didn't charge money, you know that the favor creates an obligation; they owe you time or gratitude. This feeling of having given something without receiving it yet is the core idea behind 欠. It does not always mean a financial debt; often, qiàn refers to any deficit—a piece of knowledge you need more of, or perhaps emotional time that needs replenishing.

Picture this: Now think about something slightly less tangible, like sleep. If you stay up late studying for an exam, you have created a small 'debt' against your rest. You haven't physically borrowed anything, but the lack of sleep makes you feel obligated to take a break later. This concept of owing or lacking is powerful because it helps us track imbalances in our lives—be it physical energy, emotional support, or required resources.

The simple rule for understanding qiàn is this: If something is missing, incomplete, or requires balancing out after an expenditure, you have created a deficit, which we describe as 欠. It is the recognition that your current state is not perfectly balanced with what was given or done.

Try it yourself: Think of a time you finished a difficult project at work. You gave massive effort (a surplus), but now you feel exhausted and need rest (the required balance). What deficit did that intense effort create?
