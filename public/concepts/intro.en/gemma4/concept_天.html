🍃 天

🍃 天

Imagine you are sketching a lovely, flowing river on paper. You draw the curve perfectly—it's natural and beautiful. Now, because you worry it might look too simple, you decide to add tiny little bridges connecting every single bend. Suddenly, the river doesn’t feel free anymore; it feels rigid and cluttered. This is often the trap we fall into when we try to correct or improve something that was already working just fine.

Picture this: The concept of Tiān (天), which literally means "Heaven" or "Sky," represents the natural, effortless flow—the way things simply are without forced effort. When we talk about following the Tiān, we mean embracing spontaneity and recognizing simplicity in nature. It is the principle that advises us to trust the initial draft, even if it seems incomplete to our critical eyes. It’s less about knowing complicated rules and more about observing what feels right in the moment.

The simple rule here is: Trust the natural momentum first. Before you add a fix or an extra layer of detail, pause and ask yourself, "What does this look like when left alone?" When we follow Tiān, our instinct shifts from "How can I make this perfect?" to "What is already working?"

Try it yourself: Think about a recent small project—maybe writing a short email or organizing a desk. Instead of listing every single thing you could improve, simply observe what is already functioning smoothly. That initial, un-interrupted flow is your guide back to the wisdom of the natural way.
