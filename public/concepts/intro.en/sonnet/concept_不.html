🌱 不

Imagine a traffic light turning red. The red light means stop — do not go. In English, we add the word "not" to flip a sentence from yes to no. Chinese has a single character that does exactly the same job: 不 (bù).

Picture this. You already know 学, which means to learn. The moment you place 不 in front of it — 不学 — the meaning flips completely: not learning. It is like a tiny switch. Whatever word follows 不, that action or quality is negated, turned off, reversed.

In our saying, 不 appears twice:

$$ \text{学而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{思则罔，思而} \underbrace{\text{不}}_{\text{not}} \text{学则殆} $$

Each time, 不 reaches forward and negates the character right after it. The second 不 negates 学 — giving us not learning, a phrase you can already read. We will meet the other characters one by one in the sections ahead.

Simple rule. Place 不 directly before any verb or adjective to negate it:

$$ \text{不} + \text{[word]} = \text{not [word]} $$

No extra words needed — 不 does all the work alone.

Try it yourself. You know that 学 means to learn. What does 不学 mean? If you said not learning, you already understand 不. Now look back at the full saying — can you find both places where 不 appears and flips the meaning?
