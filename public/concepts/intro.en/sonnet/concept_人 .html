🌱 人

Think about the last time you watched someone walk. Notice how they lean forward slightly, one foot in front of the other, arms swaying for balance — two legs working together to keep a single person upright.

Picture this: The Chinese character 人 (rén) is a tiny drawing of exactly that — a person mid-stride, seen from the side. Two strokes lean against each other like two legs sharing the load. Neither stroke can stand alone; they hold each other up. That is the whole character: just two lines, and a human being.

人 means person or people. It is one of the oldest and most common characters in the Chinese writing system. You will find it hiding inside hundreds of other characters, quietly lending its meaning of "human" wherever it appears.

In the idiom 水火不容 — where water and fire cannot coexist — 人 reminds us that it is people who must navigate such opposites every day. A peacemaker standing between two rivals, a friend caught between two worlds: that is 人 in real life.

Simple rule: When you see 人, think human being.

Try it yourself: Write 人 with two strokes — first the left-leaning stroke, then the right-leaning one. Say rén aloud. Can you feel the balance in it?
