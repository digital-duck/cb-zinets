🌱 井

Everyday example. Imagine you are thirsty on a hot summer day. Long before water came from faucets, people dug deep holes in the ground to reach underground water. That hole — a well — is exactly what the character 井 (jǐng, pronounced like "jing") represents.

Picture this. Look at the shape of 井 closely. It looks like a square divided into four sections — almost like a bird's-eye view of a stone-lined well with two wooden beams crossing its opening to hold a bucket rope. Ancient Chinese writers drew precisely that: a top-down sketch of a well's mouth. The two horizontal strokes and two vertical strokes crossing them form a little grid, mapping the shaft below and the rim above.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 井, think well — a deep, narrow hole in the ground that holds water. In our idiom 井底之蛙, 井 appears first and sets the entire scene. A well is small, deep, and enclosed. Those three qualities — smallness, depth, enclosure — echo through every character and idea that follows in the story.

Try it yourself. Draw 井 from memory: lay down two horizontal strokes first, then cross them with two vertical strokes. Does it remind you of a tic-tac-toe grid? That square pattern is the exact shape a well's opening makes when you peer down at it from above. Every time you write it, you are drawing a tiny map of a world that is about to feel very, very small.
