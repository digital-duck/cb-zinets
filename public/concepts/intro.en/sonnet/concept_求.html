🍃 求

When you really want something — a lost toy, a better grade, the last slice of pizza — you don't just wait. You reach out, you search, you ask. That active reaching is exactly what 求 captures.

Picture this: Imagine a child stretching both arms toward a shelf that's just out of reach. Every muscle is straining toward one goal. That outstretched, striving motion is the heart of 求. The character itself echoes this: it shows something being grasped or pursued, not merely wished for.

The simple rule: 求 means to seek, to request, to strive for. It implies movement toward a goal — not passive hoping, but active pursuit. When you 求 something, you are doing the reaching.

You'll find it in everyday Chinese: 请求 (qǐng qiú) means "to make a request," and 追求 (zhuī qiú) means "to pursue" — a dream, a person, an ambition. In the story 刻舟求剑, a man tries to 求 (retrieve) his sword from a river using a mark on a moving boat — a humorous image of seeking something in the wrong place.

Try it yourself: Think of one thing you are actively working toward right now — a skill, a goal, a conversation you need to have. That effort, that forward lean, is 求 in action. The character isn't about wanting; it's about doing the reaching.
