🌱 頁

🌱 頁

When you flip through a book, you turn one page at a time — each page holds its own story, waiting to be read. In Chinese, the character 頁 (yè) carries exactly that meaning: page or leaf of a book.

Picture this. Long before paper existed, ancient Chinese scribes wrote on silk scrolls. A single flat section of the scroll — one "face" of text — became 頁. Look closely at the character: the top portion suggests a head with hair (two short strokes), and below it stands a figure with legs. Why a head? Because 頁 originally meant "head" itself — and a page, like a head, is the visible face that leads everything else.

Simple rule. 頁 works as a building block (called a radical) inside many characters related to the head, face, and what we see. Once you spot 頁 hiding inside a character, it's often a hint that the meaning involves appearance, looking, or the front of something:

$$ \text{頁} \to \text{頭 (tóu, head)},\quad \text{顏 (yán, face/color)},\quad \text{題 (tí, topic/title)} $$

Think of 頁 as the "face" radical — wherever it appears, something front-and-visible is nearby.

Try it yourself. Look at the character 題 (topic). Can you find 頁 on the right side? The left part means "straight/upright." Together: a straight face presenting a topic — the heading at the front of an idea.
