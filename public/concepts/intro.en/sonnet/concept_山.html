🌱 山

🌱 山

Everyday example. Think about the last time you saw a mountain range on the horizon — three peaks rising side by side, the tallest one standing proudly in the middle. That simple silhouette is exactly what the Chinese character 山 (shān) looks like.

Picture this. Draw three vertical lines. Now connect them at the bottom with a single horizontal stroke. The middle line stretches a little taller than the two beside it. You have just drawn 山. Ancient Chinese writers looked at real mountains and traced their outline directly onto oracle bones thousands of years ago. The character has barely changed since. What you write today is a direct echo of those earliest strokes.

Simple rule. 山 means mountain. It is also a radical — think of a radical as a building-block that appears inside many other characters and hints at their meaning. Whenever you spot 山 hiding inside a larger character, there is a good chance that character has something to do with mountains, rocks, or high places. For example, 岩 (yán, cliff or rock) contains 山 at the top.

Try it yourself. Trace 山 three times, saying shān softly as you write. Then look at the word 火山 (huǒshān, volcano — literally "fire mountain"). Can you find 山 inside it? Notice how the meaning of the whole word comes partly from the building-block you just learned.
