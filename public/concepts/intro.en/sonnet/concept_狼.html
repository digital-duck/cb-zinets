🍃 狼

🍃 狼

Everyday example. Imagine a traveler who rescues a freezing wolf, only to have it turn on him the moment it warms up. That image — dangerous, ungrateful, hiding threat behind a calm exterior — is exactly the feeling the character 狼 carries in Chinese. It means wolf, and in stories and idioms it almost always stands for cruelty and treachery.

Picture this. The character 狼 is built from two visible parts. On the left is 犭, the animal radical — a shorthand stroke that signals "this word belongs to the world of animals." On the right sits 良, meaning good or fine. Put them together and you get a striking contrast: an animal that appears fine on the surface but is secretly dangerous. Many Chinese characters work exactly this way — a meaning clue on the left, a sound clue on the right.

Simple rule. Whenever you spot 犭 on the left side of a character, the word almost always names an animal or describes animal-like behavior. 狼 (wolf), 狗 (dog), 猫 (cat) — all share that same left-side stroke. Think of 犭 as a label on a box: it tells you the category before you finish reading the character.

Try it yourself. Cover the right half of 狼 and focus on 犭. Does it look a little like a leaping creature? Now uncover 良. The wolf disguises its nature behind something that seems fine. In the idiom 狼心狗肺, 狼 sets the opening note — ruthless, cold, and without mercy.
