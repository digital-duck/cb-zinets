🌸 Phrase 自相矛盾

Imagine a friend tells you, "I never check my phone at dinner" — and then, two minutes later, pulls it out to show you a video. Those two claims knock each other flat. The first says never; the second punches a hole straight through it. When your own words defeat your own words, that is a self-contradiction.

Picture two puzzle pieces that refuse to fit no matter how you turn them. Each looks reasonable on its own, but the moment you press them together, you can see they clash. A self-contradiction works the same way: two statements, both claimed at once, yet impossible to hold at the same time.

The four-character Chinese phrase 自相矛盾 (zì xiāng máo dùn) captures this idea with a sharp image — literally "one's own spear against one's own shield." The phrase points to any situation where a person's own claims undercut each other, the way a perfectly unstoppable spear and a perfectly impenetrable shield cannot both exist in the same world.

The simple rule: a self-contradiction appears whenever a statement and its direct opposite are both asserted at once. One must be false — they cannot coexist.

Try it yourself: read these two sentences — "The jar is completely empty" and "There is one coin inside the jar." Do they contradict each other? Yes. Completely empty and one coin inside cannot both be true at the same moment. Catching that kind of clash is the heart of clear thinking.
