🌱 乛 一

Think about drawing a straight road on a map. You put your pen down, pull it smoothly to the right, and lift it up. That single motion — one continuous line — is exactly what the stroke 一 (yī) is. It means "one," and it is the very first character most learners of Chinese ever write.

Now picture that same road, but just before the end, the driver turns the wheel and the line hooks upward — like a finger curling to beckon someone closer. That small curl at the finish is the stroke 乛. It is not a full character on its own; it is a component, a building-block shape that lives inside larger characters and gives the brush something to push off of at the stroke's end.

The simple rule: 一 is a flat horizontal line with a clean lift-off; 乛 is a horizontal line that finishes with a sharp upward hook. Both move left-to-right, but 乛 adds one extra flick at the close. You will see 乛 tucked into many characters — including shā jī jǐng hóu (杀鸡儆猴), where it quietly anchors a stroke inside a more complex shape.

Try it yourself: Draw a straight line across a piece of paper — that is 一. Now draw the same line, but at the very end flick your pen up like you are dotting a question mark from below. That flick is 乛. Repeat three times each and notice how one small motion changes the whole feel of the stroke.
