🍃 添

🍃 添

Everyday example. Imagine you bake a perfect chocolate cake. It looks beautiful, smells amazing, and everyone loves it. Then someone decides to pour ketchup on top "to make it better." They have added something — but that addition ruined everything.

Picture this. The character 添 (tiān) means to add or to increase. Look at its pieces: the left side, 氵, suggests water, while the right side hints at something growing or filling up. Think of slowly pouring water into a glass until it overflows — that overflowing, that adding too much, is the spirit of 添.

Simple rule. 添 always means adding something on top of what already exists. In our idiom 画蛇添足, the snake drawing is already complete and perfect. The person then 添 (adds) 足 (feet) onto it. The snake doesn't need feet — the addition is unnecessary and absurd. So 添 here carries a quiet warning: not every addition is an improvement.

Try it yourself. Think of a sentence like "I added sugar to my tea" — in Chinese, that added is 添. Now think: was it the right amount, or did you 添 too much? The character reminds us to pause before adding anything and ask, does this truly belong here? Sometimes the wisest move is to leave things exactly as they are.
