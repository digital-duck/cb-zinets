🌱 年

Think about the last time you watched the seasons change — spring blossoms giving way to summer heat, then autumn harvests, then winter snow. By the time the blossoms return, exactly one year has passed. That cycle of seasons is precisely what the Chinese character 年 (nián) captures.

Picture an ancient farmer hoisting a bundle of harvested grain onto their back. In old China, you measured time by harvests: one harvest meant one year had gone by. The earliest written form of 年 showed exactly this image — a person bent under the weight of ripened grain. Over centuries the brushstrokes simplified, but the meaning stayed rooted in that yearly rhythm of planting, growing, and reaping.

The simple rule: 年 = one complete cycle of the seasons, i.e., a year. You will find it everywhere time is counted. 今年 (jīnnián) means "this year," 明年 (míngnián) means "next year," and 去年 (qùnián) means "last year." Even in the proverb 百年树人 — "it takes a hundred years to cultivate a person" — 年 does exactly this job: it stacks up those harvest-cycles to express a very long stretch of time.

Try it yourself: how would you say "ten years" in Chinese? You already know 十 (shí) means ten. Put it together — 十年 (shí nián). One character, one harvest cycle, multiplied by ten. That's all there is to it.
