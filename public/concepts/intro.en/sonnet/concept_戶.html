🌱 戶

Everyday example: Picture the front entrance to a small house — not a grand double gate, but a single wooden panel you push open every morning to step outside. That single-leaf door is exactly what the character 戶 (hù) was drawn to show.

Picture this: Long ago, Chinese writing used simple pictures to represent real objects. 戶 began as a sketch of one half of a door — a single panel attached to a frame, swinging on its own. It's the humbler version of a door: the entrance to an ordinary home, not a grand palace gate with two panels swinging side by side.

Simple rule: When you see 戶 in a character, think "household" or "single door." Over time, 戶 grew beyond just meaning "door" — it came to stand for a family unit or home, since every household had its own door. You'll see this idea reused in words like 戶口 (hùkǒu, household registration) and 一戶人家 (yī hù rén jiā, "one household/family").

Try it yourself: Draw a single vertical line with a short diagonal stroke at the top, like a door leaning against a wall. Now picture that same shape standing alone, guarding the entrance to one home. Holding this single-panel image in mind will help you recognize 戶 wherever it appears, and prepare you to compare it with other door-shaped characters later on.
