🌱 飞

Everyday example. Imagine you startle a flock of pigeons in a park — one second they're calmly pecking at the ground, the next they explode upward in every direction, wings beating frantically. That sudden burst of movement is exactly what the character 飞 (fēi) captures.

Picture this. 飞 means to fly or flying. Look at the character itself: the sweeping strokes suggest wings spread wide and lifted off the ground. In the idiom 鸡飞狗跳 (jī fēi gǒu tiào), 飞 describes the chickens — they've been frightened and they shoot straight into the air. The word 鸡 (jī) means chicken, so 鸡飞 together paints the picture: chickens flying. Not a graceful, peaceful flight — a panicked scramble skyward.

Simple rule. In Chinese, 飞 works both as a verb ("the bird flies") and as a description of motion ("flying upward"). When you see 飞 in a phrase, ask: what is leaving the ground, and why? In our idiom, the answer is: chickens, because everything around them has gone chaotic.

Try it yourself. Say the syllable fēi out loud — let your voice rise slightly, like something lifting off. Now picture a startled chicken flapping wildly upward. That image is your anchor for 飞. Every time you see this character, let it remind you of wings, motion, and something that refuses to stay still.
