🍃 禾

🍃 禾

Everyday example. Imagine a farmer's field at harvest time: golden stalks of grain bowing under the weight of ripe seeds, each plant bent gently at the top. You have just pictured the Chinese character 禾 (hé).

Picture this. 禾 is one of China's oldest pictographs — a drawing that became a word. The vertical stroke is the stalk. The short horizontal stroke near the top is a leaf. The diagonal stroke curving downward represents the heavy seed head drooping to one side, just as real grain does when it is ready to be picked. Over thousands of years, brushstrokes smoothed and simplified that little sketch into the tidy character you see today.

Simple rule. 禾 means grain crop or cereal plant — think rice, millet, wheat. Whenever you spot 禾 hiding inside a larger character, there is usually some connection to crops, harvests, or agriculture. Here is the exciting part: it also hides inside 秒 (miǎo), the character for second (as in one second of time). Ancient Chinese thinkers imagined the tip of a grain stalk — the very finest, smallest part — as a symbol for the tiniest slice of time. That is why 禾 lives inside 秒, and why 秒 lives inside our phrase 争分夺秒, "racing against every second."

Try it yourself. Look at 秒 and see if you can spot 禾 on the left side. What do you think the right-hand part 少 (shǎo, meaning few or small) adds to the image of a tiny grain tip?
