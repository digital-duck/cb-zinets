🍃 侯

The character 侯 (hóu) means marquis — a noble lord, someone who holds authority over a region and its people. Think of it like a duke or earl in European history: powerful, respected, and responsible for keeping order in their territory.

Picture this: Imagine a feudal kingdom. At the top sits the king, and just below him are a handful of trusted nobles who each govern a province. The 侯 is one of those nobles — not the highest title, but still a person of real rank and influence.

Simple rule: In classical Chinese, 侯 sits in the second tier of the five noble ranks (公、侯、伯、子、男 — Duke, Marquis, Earl, Viscount, Baron). Whenever you see 侯 in an ancient text, it signals aristocracy, hierarchy, and the idea that power comes with responsibility. In modern Chinese, the word still carries that old aura of dignity; you'll find it in surnames like 侯 (Hóu) and in set phrases that evoke traditional authority. In the idiom 杀鸡儆猴, the 猴 ("monkey") is the real target of a warning — but the image works precisely because the 侯-like figure in charge needs the whole group to obey.
