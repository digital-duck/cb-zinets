🍃 乂

Everyday example. Have you ever used scissors to cut paper, watching the two blades cross each other to slice cleanly through? That crossing, cutting motion is captured in a single Chinese character.

Picture this. The character 乂 (pronounced yì) looks almost exactly like the letter X — two strokes crossing each other near the middle. One stroke sweeps from the upper-left down to the lower-right; the other rises from the lower-left up to the upper-right. Where they meet, they form a neat intersection point. You can sketch it as two directed paths:

$$ \searrow \;\text{meets}\; \swarrow \;\Longrightarrow\; \times $$

That crossing is 乂.

Simple rule. 乂 is a pictographic primitive — a building block whose visual shape echoes its original meaning. Its ancient meaning was to cut or to mow, like two blades meeting to harvest grass. As a primitive, 乂 does not usually stand alone in modern text, but it hides inside larger characters, quietly contributing its "crossing" energy to their meanings.

Try it yourself. Trace 乂 slowly with your finger: begin the first stroke at the top-left and sweep toward the bottom-right. Then add the second stroke starting at the bottom-left, crossing the first as you sweep upward toward the upper-right. Feel the moment the two paths cross — that is the heart of the character. Once you can spot that X-shape, you will start to notice 乂 tucked inside other characters all around you.
