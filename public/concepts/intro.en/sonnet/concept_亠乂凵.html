🌱 亠乂凵

Think about building with LEGO blocks. No matter how complex the finished model looks, it is always made from the same familiar pieces snapping together. Chinese characters work exactly the same way — every character is built from a small set of recurring shapes called components (sometimes called radicals when they carry meaning on their own).

Today, meet three of these fundamental pieces: 亠, 乂, and 凵.

Picture this: 亠 looks like a small hat or lid — two strokes that cap whatever sits beneath them. 乂 resembles two paths crossing each other, like a brushstroke X drawn with gentle curves. And 凵 is like an open box or cup sitting upright, ready to hold something inside.

A simple rule to carry forward: each component has a shape identity you can recognize at a glance, no matter which full character it appears in. Once you lock in what 亠, 乂, and 凵 look like in isolation, your eye will automatically spot them hiding inside larger, more complex characters — just as you might recognize a familiar LEGO brick inside a finished rocket ship.

Try it yourself: trace each shape slowly — 亠, then 乂, then 凵. As you draw each one, whisper a nickname to yourself: hat, cross, open box. Now imagine stacking them: a hat on top, a crossing stroke in the middle, an open container below. Hold that mental image — you have just built your first component-reading habit.
