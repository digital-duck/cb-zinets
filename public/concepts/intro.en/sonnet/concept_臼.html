🌱 臼

Imagine an old wooden bowl carved into a heavy stone block — deep, round, and hollow in the middle. For thousands of years, people used exactly this kind of vessel to pound grain: you drop rice or wheat inside, then crush it with a heavy stick called a pestle. The shape is so practical and ancient that the Chinese written for it has barely changed in three millennia.

Picture this: Hold your two hands out flat, palms up, fingertips touching. Now curl all your fingers downward slightly, leaving a bowl-shaped hollow between your palms. That cupped hollow is the core idea behind 臼 — a container shaped by its emptiness, defined by what it holds rather than what surrounds it.

The simple rule: 臼 (jiù) means mortar — the hollow vessel used for grinding or pounding. In Chinese writing, it appears as a rough picture of that hollowed-out stone bowl, open at the top, with two short strokes inside suggesting the worn grooves left by years of pounding. Whenever you spot 臼 inside a more complex character, something about that character usually involves grinding, hollows, or ancient kitchen work.

Try it yourself: Look at the character 舀 (yǎo, meaning "to scoop"). Can you find 臼 hiding at the bottom? The hand reaching down into a mortar gives you the whole picture. In 鼠目寸光, 臼 lives quietly inside 臼, reminding us how deep, enclosed spaces — even tiny ones — shape what we can see.
