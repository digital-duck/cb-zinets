🍃 行

🍃 行

Think about the last time you decided to stop planning and actually do something — get up, step forward, make it happen. In Chinese, that shift from intention to action is captured by the character 行 (xíng).

The earliest drawings of 行 looked like a crossroads — two paths crossing at the center of a town. A crossroads is a place of movement and decision; it is where journeys begin. From that image, the character grew to mean to walk, to travel, to act, to carry out. Whenever something moves from idea into reality, 行 is the word for it.

Here is the simple rule: 行 means to go forward — physically, practically, actually. It answers the question Is this really happening? A plan sitting on paper is not yet 行. The moment you stand up and carry it out, that is 行. You will find it inside phrases like 身体力行 ("practice what you preach"), where 行 is the vital verb: the committed, physical doing. But 行 appears everywhere on its own — in 步行 (walking on foot), in the everyday exclamation 行！ ("OK, let's go!"), and in dozens of other compounds.

The character keeps its original promise: every time you see 行, something is in motion.

Try it: Recall one thing you have been meaning to start. Picture the crossroads. Which path will you take? Say it aloud: I will 行 — I will act. Notice how the word places all the weight on doing, not on deciding.
