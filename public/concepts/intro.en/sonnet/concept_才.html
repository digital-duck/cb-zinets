🌱 才

🌱 才

Think about a friend who picks up a new song on the guitar after hearing it just once, or a classmate who solves puzzles faster than anyone else in the room. We often say that person has a natural gift. In Chinese, that gift has a name: 才 (cái).

Picture this. Imagine a tiny seedling pushing up through soil toward the light. Ancient Chinese writers saw that same image in 才 — something alive, bursting upward, full of potential. The character captures the idea of a natural force growing from within, not something you borrow or buy.

Simple rule. 才 means innate talent or natural ability — the raw gift a person is born with. You will see it pair up with other characters to build bigger ideas. In our idiom 才气过人, 才 is the first building block: the talent itself. (We will meet 气, 过, and 人 in the sections ahead, and you will watch the full meaning click into place.)

Try it yourself. Look at the stroke order for 才: a horizontal stroke, then a vertical one crossing it, then a short diagonal to the right. Practice writing it three times slowly. As you write, say cái aloud. Now think of one real person in your life whose 才 — whose natural gift — you admire most. Keep that person in mind; it will make the whole idiom feel personal and alive.
