🌱 犭

🌱 犭

Everyday example: You already know what a dog looks like — four legs, fur, loyal eyes. In Chinese, the full character for "dog" is 犬 (quǎn). But characters are like words in a sentence: sometimes a piece gets squeezed and simplified to fit neatly beside other pieces.

Picture this: When 犬 moves to the left side of a character, it sheds one stroke and leans slightly, becoming 犭. Think of it as 犬 tucking in its tail to make room for a neighbor. That compact, three-stroke shape — 犭 — is called the dog radical or beast radical.

Simple rule: Whenever 犭 appears on the left of a Chinese character, the whole character almost always connects to an animal, especially a mammal. It acts like a category label, silently announcing: "an animal lives inside this word." Even 独 (dú), the first character in 独一无二, meaning "one-of-a-kind," carries 犭 on its left side.

Try it yourself: Look closely at 独. Can you spot the 犭 on the left? Trace it with your finger — the three small strokes leaning left, like a dog tucking in its tail. Once you recognize 犭, you hold a master key: every character that carries it is telling you, right up front, that something animal-like is at the heart of its meaning. That one small shape unlocks a whole family of characters.
