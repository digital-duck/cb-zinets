🌱 目

🌱 目

Everyday example. Think about how often you use your eyes without thinking — reading a sign, watching someone across a room, or giving a disapproving look to a friend. Eyes are everywhere in daily life, and in Chinese they are everywhere in the writing system too.

Picture this. The character 目 (mù) began as a drawing of an eye. An ancient scribe sketched an oval with a pupil in the centre, then over centuries the oval tipped on its side and the lines straightened into neat strokes. If you tilt your head to the left and squint, the modern character still echoes that original eye: two horizontal lines inside a tall rectangle — the pupil sitting inside the eyelid.

Simple rule. 目 means eye and carries the meaning "seeing, looking, or watching" wherever it appears. In the idiom 高傲自大, eyes matter because arrogance often shows in how someone looks at the world — or refuses to. As a building block (called a radical, a recurring unit that hints at meaning), 目 turns up inside dozens of other characters. Whenever you spot that tall rectangle with two inner lines, you know the character has something to do with sight or vision.

Try it yourself. Find the character 目 hidden inside 看 (kàn, "to look") — you will see 目 sitting at the bottom, with a hand shading it from above, like someone shielding their eyes to peer into the distance. How many "eye" characters can you find today?
