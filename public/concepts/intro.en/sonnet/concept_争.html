🌱 争

Everyday example. Have you ever sprinted for the last seat on a bus, or grabbed the final slice of pizza before your sibling did? That urgent, competitive reaching — that is the feeling packed inside one small Chinese character.

Picture this. The character 争 (zhēng) shows two hands pulling at the same object from opposite directions. One hand reaches from above, another pushes from below, and something precious sits between them. The whole picture says: two people want the same thing at the same time, and neither is letting go. In English we might say "to compete," "to fight for," or simply "to grab."

Simple rule. Whenever you see 争, think of active, energetic pursuit — reaching for something before someone else takes it. It is not a passive wish; it is a muscular, in-motion striving. In the idiom 争分夺秒, 争 pairs with 分 (minutes). So the phrase literally opens with the image of fighting for every minute — treating time itself as that precious object being grabbed before it slips away.

Try it yourself. Close your eyes and picture a countdown clock. Two runners are neck and neck. Each one is 争-ing — competing fiercely — for the finish line. Now you know the first word of our idiom, and you already feel its urgency. Hold that racing heartbeat in mind; it will colour every character we meet next.
