🍃 爰

Think about the moment when someone passes a baton in a relay race. One hand reaches down, another reaches up, and for a brief instant both hands hold the same object — then the exchange is complete, and motion continues.

Picture this: the character 爰 (yuán) captures exactly that image. At the top sits 爪 (zhǎo), a hand with fingers curling downward — the first runner letting go. At the bottom sits 又 (yòu), another hand reaching up to receive. A short horizontal stroke connects them in the middle, marking the moment of handoff.

Simple rule: 爰 encodes the idea of therefore or thereupon — a moment of transfer where one thing leads naturally into the next. In classical Chinese poetry and prose, 爰 signals that what follows is the direct consequence of what came before. Spring grows warm → thereupon the flowers open. The warmth does not merely accompany the blooming; it passes something to the blossoms, just like the relay baton, making them possible.

You can think of 爰 as a logical connector shaped like two hands in mid-exchange: cause on top, effect on the bottom, the handoff in the middle.

Try it yourself: cover either half of 爰 and notice how the meaning feels incomplete. The top hand alone is just reaching; the bottom hand alone is just waiting. Only together — mid-transfer — do they say therefore. Can you find the same "cause → handoff → effect" pattern in the phrase 春暖花开 (spring warm → flowers bloom)?
