🌸 Phrase 飞禽走兽

Imagine you are describing a nature documentary. Instead of listing every creature one by one — eagles, deer, wolves, sparrows, tigers — you wave your hand and say, "the whole animal kingdom." One sweep, everything included. Chinese has a elegant four-character phrase that does exactly this.

飞禽走兽 (fēi qín zǒu shòu) pairs two vivid images side by side: "flying birds" and "running beasts." The first half, 飞禽, points upward — creatures of the sky. The second half, 走兽, points to the ground — four-legged creatures that roam the earth. Together they form a complete picture of the animal world, not by listing every species but by naming the two extremes and letting your imagination fill in everything between.

This is a recurring trick in Chinese: choose two contrasting poles — sky and ground, morning and evening, mountains and seas — then present them as a pair to suggest the whole. You are not just describing two things; you are drawing a line between opposite ends and implying everything along that line is included.

Simple rule: pair two vivid contrasts → imply a complete category.

Try it yourself: Think of another pair of opposites that together cover a whole group. Hot and cold? Young and old? How would you use just two images to suggest "all temperatures" or "all ages"? Jot down your pair — you are already thinking the way this phrase does.
