🍃 良

🍃 良

Everyday example. Think about a friend who says, "That was a really good meal." In Chinese, that word good — the kind that means genuinely fine, high-quality, excellent — is often expressed with the character 良 (liáng).

Picture this. Imagine a scale tipping perfectly into balance. The character 良 captures exactly that sense of something settling into its best possible state — not just "okay," but properly good. Ancient scribes drew it to suggest grain that had been sorted and found worthy. What passes the test earns the label 良.

Simple rule. 良 means good, fine, or excellent — especially as a quiet, built-in quality rather than a flashy one. It shows up in pairs: 良好 (liánghǎo, "good and sound"), 善良 (shànliáng, "kind-hearted and good"). Notice that 良 lends its quiet endorsement to whatever character stands beside it.

You can also spot it in the idiom at the heart of this book, 废寝忘食 (fèi qǐn wàng shí) — someone so absorbed in worthy, 良-quality work that they forget to sleep or eat. The implication is that the pursuit itself is good and worth that total devotion.

Try it yourself. Say aloud: liáng. Now think of one thing in your day you would genuinely call "good" — steady, reliable, worth keeping. That feeling? That is 良.
