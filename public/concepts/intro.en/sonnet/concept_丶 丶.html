🌱 丶 丶

Think of the last time you saw raindrops on a windowpane — two small dots, side by side, each one brief and weightless. That image is a perfect doorway into today's building block.

In Chinese writing, 丶 is the dot stroke — the tiniest mark a brush can leave on paper. Its name in Chinese is diǎn (点), which simply means "dot" or "drop." By itself, one 丶 already carries quiet energy, like a single drop of ink falling.

Now picture two of them sitting together: 丶 丶. Two drops. In Chinese characters, whenever you spot a pair of dots like this, they often work as a team, giving a character a sense of symmetry or liquid energy — echoing ideas of water, fire, or simply visual balance. You can find this dot-pair tucked inside many everyday characters, adding the finishing touches the way a painter adds two small highlights to a portrait.

The simple rule is this: one dot = one tiny stroke; two dots side by side = a paired unit that shapes the feel of the whole character.

Try it yourself. Take a pencil and draw 丶 丶 — two gentle downward flicks, like two small teardrops. Notice how your eye naturally wants them to match. That instinct for balance is exactly the visual logic built into Chinese character design. Every time you spot this pair inside a bigger character, pause and ask: what are these two drops doing here?
