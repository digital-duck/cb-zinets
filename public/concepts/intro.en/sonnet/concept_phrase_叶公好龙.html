🌸 Phrase 叶公好龙

Imagine a friend who says they love spicy food — talks about it constantly, decorates their kitchen with chili-pepper art — but the moment a genuinely fiery dish arrives at the table, they push it away and reach for plain bread. The enthusiasm was real, yet it was aimed at the idea of the thing, not the thing itself.

Picture the gap clearly. There is the version of something we enjoy in our imagination: controllable, idealized, safe to admire from a distance. Then there is the real version that shows up uninvited, with all its rough edges and demands. When that real version appears, the gap between fantasy and fact is exposed — and the enthusiasm evaporates.

The Chinese phrase 叶公好龙 captures this human pattern in four characters. It tells of a lord who filled his home with dragon carvings and paintings, proclaiming his devotion — until an actual dragon arrived, and he fled in terror. The story is old, but the pattern it names is everywhere: professed love ≠ genuine readiness.

The simple rule: if someone's enthusiasm survives only at a comfortable distance, it is love of the image, not the reality.

Try it yourself. Pick something you say you want — more creative freedom at work, honest criticism from a mentor, a stricter daily routine. Now ask honestly: if that thing landed in your life tomorrow, in full force, would you welcome it — or quietly hope it disappears? That answer tells you which side of the phrase you are on.
