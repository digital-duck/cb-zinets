🌱 止

🌱 止

Everyday example. Think about a traffic light turning red. Every driver knows exactly what it means: stop right here, don't go any further. That single, clear command — stop — is packed into the Chinese character 止 (pronounced zhǐ).

Picture this. Thousands of years ago, 止 was drawn as a simple picture of a human foot — toes at the top, heel at the bottom. Ancient writers used a foot to show the idea of where movement ends. When your foot plants firmly on the ground, you have stopped. Over centuries the drawing simplified into the four strokes we write today, but the original meaning stayed: to stop, to halt, to go no further.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 止 inside a larger character, it often carries a hint of stopping or feet. The character 步 (bù, "step") pairs 止 with another foot shape, suggesting one foot following another — a step in motion. And 正 (zhèng, "correct, upright") places 止 beneath a horizontal line, picturing a foot landing squarely on target.

Try it yourself. Trace 止 slowly: a short horizontal stroke, a vertical stroke down through it, a short stroke to the right, then a long horizontal stroke across the bottom. Say zhǐ aloud as you write it. Now look at the word 停止 (tíngzhǐ, "to stop completely") — can you spot 止 hiding inside, doing exactly its job?
