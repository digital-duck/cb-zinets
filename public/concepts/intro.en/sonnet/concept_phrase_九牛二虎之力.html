🌸 Phrase 九牛二虎之力

Everyday example: Imagine trying to push a car that has run out of gas. You lean in, plant your feet, and push with everything you have — every muscle straining. That feeling of giving 100% of your strength to a hard task is exactly what this phrase describes.

Picture this: You already know the characters 九 (nine), 牛 (ox), 二 (two), 虎 (tiger), and 力 (strength) from earlier sections. Here they combine with one new piece, 之 (zhī) — a classical Chinese particle that links two words the way "of" or "'s" does in English. So 九牛之力 reads as "the strength of nine oxen," and adding 二虎 gives the full phrase 九牛二虎之力 (jiǔ niú èr hǔ zhī lì): "the strength of nine oxen and two tigers." Picture nine huge oxen and two fierce tigers all straining together — an enormous, almost unimaginable amount of force used to describe a person giving their absolute maximum effort on something stubborn or difficult.

Simple rule: Use 九牛二虎之力 when describing an effort that pushed someone to their limits — physically or mentally. It almost always pairs with a verb like 费 (fèi, "to expend") or 用 (yòng, "to use"): 费了九牛二虎之力 means "expended the strength of nine oxen and two tigers," i.e., "tried extremely hard." It's not used for ordinary tasks — only ones that felt like a real struggle.

Try it yourself: Think of a recent moment when you struggled to finish something — moving heavy furniture, solving a tough problem, or convincing someone stubborn. Try saying: "我费了九牛二虎之力才把箱子搬上楼" ("I used the strength of nine oxen and two tigers just to carry the box upstairs"). Notice how the phrase adds vivid emphasis to ordinary effort.
