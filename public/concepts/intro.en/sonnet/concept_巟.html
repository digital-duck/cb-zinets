Everyday example. Imagine a wild river valley — no roads, no houses, just water rushing freely between open banks stretching as far as you can see. That feeling of boundless, untamed space is exactly what the ancient character 巟 (huāng) was born to express.

Picture this. Look at 巟 and you will notice it has two layers. The top piece, 亡 (wáng), is a small hooked shape that originally meant gone or vanished — as if something has disappeared over the horizon. Below it sits 巛 (chuān), three parallel wavy lines that painters use as a shorthand for flowing rivers. Put them together and you get a place where everything human has vanished and rivers flow wild and free — a vast, open wilderness.

Simple rule. Think of 巟 as a visual equation:

$$ \text{巟} = \underbrace{\text{亡}}_{\text{gone / lost}} + \underbrace{\text{巛}}_{\text{rivers}} $$

When you see these two pieces stacked, read it as rivers running where no one lives — boundless and desolate. You will meet 巟 again inside the more familiar character 荒 (huāng, wilderness), where a grass radical is added on top to complete the picture of overgrown, untamed land.
