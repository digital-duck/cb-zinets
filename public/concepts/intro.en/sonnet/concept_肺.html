🍃 肺

🍃 肺

Every breath you take right now is thanks to a pair of organs working quietly inside your chest — your lungs. They pull in fresh air, pass oxygen to your blood, and push out waste. Without them, nothing else in your body works.

Picture this. Imagine a sponge soaking up water and then squeezing it back out. Your lungs do something similar with air, expanding and contracting dozens of times every minute — usually without you noticing at all.

In Chinese, the word for lungs is 肺 (fèi). Notice the left-hand side of the character: that vertical shape with two small strokes is 月 (yuè), which in body-part characters acts as a shorthand for "flesh" or "organ." The right side, 市 (shì), carries a hint of the sound. Together they form a character that points directly to this vital, air-filled organ.

In the idiom 狼心狗肺 — wolf heart, dog lungs — 肺 stands in for everything that should be tender and life-sustaining inside a person. The idiom says, in colorful terms, that someone has replaced their warmth with something wild and unfeeling.

Try it yourself. Take a slow, deep breath and hold it for two seconds. As you exhale, say the syllable fèi aloud. That breath you just controlled? 肺 — lungs — made it happen.
