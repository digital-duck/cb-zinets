🍃 海

Everyday example. Imagine standing on a beach. In front of you, water stretches so far that the line where it meets the sky — the horizon — is the only edge you can see. That huge body of salty water is called 海 (hǎi), which means "sea" or "ocean."

Picture this. Now picture a map of the world. Lakes are small blue patches, rivers are thin blue lines, but 海 is the vast blue that fills entire regions, connecting to other seas and eventually to the open ocean. In the phrase 五湖四海 (wǔ hú sì hǎi), 海 represents the "four seas" — the great waters believed to surround and bound the inhabited world in classical Chinese geography. Paired with 湖 (lake), the phrase moves from smaller, contained waters to the largest, most boundless ones, building a sense of "everywhere" — from every lake to every sea.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 海, think: big, open, connecting water — something larger than a lake, often touching distant lands. In idioms, 海 frequently stands for vastness, openness, or the whole world, not just literal water.
