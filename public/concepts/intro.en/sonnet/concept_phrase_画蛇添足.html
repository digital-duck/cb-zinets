🌸 Phrase 画蛇添足

Imagine you just baked a perfect loaf of bread. It smells amazing, the crust is golden, and everyone is excited. Then you decide to paint it blue "to make it better." Now nobody wants to eat it. You didn't improve things — you ruined them by adding something unnecessary.

Picture this: An ancient Chinese contest — the first person to draw a snake wins a jar of wine. One artist finishes first and, with time to spare, thinks: Why not add feet to make it more impressive? While he's drawing those extra feet, another contestant finishes a plain snake and claims the prize. The judge smiles: "Snakes don't have feet. What you drew is no longer a snake."

Simple rule: 画蛇添足 (huà shé tiān zú) literally means draw a snake, add feet. It describes any action that goes beyond what is needed and, in doing so, makes things worse — or simply wastes effort on something that was already complete. It is the opposite of "less is more."

Try it yourself: Can you think of a time you kept editing something — an email, a drawing, an outfit — until you realized you had actually made it worse? That moment of oh no, I overdid it is exactly the feeling this idiom captures. Next time you catch yourself adding that one last tweak, ask: Am I drawing feet on my snake?
