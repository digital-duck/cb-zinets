Imagine you're learning to count in Chinese, and you've already mastered the numbers from one to eight. Now you reach the very last single-digit number before ten — and that number is 九 (jiǔ), meaning "nine."

Picture this: Think of nine items lined up on a table — nine apples, nine coins, nine pencils. In Chinese, instead of writing the word "nine," you write a single character: 九. It's a compact little symbol, just two simple strokes, but it carries the full meaning of the number nine. You'll often see it in everyday phrases, like ordering "nine dumplings" (九个饺子, jiǔ gè jiǎozi) or talking about "nine o'clock" (九点, jiǔ diǎn).

Simple rule: In Chinese culture, the number 九 (nine) has a special bonus meaning beyond counting. Because its pronunciation "jiǔ" sounds just like another word meaning "long-lasting" (久), people often associate 九 with the idea of long life or permanence. This is why you'll find nine appearing in idioms about extreme situations — including the very phrase we're building toward here, 九死一生.
