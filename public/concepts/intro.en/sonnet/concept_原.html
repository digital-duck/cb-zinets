🍃 原

Everyday example: Think about the word "original" in English. When you call a song "the original version," you mean it is the first one — the starting point everything else came from. The Chinese character 原 carries exactly that same idea: source, beginning, root.

Picture this: Imagine standing at the base of a cliff where a fresh spring bubbles up from the rock. Ancient Chinese writers saw this scene and captured it in 原. The upper part $\text{厂}$ represents a cliff face or overhang, and nestled inside it sits $\text{泉}$ — the character for a natural spring. Together, they paint a vivid picture of water welling up from its underground origin.

Simple rule: Whenever you see 原 in a word, think origin or starting point. 原来 (yuán lái) means "originally" — how things were at first. 原因 (yuán yīn) means "reason" or "cause" — literally the source of why something happened. You can remember the structure like this:

$$ \text{厂 (cliff)} + \text{泉 (spring)} = \text{原 (source)} $$

Try it yourself: Look closely at 源, the final character in our idiom 左右逢源. Notice that 源 simply adds the water radical $\text{氵}$ to 原, making it specifically a water source. Can you spot 原 hiding inside 源? Once you find it, you have uncovered the shared root — and that is exactly what learning characters this way is all about.
