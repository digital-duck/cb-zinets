🌸 Phrase 自食其果

Imagine a student who skips every study session, thinking "I'll manage." Then exam day arrives, and the grade reflects all those missed hours. Nobody else caused it — the outcome grew directly from the student's own choices.

Picture a farmer planting seeds in the fall. Whatever goes into the ground is exactly what comes up in the spring: corn seeds give corn, thistle seeds give thistles. Actions work the same way. They are seeds you plant, and sooner or later you have to eat whatever grows.

自食其果 (zì shí qí guǒ) captures this idea in four compact characters. Read them literally: zi (self), shí (eat), qí (one's own), guǒ (fruit). Together they mean "to eat the fruit one has grown oneself." The phrase names the moment a person faces consequences that came directly from their own earlier decisions — not bad luck, not someone else's fault, but the natural harvest of personal choices. In everyday use it carries a gentle sense of irony: the person who set the trap is now the one caught in it.

Try it yourself: think of a time you stayed up late the night before an early alarm — and felt exhausted the next morning. That drowsiness is your own "fruit." Can you recall another situation where the outcome was unmistakably a result of your own earlier action? That moment of clear-eyed recognition is exactly what 自食其果 describes.
