🍃 辙

Have you ever driven down a dirt road after a rainstorm and noticed the deep grooves left behind by a truck's wheels? Those ruts pressed into the mud are a perfect picture of what 辙 means.

Picture this: A heavy cart rolls through soft ground, and its wheels carve two parallel tracks into the earth. Hours later, another cart follows — and its wheels naturally settle into those same grooves. The path becomes established. The tracks become a road.

辙 (zhé) means wheel rut or track — the mark left in the ground by a rolling wheel. Over time, it grew to mean any established path, groove, or course of travel. When you say something is "off track," you are reaching for exactly this image: something that has left the expected groove and gone its own direction.

Simple rule: Think of 辙 as the memory a wheel leaves behind. A single wheel press leaves a mark; repeated wheels leave a rut; a rut becomes a road. 辙 captures that whole progression — from physical groove to established route.

In the four-character phrase 南辕北辙, this character lends its meaning of "direction of travel" — the path your wheel is actually cutting, which may or may not be the path you intended.

Try it yourself: Next time you see tire tracks in mud or snow, you are looking at a 辙 in real life. Can you trace where the wheel came from and where it was heading?
