🌸 Phrase 狼心狗肺

Let’s talk about a phrase that sounds quite dramatic: “狼心狗肺” (láng xīn gǒu fèi). It's a Chinese idiom – meaning a traditional Chinese saying – and it packs a powerful punch when you understand what it really means. Don’t let the animal names scare you; it doesn’t literally mean wolves and dogs!

Everyday example: Imagine a friend is excitedly telling you about a fantastic new job they got. But then, as soon as you start to congratulate them, they immediately launch into a bitter complaint about their old boss, exaggerating every negative detail. That feels… uncomfortable, doesn’t it?

Picture this: Think of a wolf – fiercely independent and sometimes predatory – and a dog – loyal but easily swayed by emotion. The idiom uses these animals to represent two very different kinds of inner feelings.

Simple rule: “狼心狗肺” describes someone who pretends to be genuinely happy for others while secretly harboring resentment, envy, or bitterness. It suggests a conflict between outward appearance and inward feeling. The literal translation is "wolf heart, dog lungs," but the real meaning is about hidden negative emotions.

Try it yourself: Think of someone you know who seems overly enthusiastic about something that might actually bother them. Do they really seem happy for the other person? Or are there subtle signs suggesting a different feeling underneath? Recognizing this phrase can help you spot these situations.
