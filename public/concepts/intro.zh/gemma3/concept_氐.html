##氐

Let's talk about dī (低三下四). It sounds a bit strange at first, doesn’t it? It’s a Chinese idiom that describes a very specific kind of behavior – and understanding it can help you recognize it in yourself or others.

Think about this: You're sitting with a friend, and they constantly interrupt your stories with trivial details, gossip, or just pointless chatter. They don’t really listen to you; they’re more interested in talking about themselves. That’s the feeling dī tries to capture.

Picture this: You're trying to read a book, and someone keeps loudly asking you questions, changing the subject every few minutes, or just making silly noises. It’s distracting and frustrating.

The simple rule is: dī describes behavior that demonstrates a lack of respect for another person's time, thoughts, or feelings. It’s about not being genuinely interested in what someone else is saying. It's about prioritizing your own voice and attention over the other person’s.

Let's say you’re having a serious conversation with a colleague about a project deadline. If they start telling jokes and unrelated stories, that would be dī. It shows they aren’t taking the conversation seriously. Essentially, it's a way of saying someone is being frivolous or disrespectful in their communication.

Try it yourself: The next time you’re talking to someone, observe whether they are genuinely listening and engaging with your thoughts or if they’re drifting off into unrelated topics.
