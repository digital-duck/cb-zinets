Let’s talk about 卜 – it's a really ancient practice that people have been doing for thousands of years, especially in China. Think about it like this: you’ve probably ever used a die to decide something, right? Like, “Should I order pizza or pasta?” You roll the die, and whatever number comes up guides your choice. 卜 is similar, but much more involved and with a deeper sense of seeking guidance.

Picture this: a person carefully arranges various objects – often coins, pebbles, or even animal bones – on a table. These objects aren't just randomly scattered; they’re placed in specific patterns according to a predetermined system. This system is called a hexagram (六爻, liúyáo), which simply means “six lines.” Each line represents a different aspect of a situation or question you have.

The simple rule is this: after the objects are arranged, someone interprets what they see. They look for patterns, relationships between the lines, and symbols within those arrangements. There isn’t one ‘right’ answer; instead, interpretations can vary based on tradition and the specific context of the question. It's about gaining insight and understanding, not predicting a fixed future.

Try it yourself: Imagine you have six coins. You could arrange them in a simple pattern – perhaps three rows of two coins each.
