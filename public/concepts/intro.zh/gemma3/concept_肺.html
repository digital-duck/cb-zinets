Let’s start with something familiar: breathing. You take a deep breath in, and you exhale. That's your lungs at work!

Everyday Example: Imagine you’re running a race. As you sprint, you’re breathing faster, right? Your body needs more oxygen to fuel your muscles. And when you exhale, you’re getting rid of the carbon dioxide that your body made as a byproduct of all that activity.

Picture this: Think about a bellows – that’s a tool used in blacksmithing to pump air into a hot forge. Your lungs do something similar, but much more precisely. They expand and contract to move air in and out.

Simple rule: The lung is an organ inside your body designed to exchange gases: oxygen and carbon dioxide. It's like a sophisticated filter and delivery system.

Now let’s get a little specific. We call the main part of the lung the 肺 (fèi) – pronounced "fei" in Mandarin. “肺” literally means “lung,” just as “心” (xīn) means “heart.” These are important terms to remember because they represent fundamental parts of our anatomy.

The lung isn’t one solid piece; it's divided into smaller sections, called lobes. A healthy adult has two lungs – a left lung and a right lung. They’re spongy and filled with tiny air sacs called alveoli. These are where the real gas exchange happens. We'll explore these alveoli in more detail next!
