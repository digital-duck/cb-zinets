🌱 丿一

Everyday example: Think about how you learned to write the letter "T" as a child — first a straight line across, then a straight line down. Chinese characters are built the same way, from a small set of basic strokes. Two of the very first strokes every learner meets are 一 (yī), a flat horizontal line, and 丿 (piě), a line that sweeps down and to the left, like a gentle ski slope.

Picture this: Imagine drawing a single chopstick lying flat on a table — that's 一. Now imagine picking up a pencil and dragging it from the upper right down to the lower left in one smooth, thinning stroke — that's 丿. Together, these two simple shapes are like LEGO bricks: by themselves they're plain, but stacked and combined with other strokes, they become the building blocks of thousands of characters, including 人 (rén, "person"), which uses a falling stroke like 丿 paired with another stroke leaning the opposite way.

Simple rule: 一 is written left to right in one flat motion. 丿 is written top to bottom, curving slightly left, getting thinner as it goes.

Try it yourself: On paper, draw one flat horizontal line, then below it draw one downward-left sweeping line. Notice how different they feel under your hand — flat and steady versus diagonal and tapering. You've just written your first two Chinese strokes.
