🌸 Phrase 守株待兔

Everyday example: Imagine a farmer who once found a rabbit that had accidentally run into a tree stump and knocked itself out. He picked up the rabbit without any effort and had a delicious meal. The next day, instead of tending his fields, he sat beside the same stump all day, waiting for another rabbit to appear. And the next day. And the day after that. His crops withered, and no rabbit ever came.

Picture this: Think of someone who wins the lottery once and then quits their job, spending every day buying tickets and waiting to win again — convinced that lightning will strike twice in the same spot.

Simple rule: 守株待兔 (shǒu zhū dài tù) literally means "guard the stump, wait for the rabbit." It describes the mistake of expecting a lucky accident to repeat itself, or clinging to an old method that worked once instead of adapting to new circumstances. In life and learning, we call this kind of rigid thinking a fixed mindset — the opposite of being flexible and open to change.

Try it yourself: Can you think of a moment when you (or someone you know) kept using an old strategy hoping it would work again, even when the situation had clearly changed? That feeling of "but it worked before!" is exactly the spirit of 守株待兔. Next time you catch yourself thinking that way, ask: Is the stump still there — or has the world moved on?

Payoff 🎯

Imagine a farmer who, by sheer luck, finds a rabbit that has run headlong into a tree stump and knocked itself out. Thrilled, he abandons his plow and sits beside that stump every day, waiting for the miracle to repeat. Seasons turn, his fields go to ruin, and no second rabbit appears.

Picture this: the stump becomes a kind of mental prison. The farmer has confused one lucky moment with a reliable strategy. He is no longer farming — he is just waiting.

This is exactly what 守株待兔 (shǒu zhū dài tù — literally "guard the stump, wait for the rabbit") captures in four characters. It names a universal human trap: mistaking a one-time coincidence for a repeatable rule, and then stubbornly refusing to adapt when the world moves on.

The simple rule the phrase teaches: past luck is not a plan. Conditions change. What worked once — or what seemed to work — rarely works again by pure waiting.
