🌱 亥

Think about the last hour before midnight — the house is quiet, dinner is long finished, and the day is almost over. In ancient China, people divided the full day into twelve equal time slots, each given a special name. 亥 (hài) was the name for the very last slot: roughly 9 PM to 11 PM.

Picture a giant clock face, but instead of numbers it has twelve symbolic labels called the Earthly Branches (地支, dìzhī). Think of them as the ancient world's equivalent of "o'clock." Starting at midnight with 子 (zǐ), they march through the day in order, and 亥 occupies the twelfth and final position — the last "tick" before the cycle resets. Because 亥 closes the loop, it carries a sense of completion and endings.

Each Earthly Branch is also paired with an animal. 亥 belongs to the pig (猪, zhū), which the ancient Chinese associated with abundance, rest, and the quiet of night. So when you see 亥, picture a sleepy pig settling in just before midnight.

The phrase 无时无刻 — meaning "not a single moment" — draws on this same tradition of naming time carefully. It is a good reminder that 亥 is just one stop on a continuous wheel of twelve; time never pauses, even at the very last branch.
