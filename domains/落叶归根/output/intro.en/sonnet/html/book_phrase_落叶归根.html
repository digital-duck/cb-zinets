🌱 丨 丿

Think of rain falling straight down a windowpane — that clean, vertical drop. Now think of a leaf letting go of a branch and drifting diagonally to the left before touching the ground. These two everyday images map almost perfectly onto two of the most important building blocks in all of Chinese writing.

The first stroke, 丨 (shù), is a straight vertical line, top to bottom. Draw it like a plumb line, perfectly upright, without leaning. The second stroke, 丿 (piě), is a left-falling diagonal — you start at the top right and sweep down to the lower left, the way a calligrapher's brush naturally lifts off the paper. In the phrase 落叶归根 ("fallen leaves return to their roots"), you can spot 丿 tucking inside several of those characters, giving them that graceful, outward sweep.

A simple way to tell them apart: ask yourself, does it go straight down or does it lean away to the left? Straight down = 丨. Lean left = 丿.

These two strokes appear in thousands of characters. Learning to recognize and draw them cleanly now pays dividends every time you encounter a new character later.

🌸 Phrase 落叶归根

Have you ever noticed how a homesick traveler, no matter how long they've been away, always talks about going home one day? That pull toward where you came from is one of the most universal feelings in human experience.

Imagine a great oak tree in autumn. Its leaves drift far on the wind — some land in a neighbor's yard, some float into a stream. But eventually they all settle, decompose, and feed the very soil around the tree's roots. The leaf that wandered so far has, in a sense, returned.

The Chinese phrase 落叶归根 (luò yè guī gēn) captures exactly this idea. Word by word: 落 means to fall, 叶 means leaf, 归 means to return, and 根 means root. Together they say: fallen leaves return to their roots. In everyday use, the phrase describes a person — often one who has lived abroad or far from home for many years — feeling a deep desire to return to their place of origin. It carries warmth and dignity, not sadness.

The simple rule is this: when something that has traveled far is drawn back to where it came from, you can reach for 落叶归根.

Try it yourself: think of someone you know who moved far away but still talks about going home someday. How would you describe that feeling in one image? You've already found the heart of this phrase.
