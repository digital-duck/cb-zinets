Phrase 自相矛盾

🌱 ⺁

🌱 ⺁

Think about a simple scenario: you are packing a suitcase for a trip. You decide that the suitcase must be large enough to hold your winter coat, but at the same time, you also insist it must be small enough to fit into the overhead compartment of an airplane. The suitcase cannot logically fulfill both demands simultaneously. This conflict—the requirement to be two things at once—is exactly what the concept $\text{⺁}$ addresses.

Picture this principle as a fundamental law of logic: everything needs to maintain internal consistency. When we analyze ideas, $\text{⺁}$ asks us to check for logical conflicts within a single proposition or system. If an idea states that something is X, but later implies it is not X, those two statements are fighting each other. This internal tug-of-war means the initial premise is flawed and cannot stand up to scrutiny.

The simple rule governing $\text{⺁}$ is this: A single entity or statement cannot possess two opposing properties (like being $P$ and non-$P$) at the same time, in the same respect. If you find a conflict, it signals that one of your assumptions must be incorrect, and you need to adjust your starting point.

To try it yourself, consider this: Can an animal be both entirely aquatic and entirely terrestrial simultaneously? The answer is no; those two descriptions contradict each other. Your job when applying $\text{⺁}$ is simply to point out where the logic breaks down into incompatible pieces.

🌸 Phrase 自相矛盾

🌸 Phrase 自相矛盾

Have you ever heard someone say something that simply doesn't make sense? For example, if a coach tells an athlete they need to run faster but then immediately tells them to slow down and take it easy—those two instructions clash. This idea of conflicting statements or actions is the core concept we are exploring today: self-contradiction.

Picture this: When something is self-contradictory (自相矛盾, zì xiāng máo dùn), it means that an idea, argument, or statement fights with itself; it contains two opposing parts that cannot both be true at the same time. It’s like trying to hold a rope and cut it simultaneously—the two actions cancel each other out. In Chinese philosophy, this phrase is used to point out when reasoning has fallen into such an impossible corner.

The simple rule to remember is: If you find two parts of an argument that negate or deny each other, you have found a contradiction. A clear statement must be consistent; it cannot assert 'A' and also assert 'not-A'.

Try it yourself! When reviewing any piece of writing, ask this question: Is there anywhere here where the author seems to say one thing while simultaneously suggesting the opposite? Finding these spots helps you become an expert at thinking clearly.
