🌱 乛 一

Think about drawing a straight road on a map. You put your pen down, pull it smoothly to the right, and lift it up. That single motion — one continuous line — is exactly what the stroke 一 (yī) is. It means "one," and it is the very first character most learners of Chinese ever write.

Now picture that same road, but just before the end, the driver turns the wheel and the line hooks upward — like a finger curling to beckon someone closer. That small curl at the finish is the stroke 乛. It is not a full character on its own; it is a component, a building-block shape that lives inside larger characters and gives the brush something to push off of at the stroke's end.

The simple rule: 一 is a flat horizontal line with a clean lift-off; 乛 is a horizontal line that finishes with a sharp upward hook. Both move left-to-right, but 乛 adds one extra flick at the close. You will see 乛 tucked into many characters — including shā jī jǐng hóu (杀鸡儆猴), where it quietly anchors a stroke inside a more complex shape.

🌸 Phrase 杀鸡儆猴

🌸 Phrase 杀鸡儆猴

Have you ever watched a teacher correct one student's homework in front of the whole class — not to embarrass that student, but so every student quietly recalibrates? That moment is not really about the one notebook on the desk. It is a signal sent to the whole room.

Picture a circus trainer long ago who needs a mischievous monkey to follow commands. The monkey is clever and headstrong; scolding it directly might just start a battle of wills. So the trainer makes a deliberate point of disciplining a small, easy-to-handle animal right where the monkey can see every detail. The lesson lands without a single word aimed at the monkey. The monkey understands perfectly.

That is the heart of 杀鸡儆猴 (shā jī jǐng hóu — literally "kill the chicken to warn the monkey"). The simple rule is: act visibly on a small target so a larger, harder-to-reach audience gets the message without being confronted directly. The chicken is not the real audience; the monkey is. The public action on the lesser case does the work that a private talk with the powerful party could never accomplish.

This strategy shows up everywhere — in management, diplomacy, and storytelling — whenever a communicator needs to shift behavior through demonstration rather than demand.

Try it yourself: Think of a recent news story or film scene where one character was punished publicly. Who was the real intended audience — the person being corrected, or the crowd watching?

Payoff 🎯

Think about the first day of a new job. The manager announces that a colleague who broke a safety rule has been formally disciplined — and suddenly everyone else double-checks their own habits. Nobody lecturing the whole team; one visible consequence, a hundred quiet course-corrections.

That moment has a name in Chinese: 杀鸡儆猴 (shā jī jǐng hóu), literally "kill the chicken to warn the monkey." Each character you have built up through this book — 杀 (to strike down), 鸡 (chicken), 儆 (to put on alert), 猴 (monkey) — clicks into place like a key. The phrase is not about chickens or monkeys at all. It is a compact, vivid image for the idea that one deliberate, visible act can redirect the behavior of many.

This is why 杀鸡儆猴 is the natural endpoint of your journey. Every earlier character taught you a single brick; this phrase shows you what a finished wall can do. It carries social logic (deterrence), narrative logic (example-setting), and emotional logic (a warning delivered through story, not lecture) — all in four syllables.

The same pattern appears in law, in management, in parenting, and in history. Wherever people need to shift a group's behavior without addressing every individual, 杀鸡儆猴 names what is happening.
