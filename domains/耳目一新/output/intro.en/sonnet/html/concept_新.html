🍃 新

Everyday example. Think about the smell of a freshly opened box — a new phone, a new pair of shoes, a new book. That crisp, just-made quality is exactly what the character 新 (xīn) captures. It means new, and it shows up whenever something is fresh, recent, or just beginning.

Picture this. Look at 新 closely. On the right side you can spot 斤 — the picture of an axe. Imagine a woodcutter swinging an axe into a living tree: the pale, freshly cut wood exposed by the blade is brand new, never seen before. Ancient writers chose this image to represent "new" — the crisp, raw surface revealed by a single clean stroke. The rest of the character frames that axe, but the axe is the heart of the meaning.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 新 in a Chinese word, it signals newness or freshness:

$$ \text{新} = \text{new / fresh / just arrived} $$

In the idiom 日新月异, 新 sits right after 日 (day), giving you the idea that each day brings something new. One small character, one big promise of change.

Try it yourself. Cover the character and draw what you remember — start with the axe shape 斤 on the right. Then ask: what is something new in your life today? In Chinese you can simply say 新的 (xīn de) — "a new one." You have just used your first 新!
