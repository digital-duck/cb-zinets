Imagine your employer says you will receive a $300 bonus in January and a $400 bonus in December. You feel disappointed. They quickly revise the offer: a $400 bonus in January and a $300 bonus in December. Suddenly it sounds better — even though the total is exactly the same $700. The numbers shifted; the reality did not.

Picture a perfectly balanced scale holding seven stones. You may stack three on the left and four on the right, or four on the left and three on the right. Either way, both sides together weigh seven. A person fooled by the arrangement might celebrate one version and protest the other, never noticing the balance never moved.

This is the heart of 朝三暮四 (zhāo sān mù sì, "three in the morning, four in the evening"). The phrase captures two related ideas: the trick of rearranging presentation without changing substance, and the habit of being easily swayed — shifting preferences when only the surface changes. In everyday Chinese, calling someone 朝三暮四 means they are fickle or inconsistent, changing their mind the way the numbers flip sides without the total ever moving.
