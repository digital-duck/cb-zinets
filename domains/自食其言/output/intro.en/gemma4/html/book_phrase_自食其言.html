🌸 Phrase 自食其言

Imagine planning a big picnic with friends. You promise them that you will bring enough sandwiches for everyone, and they start relying on your word. But when the day arrives, you realize you are too busy to bake them, so you only show up empty-handed. This is an everyday example of breaking a commitment or promising something and failing to follow through.

Picture this situation as a general concept: self-contradiction in speech or action. When someone speaks about their own words—or promises they made—and fails to live up to them, we call it zì shí qí yán. The phrase essentially describes the act of 'eating one's own words,' meaning that you have invalidated your own statements through subsequent actions or lack thereof.

The simple rule is this: If a person’s current action contradicts their past stated intention or promise, they are committing an act of self-contradiction. It highlights the importance of consistency between what we say and how we behave. To avoid it, one must ensure that future actions align with prior declarations.

Try it yourself: Think of a friend who promises to study for a test but is seen playing video games instead. They are exhibiting zì shí qí yán because their action (playing games) contradicts their stated intention (studying).
