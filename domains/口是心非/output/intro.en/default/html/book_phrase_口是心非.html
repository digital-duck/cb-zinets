🌸 Phrase 口是心非

Everyday example. Imagine a friend who says "I love your cooking!" while quietly pushing food around the plate. Their words say one thing, but their feelings say the opposite. In Chinese, there is a perfect four-character idiom for exactly this situation: 口是心非.

Picture this. The most important building block here is the first character: 口 (kǒu), meaning mouth — the opening through which our words travel out into the world. Picture a simple square, like a mouth held open, ready to speak. The full phrase 口是心非 (kǒu shì xīn fēi) pairs that mouth with the idea of the heart going in the opposite direction. A helpful shortcut: read it as mouth says yes, heart says no. Your friend's mouth praises the cooking; the heart quietly disagrees.

Simple rule. Use 口是心非 to describe someone whose spoken words do not match their true inner feelings — a gap between outward speech and inner truth. It carries a slightly critical tone, suggesting insincerity. Whenever 口 appears in a Chinese phrase, it is a reliable signal pointing to speech or the act of talking.

Try it yourself. Think of a time you said "No problem!" when you actually felt frustrated. That gap between what your 口 (mouth) said and what you truly felt inside — that is the moment 口是心非 captures perfectly.
