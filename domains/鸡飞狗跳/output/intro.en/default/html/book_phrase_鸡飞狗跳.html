Everyday example. Imagine it's Sunday morning and someone accidentally knocks over a pot of soup in a crowded kitchen. Kids start screaming, a dog bolts under the table, and everyone is shouting at once. That explosive, out-of-control moment — that's exactly what this phrase captures.

Picture this. The idiom 鸡飞狗跳 (jī fēi gǒu tiào) paints a vivid farmyard scene: chickens (鸡, jī) are flying (飞, fēi) into the air in a panic, while dogs (狗, gǒu) are jumping (跳, tiào) around frantically. Four short syllables conjure an image of pure, wild disorder — animals scattering in every direction because something has disturbed the peace.

Simple rule. Use 鸡飞狗跳 to describe any situation where calm has completely broken down and everyone (or everything) is in chaotic motion. It most commonly describes a home or family thrown into uproar — a noisy argument, a surprise visit that sends the household scrambling, or a child's meltdown that sets off a chain reaction.

孩子们一回家，整个屋子就鸡飞狗跳。 The moment the kids came home, the whole house was in chaos.
