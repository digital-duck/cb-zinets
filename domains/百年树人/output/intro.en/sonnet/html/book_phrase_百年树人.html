🌸 Phrase 百年树人

Think about planting an apple tree. You water it, prune it, and wait — maybe five years before it gives you fruit. Now imagine raising a child into a skilled teacher, a caring doctor, or a wise leader. That takes not five years, but decades of guidance, encouragement, and patient instruction.

Picture two farmers. One plants timber trees and harvests them in ten years. The other opens a small village school and spends an entire lifetime teaching children. The timber farmer sees results quickly. The schoolteacher's results are invisible at first — but her students go on to teach their students, and those students teach others, rippling outward across a hundred years.

This is exactly what the phrase 百年树人 (bǎi nián shù rén) captures. Breaking it apart: 百年 means "a hundred years" — not a precise count, but a way of saying a very long time, longer than any one person's plans. 树人 means "to cultivate people" — the character 树 is the same word as "tree," used here as a verb meaning to plant or to nurture. Together: growing people takes a century. The phrase is a quiet reminder that human development is the longest investment there is, and that the results often appear only after the gardener is gone.

The simple rule is this: 百 (hundred) measures the timescale, 年 (year) anchors it in lived time, 树 (to cultivate) carries the gardener's patient labor, and 人 (person) names the living harvest. Together they say: educating a human being is the longest and most worthwhile project there is.
