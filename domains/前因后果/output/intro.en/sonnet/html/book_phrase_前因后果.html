🌱 丷 一

Everyday example: Think about counting on your fingers. You hold up one finger — that is the most basic unit, the foundation of all counting. Before you can count to ten, you must first understand one.

Picture this: The component 一 (yī) is exactly what it looks like: a single, calm horizontal stroke — like a horizon line or a ruler laid flat on a table. It means one, and it is the simplest character in all of Chinese writing. Now look at 丷: two tiny, almost mirror-image strokes sitting side by side, like two small seeds or two drops of rain just starting to fall. Taken alone, 丷 does not mean a standalone word; it is a visual clue — a building-block piece that appears inside larger characters.

Simple rule: When 丷 rests above 一, picture a pair of seeds planted on flat ground. These two components often appear together as the upper portion of a larger character. The small pair sits on top; the steady line anchors it below. Together they create a sense of balance — a little detail supported by a firm foundation.

Try it yourself: Write a single horizontal stroke: 一. Now add two small outward-angling marks above it: 丷. Notice how the pair seems to grow out of the horizontal base. 🌸 Phrase 前因后果

🌸 Phrase 前因后果

Everyday example. You walk into the kitchen and find a broken mug on the floor. Your friend says, "Tell me 前因后果 (qián yīn hòu guǒ)!" They want the whole story — not just the broken mug, but what led up to it and what happened because of it.

Picture this. Imagine a timeline stretched out in front of you:

$$ \underbrace{\text{causes}}_{\text{前因 (qián yīn)}} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{event} \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\text{effects}}_{\text{后果 (hòu guǒ)}} $$

The word breaks into two pairs: 前因 means preceding causes — 前 (qián) is "before," 因 (yīn) is "cause." 后果 means following results — 后 (hòu) is "after," 果 (guǒ) is "fruit" or "outcome." Together they wrap around an event from both sides, capturing everything from beginning to end.

Simple rule. Whenever someone needs the full chain of events — not just what happened, but why it happened and what followed — Chinese speakers reach for 前因后果. It signals: give me the complete cause-and-effect story.

Try it yourself. Think of something that happened to you recently — maybe you were late to class or aced a test. Can you map out your own 前因后果? What were the causes before? What were the results after? Naming both sides is the heart of this phrase.

Payoff 🎯

You have spent time learning individual characters — 前 (before), 因 (cause), 后 (after), 果 (result) — and now they snap together into something larger than their parts.

Everyday example. Imagine a friend arrives late to dinner and explains: "There was heavy rain, so the roads flooded, so traffic stopped, so here I am." That chain — rain → flood → traffic → late — is exactly what 前因后果 captures: the cause that came before and the result that followed after, the whole story from seed to fruit.

Picture this. Think of a river. Far upstream, snow melts on a mountain (前因, the prior cause). Far downstream, a valley fills with water (后果, the later result). The phrase is the river itself — it names the entire flow, not just the mountain or the valley.

Simple rule. Whenever you need to explain why something happened and what came of it, 前因后果 is your frame. It tells a listener: "I am giving you the complete picture — beginning, middle, and end."

Try it yourself. Think of something that happened to you today. What was the cause (前因)? What was the result (后果)? Say it aloud: "Because _____, therefore _____." You have just used the logic this phrase embodies.
