🌸 Phrase 胆小如鼠

Think of someone who refuses to speak up in class, jumps at every small noise, and hides whenever there is the slightest trouble. In English you might say that person is "a real scaredy-cat." Chinese has a vivid four-character phrase for the same idea.

Picture a tiny mouse pressing itself flat against a wall, whiskers trembling, too frightened to cross the open floor. That image is baked right into the phrase 胆小如鼠 (dǎn xiǎo rú shǔ). Break it apart: 胆 means courage (literally "gallbladder," the organ ancient Chinese associated with bravery); 小 means small; 如 means as or like; 鼠 means mouse or rat. Put it together — "courage small like a mouse" — and you have a compact snapshot of timidity.

The key word here is 如, a little hinge that snaps two things together into a comparison. Whenever you see 如 sitting between a quality and an object, read it as "as … as." This single character does the work that English spreads across phrases like "as brave as a lion" or "as quiet as a mouse."
